## Chapter 1: Brunor
[7th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Hollow | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger)]
[Morning into Evening | The Blackwood Forest, north of Vespine’s Cradle, Caltheran]
*As the world gets torn asunder, what will my faith demand of me?*
Unmoved, Brunor dismounted his horse and stood quietly in the clearing. With each breath, the crisp, cold morning air, rich with the aroma of damp pine and fresh earth, bit at the lungs. Despite numerous visits, this spot’s silent forest was unlike anywhere else in Eòs. The ancient pines, tall and thick, absorbed all sound with their dark green needles, forming a nearly impenetrable canopy. Small wrens chirped and nested in the lower branches, undisturbed by horses or people. Six hundred souls resided within the dark, weathered stones of Caer Seryth, a keep situated half a league to the south, their anxieties resonating off the towering walls. Here, in this secluded spot, the wind’s whispers in the Cradle’s foothills mingled with the aromas of bark and damp moss. The silence demanded nothing of him. Nobody waged battle in these woods. House Blackwood of Caltheran had set those laws centuries ago, and nobody — not crown, not company, not desperate men on the run — had been foolish enough to test them. Brunor secured his powerful stallion to a sturdy post and moved along the edge of the dense clearing. Left treeline, right treeline. Flank. Tracks — faint footprints pressed into the soft earth, days old, headed away. Listening. Rustling leaves, distant bird calls, nothing that didn’t belong. A few paces behind him, Elaine dismounted her black purebred — the same one he gave her years ago after her captain promotion — and hitched him to a low branch. The horse, Munch, gave a quiet, indignant neigh. She smiled at him, filled with white teeth, no apology. Brunor crossed over and nudged the arm beneath her raven-feathered cloak. She flinched hard enough that he stepped back. Her head snapped toward him, hand finding her hilt, but the moment her eyes found his face, she let go of it.“Jumpy today, El?”She glanced at him, then turned away. Slowly, she started to unload her saddlebags.“Not today,” she said. Brunor watched her work. He saw Elaine every day of his life, had seen her since she was a bony girl too scared of thunder to sleep through a storm. Somewhere between that girl and this morning, the woman had arrived. The custom armor fit as it had grown with her — steel-studded leather over chain mail, three raven feathers hanging from the cloak pin among the hundreds of smaller ones that wrapped around her. On her hip, the black sword with its white leather grip and the purple amethyst set into the ivory hilt. Shoulder-length black hair matched the cloak, matched the horse. Everything about Elaine Morrandis matched, as if she’d decided once what she looked like and never reconsidered. She turned. Her smoky grey-blue eyes caught him staring. Brunor looked away too fast.“Stunned by my beauty, Commander?” Elaine said, the smile audible. Brunor cleared his throat. “Be silent and continue making camp.”Elaine nudged him aside. “Then go over there and let me.”He didn’t try to match it. Matching Elaine’s tongue was a losing game, and he’d learned that lesson enough times to stop paying tuition. He paced instead. Left treeline, then the right one. Flank. Tracks. Listening. The clearing gave back the same nothing it was known for, and he took that gratefully. By the time they had finished stacking the perimeter stones, twilight was settling through the canopy. Two plump rabbits turned on the spit. Fat hissed into the coals. Brunor looked up from the fire. The two moons sat low over the tree line, and he fixed on them because they were his oldest companions. His eyes remained until the coals and the clearing, and the sound of Elaine cleaning her blade all went to the same distance.
After a while, it wasn’t the moons he saw.
Brunor adored his father as a boy. They often walked through the keep town of Hudria, where Uthred Halvyr’s word held close to the weight of law. House Halvyr carried roots in both Varkaan and Caltheran, and the name alone was enough to make men step aside on the street or think twice about what to say when a Halvyr was near. He remembered the training yard at night. Hours spent correcting one mistake while the White Bear introduced three more, half the bouts fought before Brunor’s eyes had fully opened. He hated every session. Treasured every minute. Most of the continent called Uthred the greatest living swordsman; to Brunor, he was just his old man. But matching that — becoming it — was a dream he chased daily, to the point of hubris; Brunor thought it was only natural for the son to surpass the father.
Brunor accepted the charred rabbit from Elaine and tore into the hot flesh. To be as legendary as the White Bear. His father’s words, worn smooth from repetition: Legacy, honor, and a man’s own strength — that is what separates a warrior from a squabbling fool. Leave something worth remembering behind. And the watchtower. Stone, squat, the highest point in Hudria. Every other night, he climbed it with Elaine and Frederick, the three of them perched on the crenellations, talking about everything and nothing — dreams, fears, stories — while the city burned its lamps below. One night, his mother came instead. Johanna Halvyr’s unbraided, dirty-blonde hair caught the moonlight at the top, and she sat beside him on the ledge. She’d anticipated him being here. She wore a long white nightdress that fell to her knees, with a white shroud wrapped around her shoulders, making her look not of this world. Her hazel eyes always cut through Brunor whenever he looked too hard into them; he didn't look long.“Mother, why does father treat me like…like I'm one of the servants around the keep?” Brunor had asked, staring at the city below. It had taken all his courage to say finally. She glanced at him for a moment, then back across the city. She shook her head, and her hair waved in the night wind.“Why do you always assume such ridiculousness? Your father does not hate you, son; he loves you dearly. You are his heir and our only child. It is because he loves you that he remains harsh. Do not confuse your lord father’s sternness for hate,” she said.
That was what he always remembered first. Not the answer. The way her hazel eyes caught the pale light and held it like the flames below.
The two gazed at the moons without speaking. The city below them made small sounds — dogs, a door, a cart wheel on cobblestone. Johanna’s fingers pulled at the stone next to her. Brunor watched the pebbles float down the tower before being swept away by the wind.“I miss how Father used to be. The kind, loving man with whom I used to take walks, but now he acts mean, Mother, and treats me unfairly,” Brunor said. “Elaine, Frederick, and even little Phoebe all get smiles and sarcasm. All I get is *do this, boy,* and *don’t mess up again, or no dinner.* I’m sick of it! I want things to go back to the way they were!”He ran out of air at the end of it. Johanna giggled.“Yes, little lord, you are right. Your lord father is a bit rigid at times, even with me. Understand that he is firm solely because that’s required of him; being a Baron is not easy. He loves Elaine, Frederick, and Phoebe greatly, but you are everything to us, and nothing will ever change that fact,” she said, stroking his dark, unkempt hair. Still, El is treated better than me all the time, and she isn’t even his daughter…” the boy grumbled. Johanna giggled once more and flicked him in the ear.“What the—Ow!” He clutched the side of his head. “Why would you do that? I hate it when you flick my ear! You said you would stop!”She grinned and reached for the other one. The boy was faster. He caught her hand and flicked her ear first.“Ow!” she yelled.“Got you!” he shouted, already sprinting down the watchtower steps.“You’ll regret that! Give me your other ear, little lord!” she squealed, chasing after him. A voice pulled him back.“Huh?”Brunor looked down from the moons. Elaine was holding out a whetstone. She was frowning.“Seems like I should be the one to ask you now if you’re okay.”His head was pounding. It happened whenever the memories ran too long. It happened more often than he wanted, and always at the worst times, “I was thinking of my mother,” he said. Elaine went still.“Lady Johanna…” she said, and looked away. The fire crackled steadily. 
Beyond the clearing, a cricket began chirping, stopping, and then starting again. 
Brunor watched the coals shift. 
“I’m sorry. You don’t speak of her often, but maybe now is a good time to…well, talk of her,” Elaine said.
“No,” he replied. 
"Oh.”
Silence filled the gap between the two. Crickets chirped from the bushes. Somewhere in the distance, a wolf howled. The pack howled after.
“I—” Elaine began. 
Brunor remained fixed on the coals, but at the edge of the glow, he saw Elaine’s body shift under her cloak. 
“I wonder what she would say if she were still here. What would she say about the things we must abide just for the sake of her dream?” she said.
Brunor picked up a stick and began to break pieces off to toss into the flame. “Does it matter?” he said, tossing a piece. “She knew what kind of horrors we would face one day. The trials this world would throw at us. Mother often cried over it, do you remember?” 
Elaine’s eyes watched the flames consume another piece of the stick. The orange made her eyes glow to an uncomfortable degree. 
Brunor continued. “She was a good woman. Perhaps the best this world had. Often, I still pray to the gods for an explanation. Why would they take her? How did they take her?”
“The gods are cruel and take the ones least deserving. We shouldn't try to guess why. Honor the wish she had for us and do well not to become monsters.”
Brunor scoffed. “It's too late for us, El. If she were still here, she would no doubt be forced to make a choice too.”
“We aren't monsters,” Elaine said, low. “And I refuse to become one. Whenever I look around, I see that we are surrounded, Brunor. Take care not to confuse yourself with them.”
He smirked and tossed the last piece of stick into the fire. He picked up another and began again. 
“We breed what we are,” he said eventually. You’ve always believed we aren't, but don't be so damn gullible. We became monsters the moment we walked into that inn at Stonefield, and I cemented it by—” 
He stopped and let the dead boy fade again from the remembrance.
Elaine touched the stub on her left hand. “Yet, in that inn I killed the same men you did, in the same blood. I stood in that filth, and I defended her name out loud because someone had to, even if her own son wouldn't. That wasn’t a monster talking. Erasmus is what a man becomes when they begin to think like you are now. ”
“You sound pretty sure of yourself.”
“Because I am sure. Most of my life, I have been less so. But not that day. Meanwhile, you sound pretty sorry for yourself. When are you—”
“I don't care. Let me be.”
“It's unbecoming of you. The ones who destroy or take until someone is left hollow. Those are the monsters, not us. Johanna’s Promise was built on your father’s conviction, and I’ve come to hold it as my own — most of all where it concerns you. This is all despite that Uthred is perhaps the biggest monster of us all; however, if it wasn't for him, what would have become of Phoebe and me?”
Brunor leaned back and looked at Elaine. She was fixed back at him; the firelight in her eyes lit the way Johanna’s used to — but Elaine’s held it like permafrost trapping a flame in time, not amber.  His stomach dropped. He trampled the memory before it could surface again, and before the mark on her hand could catch the grief bleeding off him. He found himself doing that often of late.
“Gods, you are tenacious. I said, let me be, Elaine.”
“Unbecoming,” she repeated.
He held the commander’s persistent regard. Neither seemed interested in looking elsewhere. Then Elaine reached into her cloak and pulled out a wrapped cloth. She unfurled it to reveal a large, lumpy, silver-blue stone that glinted in the flames. Beside it was the neck and head of a stallion carved into a small piece of wood, unfinished.
“What is that?” he asked.
“A stone, Lady Johanna and I found by the riverbank outside Hudria. She said it reminded her of you and the birthstone Uthred had placed in your crib as a babe, so I kept it. Phoebe often slept with it. I keep it on me now for good luck.”
“And the carving?”
“Uthred had it in his study. It's what she was working on the night before her passing. I believe it was for you.”
Brunor took one hard look at the unfinished carving. The knifework used was skilled and done with a delicate hand. It was his mother‘s work.
Elaine allowed Brunor a few seconds to look, then she wrapped the cloth and tucked it away.
“The day I become a monster is the day I throw this bundle away. The world’s coming apart into violence that means nothing — loss that means nothing. We don’t get to forget why we started, or the ones who died for it. That can’t be for nothing. I won’t let it be.”
Brunor nodded. Elaine’s convictions radiated off her like heat. 
“You sound like her. I’m not sure if that is a good or bad omen,” he said, finishing off the second stick. 
“Belief is not an omen. Resignation is,” she replied.
The fire popped loudly, and one of the wooden logs split down the middle, sending sparks and ash dancing in smoke towards the stars. “
“The stone. What will you do with it?” he asked.
Elaine smiled, and her face brightened in the firelight. “When I have a child of my own. I will gift it to him.”
“Him? You sound sure of a boy.”
The scout commander folded her arms. “A girl would be too much of a headache to deal with. I was unruly. My father often said I would be punished for it with a daughter of my own one day, one like me.”
“I see…”
“Johanna also said that I—”
“Enough, Elaine.”
“Brunor…” She seemed to invite him, but the door only ever led to the lake now: the still water, the lifeless eyes that no longer held the light
Brunor stood. “I'm going for a walk.”
Elaine released the sigh she had been holding.
“Of course,” she said to herself, kicking a healthy boot of dirt towards the flame, killing it.
They did not speak for the rest of the night.

[8th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Flame | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger)]
[Dawn to Midday | The Silver Road, into Glintgate, Caltheran]
The next morning, the two rode out from the forest and onto the Silver Road.
Three days prior, Brunor and Elaine had returned from an escort job to find the summons waiting — a sealed letter bearing the mark of Archwarden Telvor, demanding their presence before the Caltheran Council within the week. They departed the same evening. Brunor never liked the council. Twelve men and women who spent more time bending the laws of nature than governing the people those laws were supposed to protect.
Johanna’s Promise often took work from Caltheran. The council paid the most silver seals, and the assignments were always the ugliest. The last time Brunor accepted a Caltheran contract — guarding Alfred’s Wall through the winter with limited supplies and fewer able men — he lost seventy soldiers to sickness before the fortnight was out. The council sent their condolences in a letter that misspelled his name.
They traveled from dawn to midday through the Aldren valley until Glintgate appeared from the river haze. The city spread across both banks of the estuary, with lower districts densely packed along the waterline and upper wards rising from the riverbanks. Everything bent toward the Bridge of Kings — an ancient, weathered stone crossing with intricate carvings that carried the main road over the Aldren River and up toward the fortress on Adian’s Mount. The fortress sat high on the hill’s crest, its pale, weathered walls towering above the treetops, visible from every quarter of the city. At noon, the sun struck its surface, causing the stones to glow with a fiery, almost luminous hue, as if the mountain itself was burning.
The sight turned Brunor’s stomach. Even now, after years on the road, he could not look at a castle without hearing his father’s voice. Pretty graves for ugly men, Uthred had called them. A lord builds a tower so the peasants look up instead of around. Spend the gold on walls, on feasts, on tournaments — and the problems on the other side of the wall rot until they stink loud enough to climb over it.
After hearing that on every march through every lordling’s territory for half his life, Brunor had stopped seeing castles as strongholds. He saw receipts — tallies of what a lord chose to spend and what he chose to ignore.
The pair rode across the great bridge and up to the Silver Gate, one of four entrances into the city proper. Brunor recognized the watch commander at the security pavilion before they’d reached the checkpoint — Mikah Cullard, built like a siege tower and twice as pleasant, screaming into the face of a guard who looked young enough to be still losing baby teeth. The boy’s chin was trembling. Mikah didn’t notice or didn’t care.
“Poor bastard,” Brunor muttered.
A line of boys filed past the gatehouse in ill-fitted mail, most of them too young to grow a proper beard. City guards. The council’s answer to whatever was bleeding this city dry — hand a pike to a boy and call it solved. A few of them glanced at Brunor’s sword as he passed. One looked old enough to shave. The rest didn’t. The fighting men were elsewhere, conscripted into the royal army. War was close. Everyone could smell it.
Mikah spotted them. His face cycled through several things at once — recognition, annoyance, something harder — before settling into the expression Brunor remembered: the one that made you feel like you were already in trouble.
The man crossed the pavilion with a hand resting on his sword. He was enormous. Not tall — Mikah was dense, built low and thick like the wall he guarded, wide enough to fill the checkpoint lane. His face was a mess of old scars and one fresh one, still raw across his left cheek, pink at the edges where the skin hadn’t finished knitting. His polished silver armor caught the sun. His eyes didn’t.
“Well, well, well.” Mikah’s voice came out as gravel dragged over stone. His dark eyes locked on Brunor’s. “Look at this.”
Brunor said nothing. The last time they’d spoken — two years ago, and badly — gave him no reason to think this would be pleasant.
Mikah’s gaze drifted to Elaine, who was watching a man across the road juggle apples with more enthusiasm than skill.
“My, yer a pretty one.” His eyes briefly traced her outline. “Who are ya?”
Elaine turned her head.
“Elaine Morrandis, 5th Scout Commander of Johanna’s Promise. The council was notified of our arrival — we’re here to answer a summons. Important matters.”
Mikah studied her the way a dog studies a bone it hasn’t decided to chew yet. 
His mouth moved before his expression changed.
“Yer Queen Lysandra’s kin. One of those lost Morrandis sisters.” His eyes narrowed. “Judging by the appeal, yer the older one. Where’s the smaller one? Phoebe Morrandis?”
Elaine’s hand shifted on the reins. 
“Not here,” she said.
“Yes, but where is she?” He repeated.
Elaine did not answer; instead, her attention went back to the man across the road, who was now asking for a volunteer. Nobody was offering themselves.
Mikah turned back to Brunor. Whatever patience he’d been wearing dropped.
“Why exactly are yer back in Glintgate? Don’t give me that ratshite about the council. Lie again, and I’ll run yer truth. Go on. Speak.”
Brunor kept his voice level. Mikah wasn’t one for games, and meeting his energy would end with both of them bleeding and the mission dead before it started.
“It’s as my fellow commander told you. We received a summons from the Archwarden himself. We are to appear before the council by week’s end.”
Nullbrand hummed against his hip. The black blade always did that when Brunor’s pulse climbed — or at least it felt that way.
“Now, may you step aside? We’re tired and hungry from riding.”
Mikah moved closer. He stuck out a thick hand and motioned for the letter.
“The summons. Give it ‘ere. Too many fools have tried forged letters to get past the gate. They won’t make that mistake twice. My sword saw to it. Give it.”
Brunor dismounted. He had to look up to meet Mikah’s eyes — the man had a way of carrying himself that added inches his body didn’t possess. He reached inside his cloak and produced the letter, stamped with the High Table’s seal of the Spire, and handed it over without a word.
Mikah’s eyes scanned the page cautiously, as someone who learned to read late and doubts their ability. Gradually, they moved from left to right, line by line, searching for something out of place.
Elaine interrupted before he could finish.
“Now that that’s done, I trust you won’t object. Some people have important things to do.” She didn’t bother hiding the edge. “Damien will flay you alive if you prevent this meeting from happening. I’m sure you’d rather not deal with his wrath again. Wouldn’t you, Mikah?”
Brunor’s fist tightened at his side. He felt something ugly rise from his chest.
Mikah glanced up from the letter and bit his lip. He handed the paper back and turned toward the gate, already walking.
“Very well,” he called over his shoulder. “Enter the city if ya must. But remember — my guards have orders to keep eyes on ya, boy. Keep this visit brief, and we won’t have any problems.” He stopped to look back at Elaine. “And you, lass. Don’t forget yer place.”
Brunor mounted and rode for the gate before his mouth could ruin what Elaine’s had just fixed. She fell in alongside him, and neither spoke until they reached the stables on the far side of the lower market.
A short, heavy man sat outside the stable doors, watching them with the enthusiasm of someone paid to sit and watch. As they neared, he stood and bowed.
“M’lord. M’lady. Commander Rowain is inside waiting for you. He does not seem pleased.”
Elaine nodded. They went in.
Frederick sat in the far corner on an upturned crate, stroking a whetstone down a blade that didn’t need sharpening. Sparks popped with each pass. His bull-shaped helmet sat on the ground beside him, and the claymore across his back rose past his shoulder like a second spine. It was odd seeing Frederick anywhere other than Caer Seryth. Frederick had more in common with the stones of the keep than most people in those days. 
He looked up when they entered, and the irritation on his face was immediate and total. His sandy blonde hair clung to his temples. The scar on the side of his head, given by Cain, had healed over, but Brunor noticed it as the sunlight beamed past the doors and onto Frederick's squared face. His brown eyes also caught that specific light and held it. Could Brunor catch it, too? He wondered this in the time it took to reach the horsing stall.
“Ah, so you two finally arrived. About damn time! Had me waiting a full day longer than expected.”
“Hello to you too, Frederick,” Elaine said, dismounting. She glanced at the sparks. “You shouldn’t sharpen a blade in a stable. You’ll start a fire and have us all hanging from a rope before the moons turn.”
His eyes widened. A laugh broke out of him, sudden and true, and he set the blade down. “Good point. Wasn’t thinking clearly — this damn war talk has my head spinning. Couldn’t go to the inn yet because I was stuck waiting for you two mongrels.”
“Quit your whinging, Frederick. It’s irritating me.”
“M’lady, you know I can’t do that! Whinging is my way to pass the time!” He got to his feet. A few joints cracked. His gaze found Brunor. “How were the travels, Commander? No trouble?”
“None,” Brunor said while dismounting. He thought of Mikah at the gate, but saw no purpose in bringing it up.
Frederick scratched the stubble along his jaw and moved toward the doors. “Good. Glad you made it safely. Roads have been rough lately.”
“Even the Silver Road?” Elaine asked.
“Even the Silver Road. Raiders and thieves are getting bolder now that there’s talk of war. We’ll speak of it later.” He pushed the door open with his shoulder and squinted into the sun. “For now, the current mission is to get as drunk as humanly possible before the council tomorrow. There’s a tavern around the corner named the Seahorse Fist that has a serving girl with three arms. I insist we investigate.”
“I’ll pass,” Elaine said.
She led Munch into the stall and began to brush him. The horse nosed into her hand, and Elaine murmured to him in a voice Brunor rarely heard her use with anything human.
“Suit yourself, El.” Frederick sounded wounded. “What about you, Brunor? Top shelf and three-armed serving girls?”
Brunor shook his head. “Maybe later. I have business first.”
Frederick threw up his hands. “Damn it. Fine. But I’m not investigating a three-armed woman without witnesses. Nobody would believe me.” He sauntered out, looking more defeated than a man heading toward good drink had any right to. “If you need me, I’ll be at the Crossroads. Drinking alone. Like a hound.”
The door swung shut behind him.
Brunor finished feeding his horse and then started toward the exit.
“Wait,” Elaine said.
She finished with Munch, set the brush down, and walked out of the stall. Her grey-blue eyes found his.
“Well? Come on. We have things to do.”
