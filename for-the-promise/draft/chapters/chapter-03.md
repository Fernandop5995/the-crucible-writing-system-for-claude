# Chapter 3: Phoebe
[8th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Flame | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger)]
[Afternoon | Caer Seryth, Vespur Plains, Halreth]
Backstep. Half spin. Strike. Pirouette. Counter. Thrust. Reset.
Backstep. Half spin. Strike. Pirouette. Counter. Thrust. Reset.
The dummy shuddered. The blade barely caught the wooden pole beneath the straw. Shallow. Phoebe groaned through her teeth.
She repeated the sequence as she had all morning, despite her balance refusing to settle. The sky was a pale blue; clouds scattered like sheep migrating across it. The wind off the Vespur Plains was not helping — heavy, cold, carrying the first warning of fall and whatever the cooks were ruining in the mess hall. Her stomach answered. She ignored it and went again.

The lost keep without a purpose. A relic from a bygone age that now housed Johanna’s Promise, six hundred strong. Phoebe never forgot the day she arrived. She had been six years old when Elaine carried her through those gates. Dirty, bruised from weeks in a carriage, too tired to walk. All she remembered of the first days inside was crying. For her dead mom. For Elaine, who was never there when Phoebe went looking. For everything they were not going to get back.
She shoved the memory aside and corrected her jab, but not before someone off to the side whistled.

She pushed the hazy thought away and corrected her messy jab, but not before someone off to the side whistled.
“Now that was sloppy, Phoebe. I thought I taught you and your sister better than that,” the voice said.
A woman. Rough and hoarse but with a gentle edge.
Tella limped forward with half a spiked flail in her gloved hand. The other half was missing, and Phoebe decided not to ask.
Phoebe did not look at her mentor. Instead, she did what Tella had taught her. She disregarded any excuse and went for it again.
The blade embedded itself in the wooden post again, and it was even shallower than the first time. Phoebe wanted to take her sword and smash it until nothing remained except straw and satisfaction.
Tella laid the broken flail down and hobbled over to the young scout captain. She placed a leathered hand over Phoebe’s strained knuckles. Phoebe immediately loosened her grip on her rapier.
“Gods, relax, Phoebe. You are too sprung. Release that pressure. If you got into an actual fight, it would just bog you down. Did I not teach you and your sister to move with the grain? With,” she emphasized, taking Phoebe’s arm and forcing her grip back onto the hilt. “Direct the blade with your soul. Fight it, and you’ll crash against the current.”
Phoebe rolled her eyes and yanked the blade free from the straw dummy. Hay flew everywhere. Tella blew a piece of it from Phoebe’s hair, her breath sweet with raspberry.
“You—” Her voice cracked and rose harshly, and Phoebe widened her eyes. Tella raised a brow. 
Another humiliation. The young captain considered becoming a quartermaster recruit if she did another in front of Tella.
Phoebe cleared her throat, her lips chapped and mouth painfully dry.
“What are you, a sailor now, here to lecture on the philosophy of the sea? Or are you only here to lecture me?” Phoebe asked. “Should the Scout’s first captain be distracted by petty things such as dummy drills?”
“Three questions. So we are angry today.”
Tella limped to stand beside the dummy.
“A dummy today. An enemy tomorrow. Don’t get smart with me. I can still defeat you in a duel with one hand tied, child.”
Phoebe scoffed and turned away rather than admit it — Tella had beaten her one-handed, more than once.
Quiet threatened to settle in. Tella broke it first.
“Don’t you hate when the commander vanishes on personal business? We’re left with Ryn barking orders, acting as if he’ll never relinquish the badge,” Tella said.
Phoebe shook her head. “It’s Ryn. He approaches everything with purpose. In that handsome dented head, he believes he’s the true commander.”
“Sounds like ego. Or the thing that comes after and is worse. Disloyalty. Only time will tell which is which.”
Tella crossed her arms and glanced down at Phoebe’s boots. Phoebe followed her gaze. One was her own. The other was smaller, black leather, grabbed in the dark and slipped on at dawn.
“So that’s why I couldn’t—” She stopped, sheathing her blade. “Any word from her yet?” Phoebe asked, taking a long drink of water.
“No, but it’s only been a few days. Patience, Captain Morrandis. Elaine knows exactly what she’s walking into at Glintgate.”
“Does that make it better? Brunor’s been restless and paranoid since the siege, and Frederick was only just cleared by West a few weeks ago. What if they run into trouble?”
“And what sort of trouble do you expect them to find in the middle of a major populated city?”
“The kind that doesn’t need a reason. Or the kind that has reason enough,” Phoebe said, turning away, her voice low and steady.
Again, she began her sequence, but Tella grabbed her shoulder, her grip firm yet gentle, demanding full attention.
“You mean *him,*” said Tella.
“Of course, I mean *him.* For years, he has held bitterness towards Elaine and, as a result, Johanna’s Promise. They are walking into his city under his jurisdiction — his watchful eye, his rules. He is going to strike when the opportunity presents itself, and I won’t be there to help her.”
Tella took a long, considering look into Phoebe’s eyes. The crow’s feet around her weathered brown eyes and the scars on her sun-darkened skin had stories behind them. Forts taken with skeleton crews. An enemy commander died behind his own lines during the war ten years back, a feat still whispered about in taverns. Phoebe yearned for those stories whenever Tella withheld them after training or during dry mess talks. Now, standing a few inches away and receiving the same look that had built that reputation, Phoebe wondered how those stories came to be.
Tella squeezed Phoebe’s shoulder more firmly this time, her eyes softening.
“Your cousin Damien is destructive, and he holds grudges longer than any man I have served under or fought against. But he is also a man of reason, whether you believe it or not. The Promise has given him no cause to act, and as long as our commanders keep their heads, things should go as planned.” She tapped the dummy. “Now run the sequence again. Properly, this time.”
Phoebe looked at her reflection in the blade and did not recognise the captain she was supposed to be.

“I’m finished for the day. I need to report to Ryn in a few,” Phoebe said, tossing the blade into the training barrel. She turned to leave but paused, casting one last look at Tella, who was studying the dummy.
Tella picked up the sword beside the dummy and ran the sequence Phoebe had been drilling all morning. The speed was difficult for Phoebe to track, even with her old injury.
Backstep. Half spin. Strike. Pirouette. Counter. Thrust
The blade embedded itself deep into the post and remained there as Tella stepped back. Without turning, she spoke.
“You underestimate your sister and, in turn, me. You have forgotten who trained her in both the standard and the political forms of warfare. Who taught her to use words as daggers and swords as bargaining chips.”
 The riftmark she carries puts weight behind every word she speaks. Elaine Morrandis is potentially the most dangerous woman on the continent and a political disaster for the Vel’Eran queen should she choose to be one, and Damien knows this.”
Tella yanked the blade free and examined it. “He is afraid of her. Of what she could do, should she need to. Remember that when you are worried about her.”
Phoebe turned and walked toward the scout wing, limping as a blister began to form on her left heel. The guard above waved to her as she stepped from the yard.
“Dangerous to her enemies and to herself,” Phoebe whispered.
