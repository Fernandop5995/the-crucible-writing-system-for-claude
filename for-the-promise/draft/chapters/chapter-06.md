## Chapter 6: Frederick

[12th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Threads | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger)]
[Midday into Evening | The Golden Scale Guildhall, Glintgate, Caltheran]

*I count what’s there. The trouble starts when what’s there counts back.*

Frederick knew the meeting had gone wrong before it began, because Brunor wasn’t in it.

He learned this in the street, forty paces from the Golden Scale’s doors, when he turned to make some remark about the guildhall’s gilded weathervane and found only Elaine at his shoulder, walking the way she walked when she had decided a thing and did not mean to argue it twice.

“Where is he.”

“Working,” Elaine said.

“Working.” Frederick stopped walking. A porter behind them swore and heaved his barrow around the obstruction Frederick’s body made of any street he stood still in. “We are about to sit down across from the family that publicly promised to disband us, in their own hall, in their own city, on a day named for pact-binding — and our commander of the garrison is *working.*”

“He’s pulling the thread. The one from the council chamber.” She kept her eyes on the guildhall doors as she said it, and that was how Frederick knew this had been settled between the two of them somewhere he wasn’t. “Someone had to. I can’t be in two rooms, and I need to be in this one.”

“He told you.”

“Barely.”

“He didn’t tell me.”

“No,” Elaine said. “He didn’t.”

Frederick took it the way he took inventory that came up short: there would be a reckoning over the missing stock later. Not now. Now the doors were opening, and a steward in Beauforth wheat-gold was bowing them in, and Frederick did what he always did on bad ground: he started counting.

Eight house guards in the entrance colonnade, spears dressed, gambesons new. Two more on the gallery above, crossbows unstrung but near to hand. The floor was Vel’Eran marble, the kind that cost more to ship than to quarry, and the chandeliers overhead burned lamp-oil sweetened against the river’s smell, and the whole vast room had been built to say one thing in a language every merchant read fluently: *we can afford to waste this.* Frederick priced the entrance hall out of habit and arrived at a figure that would have fed Caer Seryth through two winters, and felt better for knowing it the way a man feels better holding a rail in a swaying cart.

They gave up their blades at an inlaid table — Elaine unbuckled her sword as if the ceremony bored her, and Frederick laid the claymore down and watched two porters decide how to be dignified about carrying it. The steward’s eyes went over them the whole while, pricing. Frederick priced him back.

“The Lord Chancellor sends his regrets,” the steward said, leading them beneath the gallery. “Affairs of the city detain him. The Lady Beauforth will receive you.”

Beside him, Elaine’s stride did not change at all, which was itself a tell. Frederick kept his face amiable and let the fact walk with them down the corridor. Damien had demanded this meeting on pain of everything — *by week’s end, do try not to fuck up twice* — and then declined to attend it. Either the snub was the message, or the wife was, and Frederick had spent enough years reading which goods a merchant put in the window to know it was the second one. You did not send your own blade to a bargaining table; you sent the better instrument.

The receiving room was small and warm on purpose. No dais. A round table — round, so there was no head to claim and no foot to be seated at — laid with wine, white bread, salt, and honey. Guest-fare. Old-road courtesy, the kind that meant *you are safe under this roof,* offered by the house that had drawn steel on them in a public square four days ago.

Lady Beauforth stood to meet them.

She was not what the square had led him to expect, and Frederick understood before the door had closed behind them that this was the point of her. Damien wore his gold like a drawn blade; his lady wore wheat-colored silk the way a clerk wears ink — a working dress on a working woman, the lion of House Beauforth stitched small at the collar where a brooch would shout. Dark hair pinned plainly. She took them in one at a time, unhurried, and smiled like she meant it.

“Commander Rowain. Commander Morrandis.” No titles fumbled, no bastard. She gestured them to the table like a woman gathering family for supper. “Thank you for coming. My husband is a man of many gifts, but conversation with people he has bled on is not among them. Sit. Eat something, please — the bread is from our own ovens, and I will be personally offended by restraint.”

*Gracious where he’s cruel,* Frederick thought, and sat, and broke the bread, and ate the salt and honey, because refusing guest-fare said more than any sentence he might spend an hour regretting. Elaine sat after him and ate a mouthful of bread like a woman signing a document she hadn’t read.

The talk opened small, and Lady Beauforth was good at small. The state of the roads. The price the war-talk had put on grain — she asked Frederick’s professional opinion and then *listened to it*, head tilted, asking the second question and the third, the ones that proved the first hadn’t been courtesy. Within ten minutes Frederick had been walked, gently and by his own tongue, through half the Promise’s supply concerns, and he pulled himself up short on the far side with the respect one quartermaster reserves for another who has just inventoried him through a smile.

Elaine, in all that time, said nothing at all. She ate what courtesy required, kept both hands above the table, and never lost the service door at Lady Beauforth’s back — the way she held every room now that Glintgate had shown it was watching them first.

He put his cup down. “My lady. The bread is excellent, the wine is better than we deserve, and you could teach interrogation to the Council’s own scribes. What does House Beauforth want with Johanna’s Promise?”

She laughed — a real one, brief, unoffended. “Damien said you were the reasonable one.”

“He said nothing of the sort.”

“No,” she agreed, and refilled his cup herself. “He called you a pack ox with a ledger. But he meant it as I said it.” She set her own cup down untouched, and the warmth in the room stayed exactly where it was while the conversation underneath it changed key. “Very well, Commander. Plainly, then. My husband believes your company is a spent blade — dangerous to whoever grips it, worth nothing at market. I believe he is wrong. I believe you are the only neutral steel on this continent that five kingdoms will still allow inside their borders, and that whatever is coming will make that worth more than any tariff, treaty, or title. He wants you broken.” She said it the way another woman might report the weather.

She tore a piece of the bread then, and ate it — her own guest-fare, under her own roof, chewed and swallowed while they watched her do it. “I want you *solvent.* Those two desires are not as opposed as you might think. His pride only needs one of you humbled. His house needs you whole.”

“And which of you,” Elaine said, “are we speaking with today?”

“Both. That is what marriage is, Commander Morrandis. The only pact-binding that survives is the kind where each party carries the other’s purse.” Lady Beauforth folded her hands. “Now. You will want to know the price of the bread. Here is what I am prepared to give you.”

She said it plainly, and the plainness had been rehearsed:

“The men who took your Captain Alessa on the Glintgate road. Their names. The name of the broker who bought her route, the house where he counts his coin, and the ledger that ties the payments together. Delivered to you in writing, with my seal on it, to do with as your justice requires.”

Nobody reached for the wine.

Frederick did not look at Elaine. It took a discipline he intended to be paid for someday — because beside him Elaine’s listening had become aim, and he knew, the way he knew his own inventory, exactly what had just been set on the table. Not coin, not contracts. The one debt in the company’s books that had been bleeding for two months — the debt Brunor had buried and Elaine had demanded payment on in a rain-soaked alley, *where is the justice in it* — offered up now in a warm room by a smiling woman, wrapped in guest-bread.

*Offers you exactly what you need,* Frederick thought, *and watches your face while you decide whether the price is hidden in the offer.*

She was watching Elaine’s face.

“You will forgive the observation, my lady,” Frederick said, keeping his voice at market-day, buying the seconds Elaine needed, “but your husband’s court protected those men. Half of Glintgate believes he paid them.”

“Half of Glintgate believes rain is the gods weeping. My husband is vicious, Commander, not stupid — butchering a Promise escort on his own road, over pride? That is a debt with no yield. He would not spend that way.” She said it with total, unbothered certainty, and underneath the certainty Frederick heard the thing she did not know she was telling them: *she had asked herself the same question and gone looking.* “But someone spent it, in his city, wearing his colors closely enough that the smell settled on this house. That offends me in ways I find useful. So: their names. My seal.”

“For what?” Elaine asked. The cup in front of her had gone a quarter-turn, and then another.

“Three days ago, the entire Council of Twelve sat in a borrowed room in my city,” Lady Beauforth said, “with no clerks, no records, and no explanation — and summoned a mercenary company to attend them. I would like to know what they wanted.”

There was the price, hidden where prices are always hidden — inside the thing you need most.

“You want us to sell you the Council,” Frederick said.

“I want to know what moves into my city before it unpacks,” she said. “Call it what you like.”

Frederick counted. Elaine beside him, calm enough to fool anyone who had known her less than thirty years. The guards beyond the door, the crossbows above the gallery, the bread in his own stomach that meant nobody would be murdered before supper. And over all of it, the shape of the trade. Alessa’s justice against the Council’s secrets. A debt they were owed, priced in a debt they’d be taking on. Two ledgers sliding across a round table, and Damien’s wife watching to see which one they reached for.

“No,” Elaine said.

Lady Beauforth’s eyebrows rose a fraction — the first unplanned thing her face had done.

“Not because we don’t want the names.” There was no anger left in Elaine’s voice at all, which Frederick had learned to find more alarming than the shouting. “I want them so badly I can taste iron. That’s how I know what the trade is. You’d give us our own justice back at the price of making the Promise a house asset — the first payment’s always the one you’d have made anyway. My cousin’s family taught me that at an age when I still thought bread and salt meant safety.” She stood, unhurried, and inclined her head with a courtesy that gave nothing. “The bread was excellent. We’re grateful for the names you’re going to give us anyway.”

“Am I.”

“You said it yourself, my lady. Someone spent blood in your city, wearing your colors, and it offends you.” Elaine held her eyes. “You didn’t buy that ledger to file it.”

The two women looked at each other across the guest-fare, and Frederick, who had spent his life reading rooms, could not have priced this one to save his neck. Then Lady Beauforth laughed again — quieter this time, with appetite in it.

“Damien fights everyone the same way,” she said, “and one day it will kill him. I don’t. Good afternoon, Commanders. My steward will see you out.” And then, as they reached the door, without any change of tone at all: “Do give Commander Halvyr my regards. Whatever he’s looking for in the lower wards — I do hope he finds it before it finds him.”

They collected their blades in silence. The street took them back with rain on it.

-----

They did not speak until the Golden Scale was two bridges behind them, and then they spoke at the same time.

“She knew where—”
“The names are real.”

Frederick waved her on. Elaine pulled her cloak-hood up against the drizzle, and beneath the low grey light her face had the emptied look it wore when the mark had been working through a long room. “The names are real,” she said again. “Everything under her was smooth walls — she performs warmth the way Tauvor performs calm, and I couldn’t get under them without pushing. But when she made the offer, the performance and the ground under it matched. She has the ledger. She means to use it.” She pulled the hood lower. “And when she said Damien didn’t pay for Alessa, she believed it.”

“Which means someone bought Promise blood in Beauforth colors,” Frederick said, “and the chancellor’s own wife can’t find the purse it came from.”

“And it offends her.” Elaine looked back the way they had come. “It should offend her. It’s her house it’s wearing.”

Frederick had not found the bottom of that thought when a shape peeled out of a doorway on Candler’s Row ahead of them — big, hooded, moving with a gait he’d have known in full dark on no sleep — and became Brunor, soaked through, with his hood back and his face wrong.

Frederick had seen Brunor bleed, freeze, bury soldiers, and be wrong. He had not often seen him spooked.

“Not here,” Brunor said, before either of them had a word out. “Walls.”

They found a carter’s arch off the row, stone on three sides, rain curtaining the fourth. Brunor stood with his back to the dry wall, where he could see the street both ways, and told it in fragments — and Frederick, who had spent half a lifetime assembling this man’s fragments into loads a cart could carry, stacked them as they came.

The western ward first. Brunor’s old connections — the houses that dealt in the kind of information that never touched paper, the performers and the door-men and the women who heard everything said after wine. “Gone quiet,” Brunor said. “Not dead quiet. *Moved* quiet. Kessa’s house has new girls who don’t know my name. Doors that owed me are polite now. Polite and empty. The whole ward’s been —” he searched for it “— swept. Softly. Over months. Nobody ordered it and everybody obeyed it. There should be noise there, Frederick. There’s a silence where the noise should be, and the silence has edges.”

The docks second. A warehouse row on the Aldren where his last living contact still kept a counting-office. “Three warehouses paying night crews triple to move wool,” Brunor said. “I watched one crew work half a bell. Six men to a crate, dead-lifting, boards flexing. Wool doesn’t bend oak, and it doesn’t get carried like it bites. The manifests say Varkaan trade. The drafts on the barges sit too deep by a hand. Whatever’s in those crates, it isn’t written down honestly anywhere in this city — and the harbor-men have learned not to see it, the same soft way the ward learned.”

“Coin does that,” Frederick said, without believing it.

“Coin buys silence that leaks.” Brunor pressed a thumb along the wet bandage on his forearm. “This doesn’t leak. That’s the wrongness of it — it’s not corruption, Frederick, it just wears corruption’s clothes. Corruption is a hundred men each taking a cut. This is one will, spending through a hundred hands. The pattern only shows if you stand far enough back, and I’ve been standing back for three days.” He stopped. The rain filled the arch with its noise.

“Say the rest,” Elaine said.

“My contact at the counting-office. Old man, owes me his son’s life, hasn’t lied to me in fifteen years. I asked him who holds the warehouse paper.” Brunor leaned off the wall, close enough that the rain could not carry it past the three of them. “He didn’t answer that. He answered the question I hadn’t asked yet. Told me the same men came asking questions in the spring — asking after the Promise, after our contracts, our routes. And one name.” His eyes went to Elaine first, the way a man looks at the one the news is for. “*Neorxn.*” Frederick watched the color leave her face. “Said clean, said correctly, by a dockside broker who shouldn’t know it exists. Then my man leaned across his counting-desk, and do you know what he said to me? He said: *they stopped asking the day your company rode through the Silver Gate. Whatever they wanted to know, Halvyr — they know it now. And they left word, in case you came. They said to tell you the accounts are all in order.*”

Nobody said anything. The rain came down on Candler’s Row like it was being paid to.

“It’s not a cell,” Brunor said. “It’s not a scout, it’s not a spy-ring wintering in a friendly city. It’s *groundwork.* Warehouses, wards, silences, purses — laid down over years, under the chancellor’s own floor. Celisia at the gate wasn’t an arrival, El. She was a *greeting.*” He wiped the rain off his face with the flat of his hand, and Frederick saw the tremor in it, gone by the time the hand dropped. “We spent three days thinking the Order followed us into Glintgate. They didn’t follow us anywhere. They were the ground we walked in on. And they watched me count their teeth, all three days, the way you watch a man read a letter you wrote him.”

Frederick stood in the arch with the rain sheeting off the stone, and did the only thing he had ever known how to do with a night like this one. He counted it.

The Council’s contract, wrong but paying, with a lie folded somewhere in the middle of it that none of them could find yet. Item.

The Beauforth ledger — Alessa’s justice in a sealed envelope, held by a smiling woman whose city had been hollowed out under her family’s feet, who knew where Brunor spent his days better than Frederick had. Item.

And the Order of the Wyrm: not coming, not hunting, *resident* — patient as compounding interest, holding paper on half the docks, in possession of a name so buried that Frederick himself went months at a time forgetting the Hollow owned it, and courteous enough to leave a message saying so. Item.

Three creditors. One small company. Every account in order.

“Well,” Frederick said at last, because someone had to, and it was always him. “I’ll say this much for Glintgate. Nobody’s tried to sell us a three-armed serving girl.”

Elaine laughed — one breath of it, the first the day had gotten out of her — and put her back against the stone between her two commanders, and the three of them stood watching the rain hammer the empty row.

The city had its teeth in them now. Frederick had counted every one, and the count kept coming up the same.

They were not the ones holding the ledger.
