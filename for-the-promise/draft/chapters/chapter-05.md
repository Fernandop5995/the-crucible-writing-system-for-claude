## Chapter 5: Elaine
“Another round, Beatrice! The Vel’Eran spirits, top shelf — you know, the one with that silver ribbon. My friends here like the good stuff.” The serving girl had introduced herself as Beatrice a couple of minutes earlier and had been eyeing Frederick ever since. Elaine didn’t need the mark to read her; it was obvious: warmth, desire, shamelessness. Beatrice winked at the table where the three commanders of Johanna’s Promise sat and reached for the bottle.
Surrounding them, men and women of all statuses pressed inward from the storm, and the crowd further contributed to the mark, intensifying the underlying bitterness. Elaine despised the mark most when it preyed upon her vexation. It only sharpened both. 
A merchant near the window, counting his coins for the second time, carried a loss he was not going to name to anyone at this table or any other. The mark attempted another read, but Elaine shut it out. It was the press of the whole room that wore on her, the accumulated weight of forty people crammed together out of the rain, all of them carrying something, none of it hers.
Beatrice returned with the bottle and carried on with something rehearsed about Frederick’s bulging arms.
“Leave it,” Elaine snapped. The girl’s tiny head nodded and she scurried off to the front.
Brunor had not spoken since the rain. Craven, she thought, recognized it as false even as she thought it — he was the bravest man she knew, which made his behavior today all the more unacceptable. And Frederick with all his wisdom either hadn’t noticed the silence between them or had drowned it three cups ago. Elaine set her coin on the latter.
The table displayed an array of fruits, breads, and cheeses. The Vel’Eran spirit bottle caught the candlelight from the chandelier above, its silver ribbon occasionally drawing Elaine's eye. She reached for a grape but paused, for a long dark hair was wound around it. 
She sighed, and the sound drew Frederick’s warm gaze.
“El! Join me for a drink! On me! Actually…” he slammed another cup in front of her and half the ale sloshed onto the table. “ Have two! He took a heavy sip and then placed a firm hand on Brunor’s shoulder.
"Ahhh, wonderful," the quartermaster exclaimed, a satisfied smile crossing his face. He grabbed a handful of grapes, eagerly popping them into his mouth without looking, including the peculiar-haired one among them. 
“Another time. We must talk.” Her fingers drummed the table. The mark had woken fully now that she sat far enough from Brunor for Nullbrand’s reach to fade, and the room pressed against her with every pulse it carried. “There is a lot to sort through,” she said, and gave Brunor a look he refused to meet. He kept his eyes on the rain against the window.
“Right. Does this have anything to do with why our friend’s wearing a fleshwound and a face like a whipped farmhand?”
“My cousin, Damien, found us in the square.” She said under her breath. “Drew steel. Brunor couldn’t help himself. Right *old friend?*”
Brunor did not respond. 
For a brief moment, she considered tossing a spell at him.
“So they fought a contest of arms,” she continued.
The smile went out of Frederick’s face. He took his hands off the mug.
“I see,” he said. “What else?”
Elaine waited. She wanted Brunor to take it — to lay the thing bare for the oldest friend he had, to say the shameful part out loud, so that what she’d thrown at him in the rain might finally find a way in. He gave nothing. The mark caught the silhouette of him, sealed shut, and the hush only gave it more to burn.
Frederick read whatever crossed her face and pulled back into his chair.
“I should start from the beginning,” she said.
So she did. The necklace, and the man who’d bought it to be seen buying it. The note. The square, and the hooded man who’d found them there, and his letter. Damien, his golden blade, the contest — and her interruption, which earned a gasp and laugh from Frederick. Brunor poured a small flagon and drank. She told him about the woman at the gate, wearing a grey hood.
She left out the rain and the words it said.
Frederick turned the mug slowly between his palms and did not drink from it.
“The grey hood,” he said. “Any idea who it was?”
“I’m getting to it.”
“You’re getting to it in the same manner Brunor gives his apologies. When he’s cornered.”
Brunor’s grey eyes came off the window to look at Frederick, then they went back to the rain. He said nothing, and his look was hard enough that Beatrice, passing with a tray, slowed her step and decided against whatever she’d meant to ask.
Elaine watched Frederick watch Brunor. He knew. Of course, he knew — Frederick had spent thirty years learning the weather of that face, just as she had. He just refused to be the one to name it, which meant he’d already decided whose side this was, and it wasn’t going to be hers tonight.
“Frederick.”
“El.”
“Did you know about Alessa before or after you helped him decide I shouldn’t?”
The quartermaster shifted in his chair, causing it to groan in response.
He looked at her for a long moment, and to his credit, he didn’t reach for the obvious answer. He didn’t tell her it was complicated, didn’t say we did what we thought was best, didn’t do any of the soft retreats a lesser man would have hidden behind. He just held her eyes and let the answer be what it was.
“Before,” he said. “I read the report the same morning Brunor did. I sat with him in the war room, and we talked it through, and I told him you had enough rope around your neck already. So if you’re looking to hang somebody for it, you’ll need two ropes. I’ll not have you put it all on him and call it settled.”
Elaine rolled her eyes. She wanted to toss a spell at him, too.
“Two ropes. Tempting,” she repeated.
“Two.” He picked the mug back up and took a swallow before continuing with his slurred rehearsal. “I’d rather neither, but I’m a quartermaster. I count what’s there.”
Brunor said, quietly, to the window: “It wasn’t his idea.”
“I didn’t say it was,” Elaine said.
“You were thinking it.”
Elaine gritted her teeth, and she felt her temple begin to twitch. “I am thinking a great many things. Don’t flatter yourself by guessing which.”
The rain pushed harder against the glass. Somewhere behind the bar, a cask was tapped, and the room gave up a small ragged cheer, and the three of them sat inside it like a stone the river had decided to go around.
Frederick broke first as usual.
“The hood,” he said again. Gentler now. “Tell me, El. Whatever it is, it can’t be worse than the two of you sitting here trying to murder each other with looks.”
“There was a woman at the gate. Grey hood, no crest, close enough to the front that the blood should have moved her. It didn’t.” She turned her cup a quarter without noticing she’d done it. “You know how a crowd is for me now. Every soul in that square was screaming something. The choir was terrified. The merchants wanted it finished so they could go back to their stalls. Damien’s guards wanted leave to move. Even Damien…All of them pouring something out whether they meant to or not. Her, nothing.”

Frederick’s brow drew down. “Nothing how. Calm?”
“Not calm. Calm has a shape. There wasn’t a single emotion I could read. It was...inhuman. I’ve felt that once before, Frederick. In the courtyard, the day I came back from the Vigil last year, before the chroma storm. Off Mark, before any of us knew what he was.”
The mug went still in Frederick’s hands.
Brunor’s eyes came off the window. The pain in his eyes was not hidden, nor was the shame and guilt he was now radiating. Even now, Brunor blamed himself for what happened last year.
“So who could it have been? He asked. “I’ll admit she did seem familiar. I saw her for only a moment before she turned and vanished into the crowd. She reminded me of...” Brunor stopped, now coming to the conclusion Elaine had reached back in the square.
Nobody said anything.
Elaine glanced at the tables nearest them. Nobody close enough to hear, but the word itself carried weight she did not want loose in a city that was not hers. She reached down into her bag, retrieved a piece of charcoal, and traced one word on the handkerchief next to her cup.
*Scaleblade.*
“A scaleblade,” Frederick whispered finally, “You’re certain?”
“I’m certain of the gait and the feel. You don’t forget a monster like that.”
Elaine drank a small mouthful of spirit and set the cup down. The sweet wine burned her throat more than she expected, which made her cough once. 
“Celisia isn’t hiding anything. She walked to the front and held still until I noticed, and then she let me feel the same nothing he felt long ago, on purpose, and left before I could do a thing about it. I don’t know why she’s empty the way he was. I don’t know what it means that they read the same.” She lifted her eyes. “That’s what stopped the duel. Not your pride, Brunor. I needed both your blades down and both your heads up before whatever she is decided the square was worth her time.”
For a while, none of them spoke, and for once, the silence belonged to the three of them equally.
Frederick reached for the bottle with the silver ribbon and filled all three cups to the brim without asking.
“The Order followed us to Glintgate,” Frederick said. He set the bottle down. “Past the gate. Into Damien’s own city.”
“He does not know what she is,” Elaine said. “He would never believe me if I told him. And I am not certain telling him would do anything but get us both hanged faster.”
“Yet he is so busy carving lumps out of your pride he has not the first idea they are standing in it with him.”

Brunor finally moved. He took the full cup and drank half of it. When he set it back, his voice was rough from the long silence.
“The council meeting just became the least of our problems.”

The College of Cvostral sat at the end of the Goldroad behind a low wall of silver-veined stone — three storeys of pale rune-cut granite, narrow windows set deep into the facade, a building that was built to last longer than the institution inside it. The guild’s sigil hung above the main arch: the Slivermen’s crossed quill and wardstone, worked in iron that had gone green with age. On any ordinary day, the halls behind it would have belonged to journeymen bent over copywork and masters arguing leylines.

Today the doors stood open, and the crowd spilling from them told Elaine everything she needed to know before she crossed the threshold. The Caltheran court had taken it

Inside, the College no longer looked like a school. The main hall was built for study — long reading tables, high shelves, a vaulted ceiling cut with rune channels that still held faint light — but the tables had been shoved against the walls and the floor was standing room. Nobles in Caltheran weave pressed shoulder to shoulder with guild men in working leather. Runescribes clutched tomes against their chests as though the pages might be taken from them. A knot of commoners who had come from gods-knew-how-far up the Aldren waited along the far wall. The whole anxious breadth of the kingdom had come to be heard by Twelve who had no business holding court in a guild’s house.
The want off them hit as a single thing and she let it wash past. She had learned that much, at least.
They were not three steps further before the argument found them.* *
Two men stood in the aisle between the first row of benches and the guild’s main reading table, close enough to share breath. One wore the grey-trimmed robe of a landed lord — a keep holder from the northern wards, by the cut. The other was younger, heavier, with the sigil of House Darrian stitched to his breast. His face was as red as an apple, which told Elaine he was losing whatever argument he was engaged in.
”—and if the Council continues to ignore the rift while pouring silver into the Archive’s coffers, the outer settlements will empty before winter. My tenants are already moving south. I cannot hold them, and the Twelve will not protect them. What would you have me do? This *is* the only option,” the younger man said.
“Then petition for a garrison. That is what the rest of us do.”
“I have petitioned.” Darrian’s voice climbed a step; he heard it and hauled it back down. “Twice. The first sat a season in the Archive before any answer came, and do you know what the answer was, Osfeld? A runescribe. One man, sent to renew the wardstones on a border I can walk in an afternoon. My smallfolk watched him work for three days and watched him ride south on the fourth, and that same evening four more families rode after him.”
“Wardstones hold.”
“Wardstones hold against what tests them slowly. You have not seen what comes off the fen when the light fails. My reeve has. He sleeps in the granary loft now and will not say why.” Darrian drew a folded paper from his breast and held it up between two fingers. “Eleven holders’ seals. Eleven. We pool the coin, we buy the steel, and the outer wards see spears before the frost instead of a scribe after it.”
Osfeld did not look at the paper. He looked at the doors, and along the benches, and at who stood near enough to hear, and when he spoke again it was barely above the noise of the hall.
“Fold that away. Gods keep you, man, fold it away. You would stand under their sigil and tell the Twelve to their faces that the Council cannot protect its own wards?”
“I would tell them what every holder north of the Aldren already says at his own table.”
“There is a name for a lord who arms himself with hired steel because he has judged his government a failure. The Council knows the name better than you do.”
Under the old lord’s procedure the mark caught what it was draped over: fear, worn smooth from long handling. He was not defending the Twelve. He was surviving them, and had been for years.
Darrian opened his mouth to answer and stopped. His gaze had gone past Osfeld’s shoulder, past Elaine, and settled on the ruin of Brunor’s nose — the face half of Glintgate had watched bleed in Bordou Square the evening before. The paper came down. The argument died with it, and the hall’s want swung round like weather turning.
“Well,” Frederick murmured behind her. “I believe we’re the paper in his hand.”

Elaine kept walking. Brunor’s stride did not change beside her, though his hand moved a fraction closer to Nullbrand. Frederick, half a pace behind, exhaled once through his nose, the way when he was deciding whether something was worth answering. None of them stopped. None of them corrected the man. The words landed where words always landed when the people speaking them did not know who was listening.
But others knew.
A runescribe near the doors had tracked them from the threshold, his quill frozen above the ledger. Two guild warders at the far pillar exchanged a glance and straightened. A Caltheran officer in polished mail studied Brunor’s sword with the careful attention of a man cataloguing a weapon he had heard described but never seen. The room did not go quiet — it was too full for that, too many voices arguing too many grievances — but a current ran through the nearest bodies, a recognition that passed from one set of eyes to the next the way fire jumps between dry branches.
Elaine felt the shift before the mark confirmed it. Curiosity, wariness, and beneath both, the particular discomfort of people who had been discussing a thing and were now standing in the same room as the thing itself.
Then a woman’s gaze settled on her and did not move.
She stood apart from the crowd near the eastern window, where the light from the Goldroad fell across the stone floor in pale bands. She was not tall, but the space around her suggested height — no one stood within arm’s reach of her, and the absence looked deliberate, as though the crowd had arranged itself to give her room without being asked. She wore deep green over grey riding leather, no jewellery save a brooch at her throat bearing the twin-tower sigil of Morninghall. Her dark hair was braided close against her head in the military fashion, and her face had the settled composition of a woman who had stopped needing to prove she belonged in rooms like this long before Elaine was born.
Unara Lackmin. Queen of Morninghall. Elder sister of Warden Narana, who sat among the Twelve that had summoned them here. Her keep garrisoned five thousand soldiers and sat close enough to Glintgate that a forced march could put them at the Silver Gate before the moons turned. Her husband, the Hallowed Commander of the Knights of Piety, kept the church’s sword arm sharp enough that even Damien treated the order with public deference.
Elaine knew the name. She had never stood in the same room as the woman who carried it.
Unara did not approach. She did not nod or acknowledge them in any way a bystander would notice. She looked at Elaine the way Tella looked at a blade she had not yet decided to draw — measuring the weight, the balance, the question of whether the edge was worth the trouble of testing.
Then she looked at Brunor. Then at Frederick. Then back to Elaine, and stayed.
The mark gave nothing. Not the void Celisia had left — this was simply a woman whose composure was deep enough that whatever she felt sat below the waterline, and Elaine could not reach it without pushing. She did not push.
“She’s watching us,” Frederick said, low enough for the three of them.
“I know,” Elaine said. “I don’t know what she wants yet.”
“Lovely. More politics.”
“Worse,” Elaine said. “The kind with an army behind it.”
The steward found them before the hall had finished turning. A Silverman by his grey, though the ribbon at his sleeve was council-yellow, and he asked no names. “The Wardens will receive Johanna’s Promise. Come.”
The crowd gave them a lane the way water gives one, closing behind. Halfway down it, Damien.
He stood among a knot of river merchants with a cup he was not drinking from, and whatever he had just said had them laughing as eager men laugh for a lord who decides their taxations. The bruise Brunor had given him sat plain on his cheek like a champion sporting an award. His eyes found hers over the merchants’ heads and moved on without any degree of hesitation.
She reached for him with the mark and got nothing. Smooth walls. She let it slide off him to the yellow liveries at his back instead, hunting grey among them, and found none — which told her only that the Order dressed better than she feared.
The chamber doors stood open at the aisle’s end.


Archwarden Tauvor was talking when they came in, and he did not stop.
Not to them but to a bald, stern-looking warden on his left, whom Elaine recognized by the name of Jacket Onlwald, a few words finished low while the doors were still swinging, a hand turned over on the bare table to close whatever point he’d been making. Then he set it down and let the three of them walk the length of the floor in the quiet he’d left for exactly that and made to wait by a man who’d timed it.
Elaine looked around. Two doors, the one at their backs and a half-curtained servant’s door behind the dais. No windows. A long table with nothing on it — no charter, no wine, no ink. Whatever the Twelve meant to do here, they meant to leave no record of having done it.
And the room itself was wrong. Frederick felt it too. He’d gone quiet at her shoulder, the quiet he kept for rooms he might have to cut his way out of.
Then she stepped clear of Brunor and Nullbrand’s hush slid off her, and the mark came awake, and the Twelve stopped being a wall of matched robes. A Warden three seats down was frightened and wearing it as boredom. Further along the arc, an old guilt sat packed and quiet under a face doing nothing at all — she couldn’t say which man it belonged to, only that it was years deep. None of it touched their hands. Practiced men kept their secrets in their faces and let their faces lie. Only the mark called it.
“You came.” The voice took her off guard. It carried the length of the hall without rising off the floorboards, low and certain, and it pulled every eye to him without asking for them, and it came out of a head that looked three days from the grave. Grey skin drawn to the skull. Black hair lank at the collar. A dying man wearing a smith’s shoulders under the robe. “Within the week. I’d wagered against it.” The brown eyes found Brunor’s split lip, the bruise going yellow across his nose, and stayed there a moment past courtesy. “Glintgate seems to have charged you for the road.”
Three seats down, the frightened one shifted. Elaine held her eyes on Tauvor and let it pass.

“The city was generous,” Brunor said.

Something moved through the arc of the Twelve — not sound, not quite amusement. The mark caught its edges: relief off two of them, that the summoned mercenaries had wit instead of grievance. Wariness off a third. The frightened one gave nothing but the same boredom, worn smooth as a coin.

“So I see.” Tauvor did not smile. He laid both hands flat on the bare table, and Elaine understood the gesture was the whole ceremony — no wine, no charter, no clerk in the corner scratching the minutes into being. What was said in this room would exist only in the fourteen skulls that heard it. “Commander Halvyr. Commander Morrandis. Commander Rowain. Thank you for answering the summons. I will not spend your patience on courtesies. The Council requires your company’s service, and you will not understand the shape of the work until you understand what we have done. So I am going to tell you what we have done.”

*We,* the mark noted, even before Elaine did. Not *what has happened.* A man laying the first stone of a bridge he already knew was rotten.

“Two seasons past, an asset came into the Archive’s keeping. A living specimen. You know this, of course.” His eyes rested on them evenly, one after another. “Your company carried it.”

“The sealed contract,” Frederick said. His voice was easy. His hands had gone still on his belt. “Crated cargo, Caltheran roads, no questions in writing. Your seal, Archwarden.”

“Ours,” Tauvor agreed. “The specimen is called a Wyrshade. I will not burden you with the taxonomy — the relevant fact is that our researchers judged it safe to study, and I signed the order to open it up while it lived. Fifteen of the Archive’s finest attended the procedure. Scholars of the old records. Runescribes. Two masters of the Kymantic pillar.” He paused, exactly long enough. “All fifteen are dead.”

Nobody in the arc moved. That was its own answer — Elaine let the mark drift the crescent of matched robes and felt the seam where the Council split. Four of them held the stillness of men hearing a story they had memorized. The rest held the stillness of men hearing the floor creak under their own feet. *Not all of them were told. He confessed to his own Council in pieces.*

“The procedure was sound,” Tauvor went on. “The restraints were sound. The wards were sound. None of it mattered, because the creature did not resist with its body. When the third incision opened, it — showed them something.” For the first time his voice checked, a hitch smaller than a breath, and the mark read what rode beneath it and Elaine kept her face empty by old practice. Fear. Not the performing kind. The kind that has been slept beside. “Every soul in that theater was struck at once with a vision none of them were built to survive. Our survivors of the aftermath — those outside the wards — describe the researchers screaming of the same things in the same breath. A rift, open before them. A rune inside it, burning with a will of its own. And something beyond the rune, in the dark behind the light, that looked back.”

The room’s torchlight seemed to sit very still.

“Every one of them went mad,” Tauvor said. “Not in the way grief makes men mad. At once. Together. As one.”

Elaine had stopped hearing him somewhere inside the last sentence, because she had recognized the spell.

*Flow. Emotion. Intent.* Lune’s words, from a colder room, a bandaged arm, a year that had taken pieces of her she was still counting. Kymancy did not conjure and it did not burn; it moved along what was already there, the way water finds a channel. There was a working Lune had named for her once — a casting that tapped the memory soaked into a place and made the caster see it. Detect Memory, the scribes called it, and it was gentle, and it asked.

This had not asked. The Wyrshade had not attacked fifteen minds. It had opened a channel and *made them look* — turned the spell inside out and poured its own remembering down fifteen throats at once.

She said none of it. She stood with her hands loose and her face schooled and the knowledge sitting behind her teeth like a stone, because the room held at least one Warden whose fear was older than this meeting and one whose guilt was years deep, and she could not name which robes they wore. A word spoken here would live in twelve memories she could not read all the way down. And the word behind the word — the one that would drag Melody’s reports and the Vigil and the mark on her own arm into the light with it — that one she could not afford at any price.

Brunor had not moved. She knew his stillness the way she knew her own pulse, and this was the worst one — the stillness he wore when a thing stopped being a story and started being a war.

“Madness we could have carried,” Tauvor said. “The Archive has quiet rooms and patient healers, and we have weathered our own before. What came after, we were not built for either.” His hands stayed flat on the table. His voice stayed level. It cost him; the mark was under the floorboards of the man now, and the cost ran through him like water through a wall. “They turned on one another. I have read every account and I will not repeat most of them. Understand the shape of it: wounds that should have dropped a man meant nothing. Opened bodies kept working. Colleagues of thirty years took each other apart with instruments and teeth, and they did not stop when they were killed, because — ” and there it was, the crack, thin as a hair, the first uncontrolled thing the Archwarden had said “ — because killed was no longer a condition they answered to. In the end we brought in the battle-mages and burned the theater with everything in it. Everything that had been fifteen of the finest minds in Caltheran, we gave to the fire. I gave the order for that as well.”

*The hive.*

The word arrived in Elaine before she could bar the door on it. Melody’s intelligence, delivered in her flat unsentimental hand a year ago; the things the Order’s creatures left behind them; bodies that moved past their own deaths because the will inside them had never lived in any single one. The Council’s survivors had watched fifteen men and women stop being fifteen of anything. There was a name for what wore people like that. The Twelve did not have it.

She did.

She held it, and the holding was becoming the whole work of standing in this room.

Frederick shifted his weight — the small honest movement of a man giving himself something to do. She read him without meaning to: no horror on the surface, all of it packed down deep where he stored the things he would drink about later, and over the top of it, running fast, the arithmetic. Fifteen dead scholars. A burned theater. A Council that pays in silver seals and travels twelve strong to a city with no record-room. He was counting what was there. The sum was already ugly.

“There is one more thing,” Tauvor said, “and then the work.”

He drew a paper from inside his robe — the only paper in the room, and Elaine did not miss that they had built a chamber scrubbed of ink and then carried this one leaf into it. He set it down and turned it to face them, and did not take his fingers off it.

“One of the fifteen was the last to burn. A senior scholar of the old records. His body was — no longer his, by every account, and the fire was already through him. And he spoke.” Tauvor’s finger tapped the page once, softly, like a man touching a sleeping animal. “Not in his voice. Not in his tongue. Not in any tongue lettered in the Archive, and the Archive has them all. Two of the witnessing mages had the presence of mind to write the sounds as they heard them. This is what they wrote.”

The letters were ordinary Caltheran script, and they spelled nothing. Elaine read them twice and felt her mouth refuse the shapes; Frederick’s lips moved a fraction and stopped, the way a man abandons a ford halfway. Ink, holding sounds no throat in the room could carry. On the far side of the fire, someone had said this. Through a burning man. To an audience of the dying.

“We do not know what it means,” Tauvor said. “I will tell you what I believe, and you may weigh whether the belief becomes me. Fifteen deaths are a wound. Wounds close. But a voice is not a wound. A voice is an address.” His eyes came up from the paper, and the fear beneath the floorboards rose until it stood just behind the brown of them, disciplined, chained, and entirely real. “Something heard what we did in that theater, commanders. Something answered. It is the answering that keeps me from sleep.”

The silence in the chamber belonged to no one at all.

“What did it say?” Brunor asked. His voice came out rough and even. “Your scholars. The Archive. Somebody has a guess.”

“Nobody has a guess worth the word.” It was the Warden at Tauvor’s left who spoke — the bald one, Onlwald, stern as a door. “The tongue is in no lexicon, no rubbing, no trade record. The oldest holdings we possess are First Era — copies of copies, from before the Merge scattered half of what men knew. We have scholars in those stacks now who have not seen daylight since the burning. The nearest thing they have found is not a translation. It is a direction.”

“The sounds are kin to fragments out of the far south of the old world,” Tauvor said. “Records that predate every kingdom in this room. And every fragment we hold of that tongue was set down in one place.” He let it stand a moment. “The deep forests of Vel’Era.”

The name went through Elaine like a dropped stitch.

*Vel’Era.* Silver trees she had been too young to keep, a burning house, a road north in a carriage that smelled of smoke and her sister’s hair. Her blood’s country, that she had not stood in since the purge took every Morrandis but two. She kept her face empty. She was no longer certain it was working. Across the arc, the frightened Warden had stopped pretending boredom.

“And so, the work.” Tauvor drew the paper back and it vanished into his robe, and with it the only record in the room. When he spoke again the confessor was gone and the Archwarden was back, measured and purposeful, a man closing a door behind him. “The Council is assembling an expedition. Scholars of the First Era holdings, two runescribes, warders for their instruments. They go south to trace the tongue to its ground — to find what the old records circled and never named. Vel’Era’s deep forest is no gentle country, and the roads to it are worse, and the Council’s own soldiers would arrive wearing Caltheran’s flag, which in the current weather is a provocation we cannot spend. Johanna’s Promise carries no flag. Your company is neutral, it is competent, and it has carried for us before.” The brown eyes were unreadable, and beneath the floorboards nothing moved at all. “Escort work. Protection of persons and cargo, there and back. Name your rate, Commander Rowain, and the Spire will better it. You will find us generous to the point of indiscretion.”

*It has carried for us before.* The sentence sat where he had placed it, mild as an afterthought, and Elaine turned it over and felt the hook inside. The crate on the Caltheran road. The sealed contract, no questions in writing. They had brought the Wyrshade to the Archive’s door, and now the same hands were being hired to walk the road that led out of everything the Wyrshade had broken open.

“How many researchers,” Frederick said. Not a question. Already counting.

“Nine, and their gear. Two wagons.”

“And what they’re looking for. If we’re standing over your scholars in the worst forest on the continent, I’d like a better heading than *old sounds*.”

“You have the heading the Archive has.” Tauvor said it without weight, and the mark said it was a lie, and said it quietly — not the floorboard fear but something laid flatter and colder, the smooth deliberate grain of a man withholding on purpose. “Answers, Commander. The origin of the tongue. What the old records would not name. If there is more to know, it is in that forest, not in this room.”

*There it is,* she thought. The room had no ink because the room was the lie. Enough horror to make the stakes real. Enough withheld that they would walk into the dark carrying the Council’s lantern and never see the shape of what it lit.

Brunor spoke before Frederick could count his way to the next question.

“You’ll have our answer when we’ve decided it.”

For a moment the two of them looked at each other down the length of the bare table, the dying man and the bruised one, and something passed between them that the mark could not name because it belonged to commanders and not to feelings — the mutual recognition of weight.

“Of course,” Tauvor said. “You came within the week. Decide within another.” He rose, and the Twelve rose with him in one motion, matched robes swinging like a curtain drawn on the whole proceeding. “The Council thanks Johanna’s Promise. This room will not remember what was said in it. I ask that you be equally kind.”

The doors were opened from without. The lane of waiting petitioners took them back the way water takes a stone.

They came out of the College into the last of the Goldroad’s light with the crowd’s wanting washing up against Elaine like tidewater, and it was almost a relief — a thousand small hungers, honest and human and none of them wearing robes. By the eastern windows, the place where Unara Lackmin had stood was empty.

The three of them walked without speaking until the guild’s silver-veined wall was well behind them. Frederick broke first, as he always did.

“Well. It pays.”

“It’s wrong,” Brunor said.

“I said it pays. I never said it added up.” Frederick pulled his collar against the first spit of returning rain and looked at the two of them — Elaine on one side of him, Brunor on the other, and himself where he had spent thirty years, in the middle. “Fifteen of their own dead, no record, no name for what did it, and a paymaster who confesses like a man buying absolution by the seal. There are numbers missing from that ledger, and I am not fond of guessing at sums. What do we know that they don’t?”

*Everything that matters,* Elaine did not say. *The name of the thing that answered.*

The word had sat behind her teeth through the confession and the burning and the paper full of impossible sounds, and it sat there still, patient as its owner, all the way down the Goldroad. It was still there when the rain began in earnest — and past the rooftops to the south, too far to feel her looking, the empty place at the gate where a grey hood had been.

She would have to decide who to say it to.

She was no longer certain the answer was anyone.
