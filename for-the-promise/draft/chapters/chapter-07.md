## Chapter 7: Galeran

[14th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Hollow | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger) | Whispernight]
[Dusk into Night | Dovelea Market, Eastern Halreth]

*You can burn the brand off the leather. The arm remembers carrying it.*

By his fourth week in Dovelea Market, Galeran had taught himself to walk past a posted watch without reading their spacing, and he was almost proud of that, and then a wagon backed out of the tanner's yard and he had counted the crowd, the exits, and the two men whose boots were too good for their coats before his heart had finished its next beat.

Two years a deserter. Four weeks a dockhand. Still a soldier every time something moved wrong.

He hated it the way a man hates the only thing keeping him alive.

"Garret." The foreman's voice carried down the millstream walk. "You deaf, lad? I said last load's done. Take your coin and get gone — it's Whispernight. Nobody works the water tonight but the dead."

*Garret.* Four weeks in, he answered to it cleanly now — a name close enough to his own that his head turned without theater. That was the first thing the Promise had taught him about hiding, back when it was teaching him to find people who hid: keep your lie within arm's reach of the truth. He'd built himself out of arm's-reach lies. Garret, a quarry-man's son from western Halreth. Grey hair from a fever, if anyone asked. Nobody asked. Dovelea was a treaty town, amnesty-old, and its whole economy ran on not asking.

He took the coin — three carp, dock rate, half what a guild man made — and didn't sign the tally sheet, and the foreman didn't push it. The Merchant's Guild had papered him out of every licensed yard from Theralion to the border in the spring. No name on the writ. Just a description that fit him the way a noose fits a neck: *grey-haired man, young, soldier-carried, travels with an Iskari woman.* Whoever had bought that writ hadn't wanted him jailed.

Jails had records. Records could be found. Whoever it was wanted him *placeable.*

The market square had turned itself over to the festival by the time he crossed it. Stalls shuttered, lanterns going up in their hundreds — paper-shaded candles in white and grey, strung from eave to eave until the town sat inside a slow constellation. Families moved toward the millstream with cupped candles and folded slips of paper. On Whispernight you wrote your confession, or whispered it, and gave it to moving water, and the water carried it to whoever kept accounts on such things. The Stranger, the priests said, this being his month. Galeran had watched the ritual every year of his childhood and had never once seen anyone look lighter walking away from the stream.

Full night now. Both moons were up over the rooflines, and the broken one was brighter, the way it always was and the way nothing about it had ever been explained to him. Its pieces hung apart from the body like a shattered plate caught mid-fall, and it threw its light down in overlapping slants, so that a man standing under the eaves wore three faint shadows at once. The whole moon rose behind it, dimmer, decorous, keeping its distance. As a boy he'd asked a priest why the dead moon outshone the living one. The priest had cuffed his ear for the word *dead.* Naeryn, the one time he'd asked her, had answered like it was nothing: *because that is what glory looks like after the body fails. Your people just don't like what that implies.*

He found her on the wharf steps below the confession bridge, away from the lantern lines, working by moonslight.

She had a knife, a length of waxed cord, and three small pieces in her lap: a knuckle of copper, a button of dark wood, a splinter of riverbone she'd been shaping for a week. Her braids were down. He'd never seen her braids down in daylight — the charms woven through them clicked when she turned her head, a soft accounting of everyone she'd ever honored, and tonight she was adding to the count.

Rusk. Thatch. Thom.

Galeran stopped where the steps began, because his body wouldn't take him closer and he'd stopped arguing with it. Thatch and Thom had been twins who'd bickered over watch rotations. Rusk had once carried him nine miles with a bolt in his own shoulder. They'd followed Galeran because he'd been trained by the best mercenary company on the continent and it showed — that was the cruelty of it — it *showed*, right up until the plan that got them taken by a woman with white hair and a Naaren commission, and then nothing he'd been trained in had mattered at all.

*My reckless planning.* He made himself say it that way, in his own head, every time. Lyra Ounvian had given the orders. He had built the door she walked through.

"You are standing where a guard stands," Naeryn said, not looking up. "Post or corner. Pick one, or sit."

He sat. The stone still held the day's warmth. Below them the millstream ran thick with candlelight and little white slips, a whole town's worth of secrets riding the current toward the weir.

Naeryn tied off the copper knuckle into a braid at her temple and spoke a word over it in her tribe's tongue, quiet, the way she said all names — as if the owner were seated close and might be embarrassed. She took up the wooden button next. Her hands were steady. Her hands were always steady. It was the rest of her that had gone somewhere he couldn't follow since the Shadowfen, and it was getting worse, and neither of them had said so aloud.

"You could do yours," she said. "Tonight is built for it. Your people even have the plumbing." She tipped her chin at the stream, at the paper fleet of other men's guilt.

"I don't confess to rivers."

"No." She pulled the knot tight. "You keep everything in the arm."

His hand had gone to his forearm without his leave — to the flat lump under the sleeve where the old insignia rode against the skin, leather, palm-sized, the Promise's silver flame burned off it two years ago with the tip of a heated knife. He'd told himself he kept it as tradecraft: never carry the crest, never lose the habit of what the crest had taught him. It was a lie of the arm's-reach kind. He carried it the way Dovelea carried its lanterns. A light kept burning for accounts that hadn't settled.

He took his hand off his sleeve. "How would it even go, Naeryn? What do I write? *I planned the Vellwater job with six weeks of watching and it was still a trap. I walked three people I fed for two years into a Runescribe agent's teeth. I lived because a woman I distrust decided I was worth more moving than dead.*" His voice stayed level through all of it. He'd practiced. "The stream would sink."

"That is not a confession," Naeryn said. "That is a report. You have been filing reports on yourself for two years and calling it grief." She lifted the riverbone splinter, checked its edge against her thumb, and began to bore the cord-hole, unhurried. "A confession would be: *I want to go back.* You have never once said it. It comes off you anyway. Every time we pass anything with a banner."

The candles went down the water. Somewhere up on the bridge a child was crying because her paper had sunk too soon, and her mother was telling her that sinking early meant the Stranger was eager to listen, which was the kind of lie mothers had been telling on Whispernight for a hundred years.

"They hanged the last man who went back," Galeran said. "Ephram. I was there. Brunor Halvyr read the sentence himself and his voice never moved. That's the company you think I want to crawl home to."

"I did not say want it like a warm bed." She strung the splinter and did not look at him. "Want it like a wall wants to hold a roof, maybe. You were built into a structure. The structure lost you. Neither of you has stopped aching from it." Then, in exactly the same tone: "There were men at the amnesty stone this afternoon asking the treaty-clerk about grey hair."

The night kept its shape. That was the discipline — nothing on his face, nothing in his shoulders, a man taking evening air while a friend strung her mourning. Inside the discipline, everything went to drill: distances, doors, the road east, the road west, the water.

"How many."

"Four that showed themselves. They wore riverman's oilskins that have never touched fish." A click of her charms as she tilted her head. "And they queue wrong. They kept trying not to stand like a file and remembering to slouch one at a time."

"Naaren."

"Their boots are Naaren-stitched. Their coin is Halrethi and new." She finally looked at him, and in the broken moon's tripled light her bronze eyes had that faint gold shimmer that strangers took for a trick of lanterns and he had learned to take for weather warning. "And there is a fifth who did not come to the stone. He stood by the horse-line at the Serenehold road and watched the square for one hour and spoke to no one. Big through the chest. He stands like a door."

Galeran had never seen Commander Anverus closer than two hundred paces, across a marsh, through rain — a shape directing other shapes while Lyra Ounvian's voice came pleasant and unhurried out of the dark about what would be done with the twins if Galeran chose to be brave. But a body learned the people who had cost it. His own body had learned that one to the bone.

"He found the market fast," Galeran said. "Faster than the writ should let him. The guild papered the licensed yards — the writ's a net for a working man. I haven't worked licensed. Somebody sold him the town anyway."

"The clerk," Naeryn said. "Or the foreman. Or the widow who rents us the loft and lights a candle for her son in Naaren coin." She shrugged, one shoulder, an Iskari economy of contempt. "It does not matter which. A town that runs on not asking will also not ask *him.*"

He looked at the stream. Three carp in his pocket. A guild writ shaped like a noose. Four false rivermen and a door-shaped man on the Serenehold road, which meant the west was watched, which meant the choice was already made and had been made for days and he was only now reading the orders.

East, then. East was the plains and the passes, east was where the continent thinned into the places nobody papered — and east was the way *she* kept looking. That was the other thing that had not been said aloud. Since the Shadowfen, Naeryn slept facing east the way a compass needle faces north, and twice now he had woken to find her sitting up with her finger moving against the floorboards, drawing the same looping mark over and over, asleep, her face turned toward the sea he had never crossed.

She saw where his eyes had gone. She always did.

"You are about to ask me," she said, "and it is Whispernight, so I will give you this much without being asked." She touched the newest charm, the riverbone, Thom's. "The mark I made at the Shadowfen — the one that kept the hunters' eyes sliding off us in the marsh. I did not invent it. I did not learn it from any scribe." The water ran below them, freighted with candles. "The rune taught me. The way a mother teaches — it put my hand through the shapes. And tonight, with every current in the world running slow for the festival, everything is quiet except the place it touched. That is not quiet at all."

"What is it doing?" he asked.

Naeryn considered the question with her hands folded over her dead.

"Pulling," she said. "East. And Galeran — it is not calling me *toward* something. I have felt a call. Calls ask." Her thumb moved once across the burned-smooth face of the bone. "This one assumes."

Below them, the last of the confession candles rode over the weir and went out, all together, the way lights go out in a keep when the watch changes. Galeran sat in the tripled shadow of the broken moon with his three carp and his burned brand, hunted by a door-shaped man for knowing what stood at the Shadowfen, beside a woman being reeled east by something that did not ask — and did what he had been trained to do by better people than he'd deserved, in a company he could not stop belonging to no matter what he burned.

He started planning.

"We're gone before the lanterns come down," he said. "Not the east gate — the fifth man watches the west because he wants us out the east. We go north along the millstream and pick up the plains road past the weir." He was up, offering his hand to pull her from the steps, already walking the route in his head — north, then east on their own terms, toward the thin places, toward the sea, toward whatever assumed it owned the other end of the line in her. "And when we're clear of this town, you're going to tell me everything the rift put in you. All of it. If something is reeling us in, I want to walk the ground before it closes."

Naeryn took his hand and came up light as smoke. For the first time since the marsh, the look she gave him was the old one — the one from before, the one that had made a band out of strays.

"There he is," she said. "The squad leader."

They went north out of the lantern light, two shadows and then six under the broken moon, and the millstream carried the town's confessions past them in the dark — all those paper sins bound for the weir — and Galeran Quller, who confessed nothing, kept his to the arm that remembered, and walked east to earn them.
