## Chapter 4: Brunor
[8th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Flame | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger)]
[Evening | Bordou Square, Glintgate, Caltheran]
The downpour soaked through to Brunor’s spine, and he was glad of it. Cold kept a man true. 
He and Damien both took five paces apart and turned.
The golden blade hung loose at Damien’s side, point low, and he came on slow, shoulders dropped, weight back, the reach of him tucked away where it wouldn’t show. Damien was tall and armored, and he moved like neither. He gave the space nothing — no weight shifted that he didn’t choose.
The same trick had raised Brunor. Uthred could stand in a yard looking like a tired old farmer right up until the blade moved, and then there was nothing tired about him at all.
Read him before you reach for him—his father’s voice, surfacing suddenly. 
A man’s first three steps tell you the rest.
Damien's first step said very little—a placement step and angled outward. 
The chancellor led with the front foot and let the weight follow tardily. Caltherian court fencing, all tempo and invitation. He wanted Brunor to commit to the opening that wasn’t one. The golden blade dipped a finger’s width — there, take it, you won’t — and Brunor didn’t.
He stepped off instead. Nullbrand came up.
The chancellor’s brow arched.
“You don’t take the bait,” Damien said. He sounded pleased, as though they stood at a banquet. “Little cub. I’d hoped you’d be reckless.”
Brunor said nothing. Talking was Elaine’s weapon, and he had said everything he needed to say. 
Damien came.
 A lunging thrust, straight for his midguard. Brunor parried and rode the momentum off the golden blade into the riposte, a high sweep. Nullbrand caught Damien’s shoulder guard and tore the cloak from it. The metal beneath glinted, grey light on wet steel.
A boot came up and took Brunor in the gut. He gave ground, kept his feet, and Damien was already on him — fast, clean, every cut aimed not to open him but to turn him, to walk him off his balance one strike at a time. Court fencing still, even now. The man fought as if he were correcting a pupil. Damien wasn’t trying to kill him yet. He was trying to make it look easy.
The strikes came until Brunor’s forearms sang with them, and then the rhythm broke. Damien flipped his grip and drove in low. Nullbrand came down to meet it and slid off the golden blade. The black sword rang against the stones.
Damien’s boot came down on the flat of it.
One breath. The golden blade was already swinging for his head.
Brunor dropped under it. The blade sailed where his head had been, close enough to feel the wind of it, and he was already falling, twisting onto his side to scythe a leg at Damien’s ankles. Damien skipped the kick. Brunor’s hand closed on Nullbrand’s grip before the boot came back down.
He came up off the stones and advanced into an overhead cleave.
Damien’s blade met it high, steering it wide; the golden edge guided Nullbrand past so the weight carried Brunor a half-step back. The chancellor moved into the opening. A thrust shot out from his shoulder, fully extending his reach.
Brunor saw it the instant the foot moved.
The step came straight in and straight back, and Damien’s blade kept trying to recover to center, aiming for a distance a sword rarely needs. Damien fought as if he’d learned the spear first and never set it down. He kept reaching for a range that wasn’t there, resetting to a measure the golden blade didn’t need.
Brunor stopped giving him that.
He pressed forward, narrowing the line where a spearman’s footwork came apart. Damien saw it and did not care. He answered with a flourish, rolling his wrist to swing the golden blade in a low, elegant arc. It drew a thin line of red across Brunor’s forearm, and the chancellor stepped back so he could see it.
First blood. Damien wanted it admired.
“Ah. First blood. As expected.” He said it to the crowd, not to him.
Brunor glanced at Elaine. She stood near a knot of soaked choristers, white-cloaked and wide-eyed, none of them old enough to have seen blood drawn in earnest. Pink ran off his forearm with the rain and pooled between the stones.
He let his stance go and began to pace. Damien matched him, and the two circled the wet ground.
“You should never have come back to my city. The Promise never learns from its mistakes. Trifling fools, all of you.”
Damien stopped near the fountain and looked at the choristers and Elaine. The head singer bowed. The others followed. Elaine did not. Damien held on her longer than Brunor liked before turning back.
“Your father would be ashamed of what has been displayed here today.” He circled, light on his feet. Then he raised his voice for the crowd. “The great Uthred Halvyr’s son! Is he not delightful entertainment? A sellsword bleeding in a square that is not his. The Halvyr legacy, and he bleeds it out on my flagstones.”
Brunor paced. The golden blade, the feet. Nothing else.

Damien waited for a wound to show. 
 “The bastard you keep at your side.” He nodded toward Elaine without looking at her. “She must repay your kindness often. She was thrown away, and you retrieved her and called it loyalty. Sentiment — the very disease that killed your mother’s childish dream. The very thing that opened it up to the Order.” He paused, as if the connection had only just occurred to him. “Tell me, Halvyr. Do you collect broken things because you believe you can mend them, or because they make you feel whole by comparison?”

Brunor said nothing. His pace did not change.
“Stubborn. Like that old feral bastard after all.” He turned and paced the other way, searching now. “The traitorous whore you call captain. Alessa?”
The pace stuttered. Half a step. Damien saw it.
“Ah,” he said, soft. “Yes. Alessa. The one the bandits took on the Glintgate road. Held down in the mud, I heard. Made to watch her men get opened up, one by one, while she could do nothing but lie there and watch their lives spill into the earth.” He let the rain fill his breath. “And bandits being bandits — well. A woman alone, at their mercy, for hours. You know what men like that do to a woman before they are finished with her. She—”
Brunor charged. The yell came out of him before the thought did.
“Finally!” Damien was smiling when their blades met and locked.

Brunor drove into the bind with everything behind it. Damien gave ground — one step, two, his boots sliding on the wet stone — and on the fifth, he stopped them short, let Brunor’s weight pitch in over their crossed guards, and snapped his head forward into the gap.
Damien’s forehead caught the bridge of Brunor’s nose, and a fist came behind it.
Brunor’s head snapped sideways. His bottom lip split like mashed cherries. He stumbled back, the square tilting white at the edges, and swung wild for Damien’s gut — and felt nothing connect.
The next fist found his jaw. Brunor spun with it, came around in the rain, and caught the following jab on his guard, answering with one of his own across Damien’s cheek. The chancellor rode the blow, let it carry him a stride, and turned the momentum into a pommel strike for Brunor’s temple.
Brunor blocked. Came around in the pirouette — the one he had stolen from Elaine years ago, who had stolen it from Tella before that — and swept Nullbrand into the side of Damien’s gambeson.
The blade bit and slid off. Reinforced. Beneath the cloth, the black sword hummed once against something it didn’t like — there, then gone.
Enchantment.
Brunor thrust for the belly. The strike came fast enough that Damien arched away from it and gave ground from the follow-up, and for the first time, the elegance was gone — he moved because he had to.
Damien caught his footing and spat red into the rain.
He looked at Brunor the way Brunor had been looking at him the whole fight.
“This has gone on long enough.”
The golden blade came up, and this time there was no invitation in it.
“The next exchange decides it. There will be no glory in this.”
Brunor angled his stance and set himself. Neither of them had anything left to say.
They stepped off into it together.
 Nullbrand swung down to meet Damien’s rising plunge. Brunor put everything his soul would allow behind it, and his body was eager to answer the order.
Then black. Wet raven feathers across his sight.
Nullbrand hummed again in his grip.
Then he felt his own blade turn.
Steel met steel, and his downswing slid off, guided wide by a sword that had come from nowhere, and through the rain, he saw the dagger in her other hand catch Damien’s plunge at the same instant, the golden point skidding off the parry a finger’s width from her shoulder.
Elaine stood between them. One blade holding his, one holding her cousin’s.
Then her arms locked and she turned, dragging both blades with her, and the bind wrenched at Brunor’s wrist — turn with her or be turned. He turned.
She let the rotation throw them. Both men staggered apart, off-balance, blades fouled in the rain.
“Enough.” She stood in the space between them, blades out, one aimed at each. “You are not ending this. I am.”
Brunor caught himself before he fell. The point of her sword was at his chest.
“What in the hells are you doing!” He could hear his own voice breaking loose from whatever sense remained. “Why would you—”
“What? Save your life?” She cut across him. “Those were killing blows. If I had not stepped in—”
“Silence,” Damien called over her. “You interrupted a duel between two consenting parties. You have committed a vile sin.”

Elaine stayed fixed on Brunor. “Have you forgotten yourself? Is this how a commander of Johanna’s Promise presents himself to the people?”
She turned.
“And you—”
Nothing came. Brunor could count on one hand the number of times Elaine had run out of words. The sentence died in her mouth. Damien smiled at her, odd and cold.
Brunor moved to shove her aside. She caught his wrist.
“Don’t.” She leaned in. “Trust me. There’s someone in the crowd. By the gates. The woman in the grey hood.”
His eyes went to the gates. A woman in grey, face lost beneath the hood. He could not name her. The crowd around her flinched from the blood and the rain, and she did not, and Brunor’s hand tightened on Nullbrand’s grip before he understood why. She tilted her head. Then she was gone.

Brunor’s teeth ground until his jaw ached. He held Nullbrand a moment longer than he should have, then sheathed it. Blood ran down his arm and off his fingertips, and he watched it darken the stones one drop at a time.

“Must you ruin everything, Elaine?” Damien handed the torn cloak to a guard, who settled the new one over his shoulders. He touched his cheek where Brunor had struck him. A bruise was already forming. “You understand I could have you thrown in gaol for this. A contest of arms, broken by a third blade, before half the city. You are truly mad.”
Damien fixed on her and held.
Brunor watched Elaine hold the look and then lose it. Her shoulders rose and did not steady. The colour went from her face, and Brunor could not say what had taken it. Her cousin had said nothing he could hear.
Then Damien pulled his cloak up and turned to his guards.
“Golden Scales guildhall. By week’s end.” He did not look back. “Do try not to fuck up twice.”

***
They cut through the first alley south of the square without speaking. Rain ran off the eaves and pooled between the cobblestones, and Brunor walked through it because walking around it would have meant slowing down, and slowing down would have meant looking at her. His forearm throbbed where Damien had opened it. The blood had thinned in the rain and was running freely again, dripping off his knuckles in pale red threads. He pressed his other hand over the cut and kept moving. Elaine matched his pace, a half step behind, close enough that he could hear the wet feathers of her cloak dragging against the stone walls. Neither of them had said a word since Damien turned his back. The alley narrowed. Brunor stopped at the junction where it met Candler’s Row and turned to face her.
“The woman at the gate. You said I’d recognize her. I didn’t. Care to explain why she was important enough to break three different laws?”
Elaine looked back down the row. Empty. She turned to him, and he recognised the look — the one she wore before she opened someone up with words.

“Have you lost your mind, or did you leave it on Damien’s flagstones with your shallow pride?”
Brunor smiled coldly.
“I had him beaten. I was finding my footing. Another second and—”
“You had nothing! A coin flip in the air! And you wagered your life on it landing right, didn't you? Both of you, dead on those stones — I watched you decide it was worth it, Brunor! Was it?”

He did not answer.
“Answer me…” she said.
“There are things more important than my life that are worth protecting. If I allow a man like Damien to keep doing as he pleases, then everything we worked for, all the sacrifices, will have all been for nothing. Was he worth it, you ask? Yes, Elaine. He was.”
“No! Worth your life!? Worth the company’s?” She got in front of him and pushed his shoulder hard. Brunor stepped back from it. “You drew steel in his city! Accepted his contest! Bled in his square for the whole of Glintgate to gawp at? Choristers were watching, Brunor! Why? All so you could feel like a man again?”
He looked at the blood running off his fingers, not at her. His smile lessened, but it didn’t leave.
“There it is,” he said. “You don’t think I’d have won.”
“I think I wasn’t going to find out.” Her voice came down, but the anger did not leave it. “Gods, you accursed fool. I am not putting you in the ground in this fucking city. Not for him. Not for anyone.”

Elaine paced from one wall to the next. She stopped to kick a rat that tried to scurry across her boot. The thud of a rat meeting a wall echoed beneath the growing downpour. It picked itself up and limped away. 
“I hate this city. From the cat-sized rodents to these damned white stones that blind you if you stay outside too long. Beggars on every corner. Rape, murder, and theft are happening every hour right under the guard’s noses. And the moment I decide to accompany you and set foot here, the commander who leads a fourth of my company decides to ruin us before the true battle even begins.”
Brunor said nothing. 
“If I had a mage worth their coin and an hour of thought, I would level every block from the Silver Gate to the river and let the Aldren swallow what was left. Turn the cobblestones to mud and drown the rats underground with the rest of it. Do you know they charged us six carps for stabling two horses? Six! Munch isn’t even difficult!”
She pivoted before he could answer. The rant moved from Glintgate to the council, to why Brunor would be the death of her, then briefly to what she intended to do to the man named Hans who bought the necklace she was considering going back for, and how it would have matched her sword. Brunor leaned against the wall and let her go. He had learned years ago that Elaine’s anger ran until it was finished, and nothing he said would reduce it.

“And Alessa. When exactly were you planning to tell me that one of our captains was ambushed on a road I am responsible for? Anything that happens on that road—” She stopped. Took a deep breath and spoke with a lowered voice. “I am supposed to know. Why didn’t my scouts inform me? Ferrena will catch the fury of the hells over this.”
Brunor’s hand tightened over the cut on his forearm. He was the reason her scouts had not informed her, and he could not say that now.
“Did Frederick know?”
“Yes, he knows. And do you even have to ask, Elaine? Out of everyone this past year, you and the Hollow have had the most to worry about. Assassins, spies, and the rest. I was reluctant to add more to what was already suffocating you.”
“*You* are suffocating me, Brunor…”
Brunor reached inside one of his pouches and pulled out a small cloth. He tied it over his cut forearm and tightened it until he felt his pulse through it. Then he took a long look at Elaine, who was watching him. Rain ran down her face and off the feathers of her cloak, and she did not wipe it or blink through it.
“Men died on my roads. Some of those were my scouts. Men, I assigned. Routes I approved. And you allow me to find out like this.” Her voice had dropped lower and forfeited its base. “How long? How long has she been recovering?”
“Two months.”
“Two months.” She repeated it as if she were tasting it. “And in that time, you and Frederick decided I didn’t need to know. The same way you decided about the investigation last year. The same way you decided about the Hollow’s confinement. Every time I come back, there’s a problem created, and nobody thinks to tell me before it spirals out of control.”
“That is not what this is.”
“Then what is it? Because in my mind, you have spent the past year deciding what I can carry and what I can’t, and I did not ask you to do that. I have never asked you to do that. Your priorities are severely misconstrued, Halvyr.”
Brunor pushed off the wall. The rain was heavier now, hammering the stones between them. His ears grew hot.
“You want to know why I didn’t tell you? Because when I got the report, you were three weeks into a scouting mission at the Vigil while surrounded by the Order. The Kymancy mark. Phoebe. The Hollow. Tella. Hells, that damned rift hovering above it all! I made a judgment call! Frederick agreed and even pushed for it because even he knew you were reaching a limit! We handled it! We promised to help you, Elaine. We are trying to relieve some of that burden!”
“You handled it, sure. Then why in the hells is the issue still being fought over? Where is the justice in it all when the men who did it walk free because you decided for me?”
“Justice.” Brunor shook his head. “You ask that? Really? How can justice be served when the men who did it were rewarded and are now protected by your cousin? You know he was responsible.”
Elaine bit her lip. “Don’t simplify things. There is a way we do things. If it were Damien, then there would be no way to prove it. He covers himself too well.”
She paused to look back down the alley. A beggar was pleading with a woman to let him inside before the cold took his feet.
“Merea? Does she know?” Elaine asked.
Brunor turned to look with her. The woman shoved the man back onto the stones and shut the door. He spat once and walked into the downpour.

“Merea is a garrison captain and the keep’s first method of security through the watchtower. She was informed during the security appraisal. That’s code.”
“Oh, be silent. Code.” Elaine’s mouth moved around the word like it was rotten. “I don’t want to hear you mention anything about the fucking code when you purposely broke it yourself today.” Elaine looked back at him. “You informed your garrison captain, but not your scout commander. The commander whose roads they died on. Because of the code.”
“Don’t do that, El. You know what I did wasn’t malicious.”
“Don’t do what?”
“Make this into something it isn’t.”
“I’m not making it into anything. You already made it. I’m just the last to find out. As usual, it will be up to me to clean up the mess you created, just like the spy investigation.”
Brunor’s shoulders dropped. 
*Another who questions my choices, and now it’s Elaine.*
The inn at Stonefield tried to surface. It always did when someone stood in front of him and told him his choices had taken too much— the boy’s face, the promise Brunor made to himself that their deaths would mean something, and the quiet certainty, growing louder with each year, that it never had. He fought the name back down before it reached his mouth.

They were at the crossroads now. The rain had soaked through everything. Brunor could feel his shirt sticking to the cut and the cold settling into the bruise across his nose where Damien’s forehead had caught him. Elaine stood four paces away, arms tight against her sides, and the look on her face was one he had seen rarely; despair covered by that emotionless mask she despised wearing. The last he saw it was the night with her and Frederick inside her quarters at Caer Seryth, when he told her about Merea, and she smiled at him and said congratulations, her face looking like a doll learning emotion for the first time.
Elaine opened her mouth.
“I think we should—”
“There you are.” The voice came from behind the fountain. “I’ve been standing in this cold rain for an hour like a complete idiot. The guards kept asking me for—Wait, what in the gods’ names happened to your face, man?”
