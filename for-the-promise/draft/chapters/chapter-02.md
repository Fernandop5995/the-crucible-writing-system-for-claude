## Chapter 2: Elaine
[8th of Ashenfall, 982 A.M.]
[Day of Flame | Late Summer — Death (The Stranger)]
[Afternoon into Evening | Glintgate, Merchant Quarter]
*Fate has suffocated me. It's laid a path no mortal should ever tread. Will I ever be free to choose again?*
“And this? Are these also real amenicite?” Elaine pointed a gloved finger at a pair of daggers. The grips were wrapped in goatskin. The blades had a dark red hue and reflected nothing—Amenicite from the Sol’telarī region of Varkaan.
The peddler, a heavy woman wrapped in exotic silks of blue, red, and black, save for her warted nose and bloodshot brown eyes, spread a hand over the daggers. Under the haggler’s patience was something sourer — the woman had been cheated recently and hadn’t forgiven it. “Of course, pōndera,” she rasped from behind the silks. “Do you believe me foolish? The stories alone of finding a spot and being approved would convince you to buy. I sell no crude wares here, mercenary.”
The blades were real. However, the price was a different argument. Beside her, Brunor was losing the battle with his own patience — it came off him in a low hum, which meant he’d start sighing through his nose soon. A different gift, then, one he could use daily.
“Thank you,” Elaine said, and steered them both off into the crowd.
Glintgate’s merchant quarter could be heard before one turned the corner from the city entrance. Hawkers, hammers, the scrape of cart wheels on cobblestone. Then the overpowering smell: tanned leather, hot wax, fried bread, and underneath it all, the salty mineral tang of the Aldren running close.
She fell into old habits and began counting. Three exits off the main road, the alley on the left too narrow for a drawn blade, more eyes on Brunor’s sword than on her face. Market stalls under brightly dyed cloths, crimson and emerald and saffron, lined both sides, each vying for the same coin. Pale weathered limestone, chipped and smoothed by centuries, and the magical skybridges arching overhead like translucent spiderwebs. Shadows of foot traffic crossed the bridges and slid down the rough stone, and she kept catching herself watching them instead of the road. The mark didn’t care what she watched. It took the quarter whole and handed it back to her in slices — the hawkers’ practiced cheer lay thin over something wound too tight underneath, the same unease she’d felt off every city since the war talk started last winter.
A whole street pretending the entire city didn't reek of the smoke from the blaze burning beneath the stones.
The cobblestone shone beneath the grime, a warm shimmering gold where sunlight broke through the canopies.
“Goldroad,” Brunor said. “Now I see why they call it that.”
“You’ve been to Glintgate. It's a tad hard to believe you’ve never made it this far in.”
“No. I usually stick to the western district. It’s where most of the Promise connections are.”
“That’s the entertainment ward, if I’m not mistaken. I saw a city map once. Who are these connections you're so eager to keep in touch with?”
Brunor's mouth shifted slightly to the right. She knew that one, even with Nullbrand muting her mark, was discomfort.
She stifled the urge to smile in satisfaction at the rare sight.
“Well, it's important to keep an open mind when it comes to insider affiliations, and to respect the fact that many men and women are risking their lives for us. Those same men and women who—”
“Those same whores you mean...” Elaine interrupted. “No?”
“What?” he responded.
“They are whores, aren't they?” she asked.
He bit his lip. “They corrected me once and said they prefer to be called performers or entertainers of the human arts.”
Elaine sighed deeply. “I figured.  You sound like you’re trying to convince yourself, not me.”
Brunor nodded slowly. “I know. The things we have to do now…It's just… sometimes I forget how much things have changed.
Elaine twisted a loose strand of hair that had come free from her braid. “You mean how much I have changed,” she clarified softly.
Brunor studied her face briefly before nodding in agreement. 
Promise couriers had taken work from Glintgate before — the city paid well and asked fewer questions than most — but Elaine had never walked this side of it herself. Brunor kept to the western ward on his visits. She understood why the moment she turned the corner: a scout could lose an hour here without meaning to. The stalls pressed close on both sides, and the crowd was thick enough that her hand found her hilt before she thought about it. She made herself let go. An armorer’s forge threw heat across the road. Past it, a leatherworker stitching something too fine for soldiers. The rest of the quarter blurred into noise and colour she did not need yet. A journeyman blacksmith spotted her sword and came at a half-run, holding out a knife with the starved look of a man who had been doing it all morning.
She took it. Turned it over. The blade was basic work, a little crooked at the spine, but the handle had been wrapped with care, and someone had etched a small maker’s mark into the tang.
“Not today,” she told him. He took the knife back and was already scanning the crowd for the next prospect before she had finished the sentence.
Hawkers worked the road from both sides, voices stacked over one another until the words lost meaning. Elaine stopped hearing them within a dozen paces. The beggars were harder to pass — pressed against the fountain stones, wedged between stalls, still enough that the foot traffic split around them without glancing down. She gave a copper carp to the first. Then the next. Then four more before Brunor’s hand found her arm.
“Stop that. You’ll be broke before we reach the guildhall.”
“I’m aware.”
“Then enough.”
She stopped giving.
They passed a jeweler’s stall near the midpoint of the road, and Elaine’s stride shortened without her meaning it to. A necklace on dark velvet — silver chain, fine-linked, studded with purple amethysts that held the afternoon light like small bruises. The merchant was talking price before she had opened her mouth.
She shook her head and kept walking. The necklace stayed behind her.
Two streets later, she glanced down a narrow alley between a tanner’s shop and a boarded storefront. Habit. A tall, thin figure stood against the far wall, face lost inside a heavy hood. Around his neck, catching what light the alley gave, hung the necklace. The same silver chain. The same amethysts.
The man turned. Saw her looking. Disappeared into the dark at the alley’s far end.
Elaine’s count reset. She retraced the route from the jeweler’s stall — two streets, three turns, and he had kept pace close enough to buy what she had lingered over and let her find him wearing it. They had been followed since the gate. He wanted her to know it.
She closed the distance to Brunor.
“We should go. I think I just saw one of Damien’s.”
Brunor said nothing. He did not need to. She had watched this hatred build for years — the contracts that collapsed with Damien’s fingerprints on the wreckage, the slander that always found its way inside Caer Seryth’s walls no matter how carefully they sealed the gates. She knew it as well as he did. She had paid for knowing it. The day she left Damien’s house for the Promise, he called her a traitor in front of their family and made certain she understood the cost.
She had never regretted the choice. But the meeting was close now, and for the first time, she did not know what she would say to him.
The rain found them before the afternoon was done. Thin at first, then steady, and the temperature dropped with it.
They handled what remained of the day’s errands — Frederick’s supply contacts, message drops for the Promise — and rested in Bordou Square. The locals called it the Crimson Cage. Elaine looked at the empty centre of the square, where no merchant set a stall and no foot traffic crossed, and remembered why they called it that. A hundred years ago, the city fell, and Emperor Leonard put every man and boy to the sword in this square. The retreating army took the women and girls. 
The stones had been replaced twice since. People still walked around the middle as though the new mortar had not fully taken.
Brunor tapped her arm and pointed.
The cloaked figure from the alley was crossing the square toward them, a folded note between two long fingers.
“This should be good,” Brunor murmured.
The man stopped before them and extended the letter. His teeth were brown and rotting, his lips dark. His breath froze in the wet air with every word.
“Lord Chancellor Damien Beauforth requests your presence at the Golden Scale guildhall. Immediately. I am to escort you.”
Brunor took the note and read it aloud.
“To Brunor Halvyr and Elaine Morrandis the Bastard. You are to report to the Golden Scale guildhall to discuss matters of utmost significance relating to, but not limited to, the presence of the named party in my city. Do not delay.”
Brunor crumpled the parchment and tossed it into the fountain.
“And if I refuse?”
The man’s lips tightened. His hands were shaking — not from the cold.
“That would be most foolish, boy. Damien is the chancellor. My lord’s word is absolute.” His voice steadied, rehearsed. “To disobey a direct order is treason. Do you wish to die?”
“Threatening me with death is a waste of your time,” Brunor said. “I don’t take commands from your *master*. If I visit him, it’s because I chose to.”
The man laughed. The sound scraped out of him. “You must be touched in the head. I’ll relay the message again. Slowly this time. Chancellor Damien Beauforth demands you, and this bastard bi—”
Brunor’s dagger was at his throat before the word finished. The tip pressed against the soft skin below the man’s jaw, deep enough to dimple, and the laughter died on his ruined teeth.
“Tell your lord that if he wants to meet us, he comes to us himself.”
“Has madness taken you, boy?! Lord Damien will hear—”
Brunor leaned the blade in. A thread of blood ran down the man’s neck, and the rain took it. The fury on his face was more than insults.
“Call me a boy again, and it just might.”
“You won’t kill me in a crowded square! You wouldn’t dare!”
They stood in the rain, eye to eye, and the square around them went still. Elaine’s hand was already on her sword, her pulse so high she felt it in her teeth. Killing this man — any of Damien’s men — in the open, under his jurisdiction, would be the end of them both. She knew it. Brunor knew it. He was doing it anyway.
Then a voice cut through the rain. 
Deep. Cold. Familiar.
“Enough of this brutish nonsense. Release your blade from Hans’ throat. At once.”
Elaine turned. Five royal guards stood in formation, spears at the ready. When had they arrived?
 And walking between them, hooded in heavy yellow over a wheat-gold gambeson, the lion crest of House Beauforth sewn into the breast — Damien.
Brunor withdrew the dagger. Sheathed it. His face had shut. The rage was still in him — she could see it in his hands, the way the fingers locked and did not open.
Damien pulled back his hood. Dark hair pulled tight into a top knot. Dark blue eyes that didn’t blink in the rain. On his hip, his golden blade. In his left hand, Gungnir — the family lance, its brilliance catching the downpour’s light as though the weather was an inconvenience it chose not to acknowledge.
Five years since Elaine had seen him. The last time, she’d put him on his back after a disarm and walked out of this city. Looking at his face now — leaner, harder, the boyishness ground out of it — she understood he had not forgotten.
“Why such hostility, Halvyr?” Damien’s voice was measured and distributed evenly. “I extended an invitation to speak as civilized men, and you behave no better than a wild creature; all you northmen and your old, forgotten ways. Predictable to the last. I knew you’d give Hans trouble. It’s why I came myself.”
“You waste your time,” Brunor said. “We have nothing to discuss.”
“Is that so? What a pity.” Damien tilted his head towards Brunor. “Tell me then, Halvyr. What have I done to warrant the rage of the cub?”
She watched Brunor weigh it. He knew the smart play — she had seen him swallow worse from richer men when a contract demanded it. He knew that nothing said in this square could land without Damien turning it into rope. She saw the moment he stopped caring.
“What haven’t you done?” His voice was flat. “But you crossed a line, Damien. Two months ago. Captain Alessa was ambushed on the road from your city. Bandits who knew every detail of her route — details not even Alessa had been given. They trailed her for leagues. Killed every man in her escort but one and held her down to watch them die.”
He stepped forward. “Similar incidents all year. Every one of them stinks of your coin and your connections. Only a handful of people knew her itinerary. You were one of them. Do you deny it?”
Elaine hadn’t known. Her grip shifted on her sword — not to draw. To hold on to something before her mark flared.
Damien chuckled, took a long look at the two commanders, then sighed. Somewhere past Nullbrand’s suffocating aura, Elaine felt something from her cousin. An emotion too brisk to catch but potent enough to reach before being snuffed. 
“Of course I deny them. Tales and fallacies. You’re inventing reasons just to try to kill me. But tell me. Why would your captain visit Glintgate at all, I wonder? Perhaps because the whore was suspected of meeting with a known spy linked to Naaren? What was your Captain doing? Want to know what mine told me? He told of a Promise Captain in my city exchanging Caltherian intelligence for gold and a promise that Naaren would spare your little company from its tariffs next month.” Damien cleared the wet hair from his face. Do *you* deny this?” 
Brunor’s face went white.
“More poison. He’s trying to provoke you,” Elaine said low and close. “Don’t give him what he wants. Let’s go. Frederick is waiting for us.”
Damien’s gaze found her. He said nothing for a long moment, then he moved forward. He handed Gungnir to a guard, then drew steel—no—gold. A golden blade cleared the sheath with a high, clean note, and the sound triggered a crescendo from every weapon in the square: Elaine’s, Brunor’s, every guard’s. Runes were etched into the guard, causing the rain to sizzle whenever it met the raised symbols.
Damien pointed the golden blade at Elaine.
“And you. Do not think I have forgotten how you disgraced my house with the smear of your birth. You should have been left in Qunthren bay the night they pulled you from your mother. Perhaps I should finish what those assassins could not.”
“I don’t want to fight you, cousin. Leave us be. We’re here to answer a summons.”
“I am not your cousin. Do not use that word with me again.” He said it absentmindedly, automatically, and finally. “Never forget what you are, Elaine.”
Her fingers dug into the grip until the bones creaked. The missing pinky throbbed where it used to be, and the grip felt weak for it. She squeezed harder.
The square was watching — merchants, priests, scholars, beggars, guards, a knot of choristers who should have been inside the church a few paces away. The rain came down harder. No one moved.
Brunor stepped between them.
Damien lowered his blade and rested the tip on the stones. “I heard what happened to your keep. The Order had its way with you and violated you in more ways than one. To lose the rift and your dignity in the same night. It must have been hard for you, Halvyr.”
“It’s even harder now,” he replied.
“Harder than the men who lie dead in the ground because of you and the bastard’s leadership? Weak kings trusted you to handle something important to us all, and look how you failed. Perhaps those casualties were for the best, as it will now teach the world who their true saviors are. I won't let those men’s pointless sacrifices be in vain.”
“Savior? Is that how you see yourself? You overestimate your worth and underestimate the Order. They will have their way with you just as they had with us, and this war will all but guarantee it.”
“There is not going to be a war. Naaren declaring against the four kingdoms is suicide. In the actual world, men and women will be forced to band together to clean up the mess you two made and put a stop to these rift worshiping cravens. But before that. I think the first order of business will be disbanding your worthless fucking company.”
Brunor shifted his weight forward.
“Enough. Are we going to do what you came here to do, or are you going to try and keep rambling ?”
Damien locked his eyes onto Elaine. “You would like that, wouldn’t you? To see blood spilled?”
She opened her mouth.
“What is your answer?” Brunor interrupted before she could speak.
Damien tucked the blade beneath his arm. The annoyance on his face brightened into something hungrier. He had never refused a duel and never lost one. Courtesy could not reach the part of him that woke when steel cleared a sheath.
She grabbed Brunor’s arm. “The moment you swing, his guards move in. You’ll die in this square, and everything we came here to do dies with you.”
Brunor pulled free.
Damien’s laughter was rare and false, and it carried across the growing downpour. 
“Very well. I will fight you, Brunor Halvyr, son of Uthred Halvyr. To kill the White Bear’s cub would bring me satisfaction beyond measure.” 
He pointed his blade toward his guards. “Do not interfere.”
Elaine could do nothing. She backed away next to the group of white-cloaked choirboys. The choirmaster, a tall skinny boy with freckles and a crooked nose, tugged her cloak.
“Excuse me. Milady. Is someone going to die? Should I go get the Mothers?”
“No. Nobody will die. Let the Mothers worry about the dead, not the living,” she replied, but she sounded uncertain even to herself. 
The choirmaster stepped back and whispered something to one of the boys, who walked off and headed into the church.
Half a day. They had been in this city half a day, and she had watched it all devolve into more senseless violence.
Damien’s guards stepped back. The crowd in the square pressed outward, forming a ring. Nobles. Merchants. Laborers. All of them are here to watch.
“State your terms,” Damien said. “The challenger decides. First blood?”
“You know my terms. Do you even need to ask?”
“Good.” Damien raised his blade. “To whoever yields.”
The rain fell harder.
