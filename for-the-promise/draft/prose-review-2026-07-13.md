# Prose Review — Full Manuscript (2026-07-13)

Scope: chapters 1–6 as they stand in `draft/chapters/`. Chapters 1–4 and the
first half of chapter 5 (above the draft marker) are the author's prose;
chapter 5's second half and chapter 6 are the assistant drafts the author
ruled stand. **No prose was edited for this review** — per project rules the
manuscript is untouched. Every finding below is a report with a location, so
each one is a yes/no decision rather than homework.

Findings are measured against the author's own standards: `planning/source-banned-list.md`
and `style-profile.json`. Line numbers refer to the current files.

---

## What is working (keep doing this)

The instincts are right and the best material is genuinely strong. Specifically:

- **The duel (ch. 4)** is the best sustained writing in the manuscript.
  Legible technical fencing, fights-as-characterization ("The man fought as
  if he were correcting a pupil"), psychological warfare mid-bout, and the
  spear-tell read. This is the style profile working at full strength.
- **Worldbuilding through use and opinion.** Castles as "receipts," the
  condolence letter that misspelled his name (ch. 1), the Crimson Cage and
  the mortar that "had not fully taken" (ch. 2), the Darrian/Osfeld argument
  in the College (ch. 5) — no exposition dumps anywhere. The banned list's
  hardest rule, and it's being kept.
- **The mark rendering** is consistent and distinctive: emotions as read
  objects in slices, "fear, worn smooth from long handling," "Calm has a
  shape." The Nullbrand-mute mechanic is tracked correctly scene to scene.
- **Dialogue idiolects hold.** Frederick's counting, Elaine's rants that
  pivot to absurd specifics (six carps for stabling), Mikah's dialect,
  Damien playing to the crowd. Nobody sounds like anybody else.
- **Small beats that pay**: the mismatched boots → the blister (ch. 3), the
  haired grape Frederick eats (ch. 5), the rat meeting the wall (ch. 4), the
  unfinished horse carving (ch. 1).

The improvements below are mostly about (1) mechanical cleanup, (2) holding
your own banned-list line in chapters 1–2, and (3) rationing devices that
are good once and diluted by the fourth use.

## Tier 1 — Mechanical (objective errors; highest return per minute)

### 1.1 Dialogue paragraphing in chapter 1
Lines 6, 9, and 11 are wall-of-text paragraphs with dialogue run directly
into narration, often with the space missing after the closing quote:

> …she let go of it.“Jumpy today, El?”She glanced at him…

New speaker → new paragraph, throughout. This is the single biggest
readability problem in the manuscript and it is confined to chapter 1
(chapters 2–6 are formatted correctly, which suggests ch. 1 predates the
current process).

### 1.2 Unbalanced / broken quotation marks
- ch. 1, line 11 — `Still, El is treated better than me…` opens with no
  quote mark.
- ch. 1, line 16 — `"Oh.”` mixes a straight open quote with a curly close.
- ch. 1, line 28 — Brunor's speech continues past `he said eventually.` with
  no reopening quote (`You've always believed we aren't…`).
- ch. 1, line 49 — stray orphan quote after "towards the stars."
- ch. 1, line 43 — `mother‘s` uses a reversed apostrophe.
- ch. 2, line 85 — Damien's `Do *you* deny this?”` has a close quote but no
  open (the tag "Damien cleared the wet hair from his face." splits his
  speech).
- ch. 3, lines 52–53 — Tella's speech spans two paragraphs; line 52 closes
  its quote AND line 53 opens without one. Convention: no closing quote on
  the first paragraph, opening quote on the second.
- ch. 5, line 10 — `“ Have two!` — stray space, and the quote never closes
  before the narration (`He took a heavy sip…`).
- ch. 2, line 102 — `keep rambling ?` space before the question mark.
- ch. 5, line 72 — missing period: "The Caltheran court had taken it"
- ch. 5, line 76 — stray `* *` markup artifact.

### 1.3 Duplicated revision artifact — chapter 3, lines 11 and 13
Two versions of the same sentence both survived:

> "She shoved the memory aside and corrected her jab, but not before someone off to the side whistled."
> "She pushed the hazy thought away and corrected her messy jab, but not before someone off to the side whistled."

Pick one, delete the other (and the whistle in line 14 answers it once).

### 1.4 Tense slips
- ch. 2, line 117 — "All of them **are** here to watch." (past narration)
- ch. 1, line 99 — "as someone who learned to read late and **doubts** their
  ability" (also: "cautiously" is a banned-family adverb; "like a man who'd
  learned to read late and doubted it" fixes both)

### 1.5 Spelling standardization (flagged in style-profile as open)
Both systems are live in the same book: *colour* (ch. 2, ch. 4) vs *color*
(ch. 6 ×5); *recognised* (ch. 4) vs *recognized* (ch. 1, ch. 5); *gaol*
(ch. 4); *jewellery* (ch. 5). Pick one system; a single grep pass fixes it.
Also *amenicite/Amenicite* capitalized both ways within ch. 2 line 6.

### 1.6 Em-dash convention
Spaced (`word — word`) and closed (`word—word`) em-dashes are mixed, often
in the same chapter (ch. 4 line 9 vs line 7). Pick one and standardize.

### 1.7 Known one-word fixes already on the docket
- ch. 1, line 67: "Archwarden **Telvor**" → Tauvor (author's call, still open)
- ch. 4, line 124: "forfeited its **base**" → *bass* (voice register)
- ch. 5, line 87: "Under the old lord's **procedure**" — suspect wrong word
  (*composure*? *posture*?). As written it doesn't parse.
- ch. 5, line 91: "exhaled once through his nose, **the way when he was
  deciding**" — missing words ("the way he did when…").
- ch. 5, line 114: "…walk the length of the floor in the quiet he'd left for
  exactly that **and made to wait by a man who'd timed it**" — the final
  clause has lost its subject; needs untangling.
- ch. 3, line 43: "An enemy commander **died** behind his own lines… a feat"
  — the feat is Tella's, but the verb gives it to the commander. ("An enemy
  commander dead behind his own lines" keeps the fragment rhythm and the
  agency.)

## Tier 2 — Banned-list hits in the author's chapters

The banned-list discipline is visibly strong from chapter 4 onward. Nearly
all of the violations cluster in chapters 1–2, which reads like they were
written before the list hardened. Instances, by ban:

**Beat placeholders — "for a (long) moment" (banned 2.4)**
- ch. 1, line 9: "She glanced at him for a moment"
- ch. 2, line 88: "He said nothing for a long moment"
- ch. 5, line 35: "He looked at her for a long moment" (author's half)

**Vague interiority — "something" + verb (banned 1.2/2.2)**
- ch. 1, line 102: "He felt something ugly rise from his chest." — this is a
  real moment (Elaine wielding Damien's name has power Brunor's own doesn't;
  what rises is closer to shame with jealousy under it). Naming it is worth
  more here than anywhere else in the chapter.
- ch. 2, line 84: "Elaine felt something from her cousin. An emotion too
  brisk to catch" — half-exempt because the mark mechanic is *about*
  incomplete reads, but the second sentence already does the work; the
  "something" sentence can go.
- ch. 1, line 75: "recognition, annoyance, something harder" — the list
  names two and hedges the third; the third is the one that matters.

**Anthropomorphized silence (banned 1.31)**
- ch. 1, line 17: "Silence filled the gap between the two."
- ch. 3, line 30: "Quiet threatened to settle in. Tella broke it first." —
  the second sentence is the approved move; the first can simply go.
- ch. 1, line 6: "The silence demanded nothing of him." — flagging for
  completeness; arguably this one earns its place because the *demand*
  contrast is the point of the sanctuary. Author's call.

**Stock physical tells (banned 2.1)**
- ch. 1, line 35: "His stomach dropped."
- ch. 1, line 11: "Elaine went still." / ch. 5, line 51: "The mug went still
  in Frederick's hands." — the mug variant is the better one; two uses of
  the same freeze in the same book, keep one.
- ch. 4, line 86: "Brunor's teeth ground until his jaw ached."
- ch. 1, line 59: "Elaine released the sigh she had been holding." — the
  breath-she-was-holding tell, lightly disguised.

**Adverb/tonal dialogue tags (banned 2.5)**
- ch. 2, line 27: "she clarified softly"
- ch. 5, line 57: "Frederick whispered finally"
- ch. 2, line 92: "absentmindedly, automatically, and finally" — three
  adverbs and a banned word; the *idea* (dismissal so practiced it's
  reflexive) deserves a real sentence.
- ch. 1, line 26 "Elaine said, low." / ch. 2, line 87 "said low and close" —
  borderline; noted, not urged.

**Temperature-for-emotion (banned 2.10)**
- ch. 1, line 46: "Elaine's convictions radiated off her like heat."
- ch. 2, line 70: "Deep. Cold. Familiar." — double hit: temperature plus a
  triple-fragment beat (banned 1.5/1.6) for Damien's entrance.

**Triple fragments (banned 1.5/1.6)**
- ch. 2, line 117: "Nobles. Merchants. Laborers." (plus the tense slip in
  the next sentence). Note the style profile *does* license tactical
  fragments ("Left treeline, right treeline. Flank.") — the ban is for
  rhetorical emphasis, which this is.

**Stone/water impact imagery (banned 2.12 family)**
- ch. 5, line 44: "sat inside it like a stone the river had decided to go
  around" — fresher than the dead version and defensible alone, but see the
  water-density note in Tier 3.

**Articulation by proxy (banned 1.993)**
- ch. 2, line 44: "Brunor said nothing. He did not need to." — the paragraph
  then *does* supply the content, so the fix is just cutting the second
  sentence.

**Gaze-cluster vocabulary (banned 2.6)**
- ch. 5, line 93: "the careful attention of a man **cataloguing** a weapon"
  — the observation is excellent; the one banned word in it is swappable
  (*pricing* is already this book's word, though Frederick owns it —
  *memorizing* might sit better on a soldier).

**Elegant variation (banned 1.998)**
- ch. 3: "the young scout captain" (l. 19), "The young captain" (l. 23) for
  Phoebe; ch. 1, line 53: "The scout commander folded her arms" for Elaine;
  ch. 5, line 11: "the quartermaster exclaimed" for Frederick. All four are
  name-avoidance rather than POV work.

## Tier 3 — Craft patterns (the needle-movers)

### 3.1 Ration the signature devices
Several excellent devices are being spent too often, and repetition is
converting them from voice into tic:

- **Eyes catching/holding light.** Johanna's eyes hold the light (ch. 1,
  twice — it's the emotional key of the chapter); Elaine's "permafrost
  trapping a flame in time" (ch. 1); Frederick's "brown eyes also caught
  that specific light and held it. Could Brunor catch it, too?" (ch. 1,
  l. 110); Damien's armor catches the sun, "His eyes didn't" (ch. 1, l. 76);
  the amethysts "held the afternoon light like small bruises" (ch. 2). The
  Johanna/Elaine pairing is the motif doing its job; the Damien inversion is
  sharp. The Frederick instance is the one to cut or clarify — as written,
  the reader can't tell what "that specific light" is or why Brunor is
  auditioning for it, and it muddies the motif's meaning (if it's about who
  carries Johanna's kind of light, say so in Brunor's terms).
- **"Worn smooth"** ×3: "worn smooth from repetition" (ch. 1, l. 9), "worn
  smooth from long handling" (ch. 5, l. 87), "worn smooth as a coin" (ch. 5,
  l. 125). All three are good; the book supports one, maybe two.
- **"Frederick broke first / as he always did"** ×4: ch. 3 l. 30 (Tella),
  ch. 5 l. 45, ch. 5 l. 215, ch. 6 l. 120. Twice in chapter 5 alone. The
  characterization is right; the *sentence* shouldn't be the same each time.
- **"Thirty years"** ×6 across chapters 5–6 (ch. 5 ll. 30, 36, 221; ch. 6
  ll. 95, 132, 160). One establishment per chapter is plenty.
- **Rain intensifiers**: "The rain came down harder" (ch. 2, l. 94) and "The
  rain fell harder" (ch. 2, l. 121) within the same closing sequence. Rain
  as pressure is a declared motif; these two near-identical beats should be
  one.
- **Water similes in chapter 5** ×4: the river-stone (l. 44), "a lane the
  way water gives one" (l. 107), "the way water finds a channel" (l. 147 —
  this one is load-bearing, it's how Kymancy works), "washing up against
  Elaine like tidewater" (l. 213). Keep the Kymancy one, thin the others.

### 3.2 Filtering and eyes-as-agents
Close third is strongest when perception is direct. Two habits add distance:

- **Filter verbs** — "Brunor watched the coals shift," "He watched Elaine
  hold the look," "took a long look at Elaine, who was watching him"
  (ch. 4, l. 123 stacks two watchers in one clause). *watched/watching*
  appears ~30 times across the six chapters. Many are fine; the ones worth
  cutting are where the watched thing could simply happen on the page.
- **Autonomous body parts** — "Her head snapped toward him, hand finding her
  hilt" works (reflex is the point), but "Elaine's eyes watched the flames"
  (ch. 1, l. 22), "Brunor's grey eyes came off the window… then they went
  back to the rain" (ch. 5, l. 29, recurring in that scene), "His eyes
  remained until…" (ch. 1, l. 6) hand agency to organs. When it's a choice,
  let the character make it.

### 3.3 The chapter 1 problem, specifically
Chapter 1 is the weakest chapter and the first thing an agent or reader
sees. Beyond the formatting (Tier 1), two content notes:

- **The opening paragraph** (l. 6) front-loads geography and does one thing
  the POV can't: "Six hundred souls resided within the dark, weathered
  stones of Caer Seryth… their anxieties resonating off the towering walls."
  Brunor has no mark; he can't perceive a keep's anxieties from half a
  league. It's also the banned atmospheric front-load (1.94) in miniature.
  The paragraph comes alive exactly where Brunor starts *doing* ("Left
  treeline, right treeline. Flank.") — consider letting it start closer to
  that. Also within the same paragraph: "aroma… aromas," "powerful stallion…
  sturdy post" (empty adjectives), "bit at the lungs" (whose?), and the
  dangler "She smiled at him, filled with white teeth" (the smile was, she
  wasn't).
- **The Johanna flashback dialogue** is stiffer than any present-day
  dialogue in the book: "Your father does not hate you, son; he loves you
  dearly. You are his heir and our only child." It's delivering theme at
  the reader. The ear-flick chase that follows is the scene's real cargo —
  it does more characterization in six lines than the speech does in
  twelve. Rough up or halve the speech; protect the chase. The frame lines
  around the memory ("After a while, it wasn't the moons he saw." / "That
  was what he always remembered first. Not the answer.") are excellent and
  need no help.

### 3.4 Expository dialogue in chapter 3
Tella's closing speech (ll. 52–54) tells Phoebe things Phoebe knows —
"You have forgotten who trained her in both the standard and the political
forms of warfare… Elaine Morrandis is potentially the most dangerous woman
on the continent." It's the reader being briefed. The information can
survive as Phoebe's own interiority (she's the POV, and she *believes* this
about her sister) with Tella keeping only the line that's actually aimed at
Phoebe: "He is afraid of her. Remember that when you are worried about her."

### 3.5 Register slips (light modernism scrub)
The narrative voice absorbs a few contemporary idioms that sit oddly beside
"gods-knew-how-far": "checkpoint" ×2 (ch. 1, ll. 72, 76 — *the gate*, *the
lane*), "a couple of minutes earlier" (ch. 5, l. 2 — ch. 6 already uses
"half a bell"), "in a few" (ch. 3, l. 35), "The table displayed an array of
fruits" (ch. 5, l. 8 — catalog language; "The table held…"). Borderline
keepers that read as voice rather than slip: "stop paying tuition" (ch. 1),
"patient as compounding interest" (ch. 6, justified by Frederick's ledger
idiom).

### 3.6 Sentences that don't parse or say less than intended
- ch. 2, line 65: "The fury on his face was more than insults." — more than
  the insults warranted? caused by more than insults? Unclear.
- ch. 2, line 77: "Damien's voice was measured and distributed evenly" —
  "distributed evenly" reads statistical; the intent (courtroom evenness for
  the crowd) deserves its own image.
- ch. 2, line 88: "the sound triggered a crescendo from every weapon in the
  square" — a crescendo is gradual; what happened is everyone drew. Saying
  that is stronger.
- ch. 1, line 109: "Frederick had more in common with the stones of the keep
  than most people in those days." — than *with* most people? than most
  people *did*? Both readings are live.
- ch. 4, line 6: "He and Damien both took five paces apart" — "each took
  five paces."
- ch. 4, line 12: "let the weight follow tardily" — "tardily" is nobody's
  voice; "late" is.
- ch. 5, line 6: "The girl's tiny head nodded" — reads as anatomy, probably
  means timid. If the comedy is intended, keep it.

### 3.7 Structural notes
- **Chapter 3 is ~1,350 words** against a 3,200–4,500 norm and reads
  unfinished (the duplicate lines, the abrupt exit). If it's meant as a
  short interstitial, the duplicates still need resolving; if not, it has
  room — the Ryn/disloyalty seed and the Tella backstory paragraph both
  invite one more scene beat.
- **Chapter 5 lacks the dateline block and epigraph** every other chapter
  carries (already flagged in the draft notes; repeating so it doesn't get
  lost).

   thirty years, rain-harder, water similes — one read-through with a list
   in hand.
5. **Chapter 3**: resolve duplicates, decide short-or-full, move Tella's
   briefing into Phoebe's head.
