# Geography
## Halreth
VESPUR PLAINS — Canon Profile

Territory: Halreth. The plains constitute the kingdom’s heartland and primary agricultural base.

Shape and Extent: Wide at the western coast where Halreth faces the Palecrest Sea, narrowing eastward through the middle of Eòs toward the geographic center where Johanna’s Promise sits. The western mouth is the broadest, most populated stretch — coastal towns, port access, the bulk of Halreth’s farming and trade infrastructure. As the plains narrow east, settlement thins, the land becomes more open and exposed, and the road toward Caltheran runs through territory that feels increasingly frontier.

Northern edge: Vespine’s Cradle runs along the top of the plains, separating them from Caltheran’s territory and deflecting Varkaan’s northern weather systems. The passes through the Cradle are the only viable land routes from Varkaan and Caltheran into the plains. Caer Seryth — now Johanna’s Promise — sits at the base of the Cradle at the eastern narrowing of the plains, commanding the southern mouth of those passes. Its strategic position is geographic fact before it’s political choice.

Leylines: The deepest concentration of leyline activity on Eòs runs beneath the Vespur Plains, originating ultimately from the Merge impact on Ao and threading through the planet’s bedrock before surfacing — or nearly surfacing — here thousands of leagues away. The plains are habitable and fertile. The common belief is either that the leylines run deep enough beneath the bedrock that surface exposure remains below lethal threshold, or that something in the plains’ composition buffers the effect. Neither has been formally proven. Caltheran scholars debate it. Halreth doesn’t fund the research.

Magical weather: Runelight Rain falls most frequently and most intensely over the Vespur Plains. On heavy nights the grass holds a faint luminescence until dawn. Locals treat it as ordinary. Visitors find it unsettling. Chroma-Hares in the plains have unusually vivid fur-color shifts during arcane events — they’re considered the most reliable magical weather indicators on the continent, and Halreth exports them for exactly that purpose.

Agriculture: The plains are fertile. Whatever the leylines do to the soil — whether by depth, diffusion, or the buffer that the people believe in — the result is exceptional crop yield. Halreth feeds a significant portion of Eòs. Grain, livestock, orchard produce from the valley regions. This is the economic engine of Halreth’s diplomatic influence. You don’t go to war with the kingdom that feeds you.

The founding ideology: Halreth’s first king understood what sat beneath the plains. He made a choice — not weakness, not ignorance, but a deliberate decision that harnessing a continental weapon would cost more than it could ever buy. That decision became Halreth’s political identity. Two centuries later most Halrethians have inherited the philosophy without fully understanding its origin. The king’s reasoning is recorded somewhere. Whether the current political class has read it carefully is a different question.

The plains are shaped like a narrowing peninsula running west to east. The wide western mouth is the most habitable, most populated, most coastal-facing stretch. As the plains narrow eastward toward the continent’s center, the leylines — which branch outward from the deep concentration beneath the plains — would naturally push closer to the surface where the bedrock narrows and the buffer thins. The eastern tip, near the Vel’Eran border and Shadowfen, is where the geology runs out of room. The leylines surface. Things get dangerous.

That also means Shadowfen isn’t just marshy and politically peripheral because of geography alone. The ground there is actively hostile on an arcane level. The Kymancy rift sitting in that same corridor isn’t coincidence — rift activity and leyline surface proximity in the same zone is a pattern that scholars in this world should find alarming and probably don’t fully understand yet.

## Halreth river system:

The Renavon — primary river, flows west through the Vespur Plains, empties into the Palecrest Sea near Theralion City. The economic spine of Halreth’s inland trade.

Amos’ Fingers — the Renavon’s delta, several leagues from the coast where the river spreads into multiple channels before meeting the sea. Named for the deity of Judgment and Protection. Historical site of repeated military engagements between Halreth and rival kingdoms. Strategically contested because whoever controls the delta controls river access to the Halreth interior.

The Orella — secondary river running along the southern face of Vespine’s Cradle, colder and faster, feeding mill towns and fishing settlements before curving southwest toward the sea.

The Chimera River — eastern river draining the marshy tip of the plains. Behaves differently across its length — fast and cold in the upper stretch, slow and silty in the middle, brackish and unreliable near Shadowfen. Does not reach the sea. Dissolves into the Shadowfen marshlands. Named for what it is.

## Theralion City and Old Thenbury:

Theralion City — Halreth’s capital, built into and on top of marble coastal cliffs above the Palecrest Sea. The city is inseparable from its geology. Approached from the water it presents as white stone rising directly from the sea. The Renavon reaches the coast nearby, its delta infrastructure feeding the port below. Access from the waterline to the city’s sea gates is a two-hour climb. The cliffs are the walls. The sea gates are the chokepoint.

Old Thenbury — the port settlement at the base of Theralion’s cliffs. Predates the capital. Fishing, trade, dockwork, sailors, merchants. Culturally maritime rather than plains-oriented. Looks up at Theralion with the particular indifference of something that was there first. Technically part of Theralion. Practically its own world.

# Caltheran — Geographic Profile:

Territory running from Vespine’s Cradle in the south to a narrow, jagged, largely uninhabitable strip of the Ebonmaw Ocean in the north. Longest border shared with Varkaan to the northwest — the most historically contested boundary on the continent. Western border with Halreth. Southern access through Vespine’s Cradle passes into the Vespur Plains.

Aedric Spire dead center. Built for position and arcane access, not trade or water. The Echoa Archive sits on the most magically saturated ground in Caltheran where leyline branches from the Vespur Plains push northward through the kingdom’s interior. Athens is the model — a civilization that made knowledge its primary currency and built its seat of power on sacred ground.

Glintgate in the south near the Cradle — the commercial engine. Where goods enter from Halreth, where the Aldren begins its northwestern journey, where the runewright guilds operate. The city doing the economic work the capital doesn’t bother with.

Hudria near the Varkaan border on the Aldren — originally Varkaan’s, now Caltheran’s, governed by House Farryn since the War of Copper Kings. Original keep of Uthred and Johanna Halvyr. A city that has changed hands enough times that its identity is genuinely split between two cultures.

Marisport on the northern coast — the only maritime access Caltheran has, facing the Ebonmaw. Not a thriving port. Nobody sails the Ebonmaw beyond the coastal shelf. Marisport exists for coastal trade between the three northern kingdoms and not much else. It has the specific character of a port city that knows the sea it faces is not going anywhere good.

The Singing Frost — shared with Varkaan, occurring around the winter solstice. Evocation-charged cold air rolling down from Varkaan’s highlands. Hits hardest along the border, fades as it pushes south. Hudria gets it badly. Aedric Spire gets a muted version. The Vespur Plains are largely sheltered behind Vespine’s Cradle.

The Old Road running north from Caer Seryth alongside the Aldren through ancient battlegrounds toward Varkaan. The Gravewrens haunting that corridor.


# Varkaan — Geographic Profile:

Scale — half of Canada. The largest single kingdom on Eòs by a considerable margin. Governing it requires systems, not just force.

Northern position — widest point of the continent. Highland mountain ranges dominating the north and west. The Skyplate Highlands running east to west across the upper territory, terminating at the Ashen Coast region on the eastern end. Magic-resistant terrain throughout the Skyplate range. Abandoned Era of Collapse keeps and siege ruins throughout — The Shell being the most significant known site.

Capital — Druhmir Hold at the plateau edge where highlands descend into the interior. Administrative and commercial crossroads of a kingdom too internally diverse to govern any other way. Seat of the Iron Guilds.

Interior — the Sol’telarī, an arid interior plateau and desert region produced by the highland rain shadow effect. Black sand region within it — House Al-Khem’s territory. River Kýe running through the desert, named by Iskari settlers before the kingdom existed, flowing toward the Ebonmaw.

Highland interior — riddled with large lakes and hundreds of smaller ones fed by snowmelt. Abundant freshwater. Fishing culture alongside mining culture.

Iskari substrate — desert nomads descended from pre-kingdom Iskari settlers. Iskari cultural influence embedded throughout the Sol’telarī. Multiple historical territorial wars. The Varkaan Dominion built on contested land.

Northwestern coast — fertile strip facing the Ebonmaw, fed by a moderate Restoration leyline current. Site of centuries of wars with Caltheran. The Aldren runs through this contested corridor.

Buffer Zone — northwesternmost Varkaan, past the Skyplate Highlands. Uninhabited wilderness. Era of Collapse ruins throughout. The Marshall’s keep sits on the Ebonmaw coastline. Order of the Wyrm connected. Climatically isolated by the Skyplate Highland magic resistance deflecting the annual Ebonmaw cold front east and west rather than allowing it through.

Seasonal weather — the Ebonmaw cold front splits annually at the Skyplate Highlands, deflecting west toward Caltheran and east toward Naaren’s coast. The Singing Frost — evocation-charged, shared with Caltheran, winter solstice.

Naaren — Geographic Profile, Locked:

Position — east/northeast Eòs. Bordered by Caltheran to the north and west via The Point cutting off direct Varkaan access. Vel’Era to the direct south where the forest systems merge without a natural border. Halreth to the southwest beyond the Shadowfen. The Selenic Deep facing the entire eastern coastline.

Shape — the kingdom bulges eastward, its northeastern tip forming the Ashen Coast before bending south along the Selenic Deep. Caltheran’s strip pressing in from the northwest is what creates the isolated northeastern character.

Terrain gradient — plains in the south transitioning into woodlands, deepening into forest, descending into swamp and marshland as elevation drops and the water table rises. Shadowfen is the largest marsh, centrally positioned, fed by the Myrn’s lower drainage and the saltwater table. The forest in the south is the northern extension of Vel’Era’s silver forest system — the border is political, not ecological.

Eastern coastline — Hawaiian in character. Dramatic, volcanic-formed, dark rock meeting the Selenic Deep. Ships hug this coast in the established trade lanes, staying close to shore rather than venturing into the Deep. Nightshade Port positioned to surveil every vessel moving through the lane.

Northeastern corner — the volcanic ring running under the coast produces a geothermal zone past the Ashen Coast’s grey bluffs. Hot springs, geysers, some toxic vents, steam rising from the ground. Unique fauna evolved around geothermal heat. The cold front deflecting east from the Skyplate Highlands annually collides with this geothermal activity — cold air meeting ground heat, persistent thermal fog, mineral-glinting grey ash sand. A climate that exists nowhere else on Eòs.

The Ashen Coast — grey-stoned bluff country at the far northeastern tip. Practically ungoverned. Caltheran pressing in at its back. The Selenic Deep at its face. Canon locked in full detail from the Renu Arc.

Capital — Keth’var Bastion. Positioned on sorcerer-altered elevated ground in the middle of Naaren’s southern forest and plains zone. The Myrn runs under the city through the transmuted terrain. All commerce funnels through the lower city at the river passage — a chokepoint that gives Keth’var total economic control over Naaren’s interior trade and makes it effectively impregnable from outside while being catastrophically vulnerable to internal crisis. The sorcery that altered the ground dates to the Era of Collapse. The original method is either lost or archived somewhere in Keth’var and not discussed publicly.

The Myrn — primary river, splits the kingdom, runs under Keth’var, terminates in a saltwater inland lake near the eastern coast. The Chimera River feeds the marshland system from the west and connects to the same terminal lake.

Governance — the Republic of Naaren in name. High Chancellor in practice. Byzantine structure — elaborate constitutional forms maintained while effective authority is personal and dynastic. The Cipher’s Guild is the institutional backbone, predating and outlasting individual Chancellors. Genetic magic control policy is legislation, not decree — embedded in republican law and therefore harder to dismantle than a monarch’s edict.

The Selenic Deep — Naaren faces it but doesn’t use it. Ships stay on the coastal lane. No Naaren vessel has ventured into the deep waters within living memory. Whether Naaren’s archives contain records of what was found by those who tried is a question nobody asks officially.

# VEL’ERA — Geographic Profile

Position — south/southeast of Eòs, occupying the continent’s narrowing lower extent. Borders Naaren to the north along the shared forest system — no natural dividing line, political border only. Borders Halreth to the northwest. Borders Caltheran to the north via Twilight Bastion. Southern coastline faces the Veiled Expanse. Southwestern coastline faces the Shardwater Reach.

Shape — the continent narrows here. Vel’Era doesn’t end sharply — it runs out the way old things do, with irregular southern edges, peninsulas, open sea beyond. The coastline is fragmented and organic rather than clean. Islands scatter the coast across the full southern and southwestern face.
Vel’Era’s water system:

Tyranu’s Vein — primary river running through the entire kingdom from the deep forest interior to the Halreth border at the western edge. The geographic spine of Vel’Era’s inland territory. Named for Tyranu — significance and era unknown, likely pre-queendom.

The Parisia — river running past Silvanawr. Named for Parisia Arsenia, founder of Vel’Era and first queen. The capital has been built on her name since the beginning.

The Mouth of Endurr — major lake in western Vel’Era. The name predates the queendom and its origin is ambiguous — Druidic term, primordial word, spirit name, or something else. Vel’Era’s scholars have theories. None are confirmed.

Furaha’s Fist — Vel’Era’s navy, named for the third queen who founded it. The only institution on Eòs whose name is a woman’s fist.

The Silver Forest

The defining geographic feature of Vel’Era is not a mountain, a river, or a sea. It is the forest. The silver-leaved trees are pre-Merge — their age is unknown and the question of how old they actually are is one Vel’Era’s scholars have learned not to press. They predate civilization. They predate the continent’s recorded history. They may predate the goddess herself taking physical form as the planet, or they may be the first thing she grew when she did.

The silver color is not a Chroma effect. It predates Chroma. It is the primordial substrate — Eōtria’s own pre-Merge magical essence — suffused into the wood over millennia of Druidic tending. Silver is pre-spectrum. The color of the world’s magic before Ubil’s seed shattered it into thirteen fragments. Every silver leaf is a piece of the planet’s unbroken original self still breathing.

The forest covers Vel’Era’s interior almost entirely. It thins at the coast, opens into clearings where settlements exist, and bleeds northward across the Naaren border where it continues as Naaren’s deep forest. A traveler moving south through Naaren’s woods would notice the silver-leaf species becoming more prevalent gradually — no wall, no marker, just a slow change in the character of the light filtering through the canopy.

Chroma-touched wildlife in the forest is treated as invasive by old-ways practitioners and celebrated by twelve-deity adherents. The Tarnweald Stags — enormous, antlered, mossy-hided — are primordial creatures that predate the Merge. They guard sacred groves and judge travelers’ intentions. You do not approach them with Chroma-based ritual. You bring seeds and songs.

The Druids

Unknown to the world. Known only to the highest members of Vel’Era’s government. They tend the silver trees — the male Druids ancient and rare, the female Druids created from young girls through a primordial pathogen that transforms rather than awakens. In Silvanawr one or two blend into the population as tattooed mages, mistaken for Restoration practitioners. Nobody suspects. The cover has held for thousands of years.

The Hollow Crown in Vel’Era’s forbidden zones is a corrupted Druid. What corrupted it — Order of the Wyrm, Throng contact, something older — is unresolved and should remain so.

Silvanawr

Capital built among the silver-leaved arboreal spires — structures grown from or around living wood, the city and the forest inseparable. The architecture reflects the old-ways tradition even as the twelve-deity culture operates within it. The tension between the two isn’t architectural — it’s in the institutions, the temples, the competing ritual calendars, the quiet arguments about which magic is real and which is new money dressed in old clothes.

The Islands

Islands scatter the full southern and southwestern coastline. All Vel’Eran territory. Inhabited, some more than others. House Pevlium originates on one called Aleisha’s Rest — named for a founding figure of the house. The island hosts a manor beside an endorheic lake: enclosed water, no outlet, self-contained. The lake doesn’t drain. Whatever is reflected in it stays.

The Southern Seas and the Navy

Vel’Era’s southern coast faces the Veiled Expanse directly — the breeding ground for Chroma typhoons that track north and strike Eòs. Every major arcane storm on the continent forms in those waters and hits Vel’Era’s coast first before anything else feels it. Vel’Era’s navy exists because of this. Not primarily for trade or conquest — for survival and warning.

Divination mages stationed along the coast combined with sophisticated Artifice weather-tracking technology monitor storm formation in the Veiled Expanse. When a Chroma typhoon forms, Vel’Era knows first. The navy patrols the coastal waters, maintains the warning infrastructure, and ensures that information reaches the continent’s interior before the storm does.

Whether any part of that warning system has ever detected something in the Veiled Expanse that wasn’t a storm — and what happened to the people who reported it — is a question Vel’Era’s naval records presumably contain and nobody outside the highest government levels has read.

The Culture War

Old ways versus twelve-deity tradition. Primordial versus Chroma. The old-ways practitioners are the majority and the dominant culture — they are right about the primordial magic being the world’s original foundation, they simply don’t know exactly how right they are or why. The twelve-deity tradition is newer, growing, backed by the continent’s institutional structures. The tension is the same one that played out across medieval Europe between paganism and Christianity — intimate, generational, sometimes violent, always personal.

Queen Lysandria sits at the intersection. She carries the Enchantment Riftmark — Chroma-based — in a Freyr-descended bloodline whose kingdom’s dominant culture views Chroma as secondary to the primordial. She knows about the Druids. She probably knows more about what the silver forest actually is than anyone alive outside the Druids themselves. Whether that knowledge is a burden or a weapon depends on the day.

Vel’Era’s arc — learning that reality is more complex than the spirits tell — isn’t about discovering the old ways are wrong. It’s about discovering that the old ways are more right than even their practitioners understood, and that the world built on top of that truth is sitting on something it has never fully reckoned with.

CONTINENTAL OVERVIEW

The Vespur Plains belong to Halreth, not Caltheran. The road to Caltheran runs through them but Halreth owns the territory. Caer Seryth sits at the eastern narrowing of the plains at the base of Vespine’s Cradle — its strategic position is geographic inevitability, not political choice. Whoever holds that position controls the southern mouth of every pass from Caltheran and Varkaan into the plains.

Leylines originate from beneath Ao where the Merge struck the planet. They run through the planet’s bedrock worldwide but concentrate most densely under the Vespur Plains. Leylines are attuned — smaller ones carry single pillar attunements, larger ones carry multiple or all pillars. Standing in a major leyline concentration is lethal — radiation sickness equivalent. The common belief is that the Vespur Plains are habitable because the leylines run deep enough beneath the bedrock that surface exposure stays below lethal threshold, or that something in the plains’ composition buffers the effect. Neither has been formally proven. This is what people believe, not confirmed fact.

The Ebonmaw Ocean sits above the northern coast of Eòs facing Varkaan, Caltheran, and Naaren. No ship has returned from its deeper waters. No charts exist beyond the coastal shelf. It is designated an Ocean rather than a Sea — the only body of water around Eòs with that designation — implying scale that the other seas don’t have. Direct opposite of the Palecrest Sea in character.

The Point — narrow strip of Caltheran territory running northward between Naaren to the east and Varkaan to the west, separating the two kingdoms entirely. Naaren has no shared border with Varkaan. Naaren requires Caltheran’s permission to access Varkaan territory. Known formally as The Point. Commoners call it the Cock. Both names are in active use.

The Skyplate Highlands run east to west across Varkaan’s northern territory, terminating at the Ashen Coast region on the eastern end. They are the same mountain range system as Vespine’s Cradle — continuous spine running across the upper half of Eòs. Magic-resistant terrain throughout the Skyplate range — spellcasting disrupted or impossible at higher elevations. Era of Collapse ruins throughout, including The Shell — massive buried siege engines from 0–280 A.M.

Seasonal cold front from the Ebonmaw splits annually at the Skyplate Highlands. The magic-resistant terrain means the arcane weather system cannot penetrate above it. The front deflects west toward Caltheran and east toward Naaren’s coast. Nothing crosses above the Highlands. The Buffer Zone exists in climatically isolated territory as a result.

The Singing Frost — evocation-charged seasonal event occurring around the winter solstice. Shared between Varkaan and Caltheran. Cold highland air rolling down from Varkaan carries latent evocation charge. Hits hardest along the Varkaan/Caltheran border. Fades as it pushes south. Hudria gets it badly. Aedric Spire gets a muted version. The Vespur Plains are largely sheltered behind Vespine’s Cradle.

HALRETH

The Vespur Plains — shaped like the Florida panhandle except facing west with the tip branching toward the continent’s center and massively larger. Wide mouth at the Palecrest Sea coast, narrowing eastward. The leyline concentration is deepest here. Dead zones exist near the Vel’Eran border and Shadowfen where leyline proximity is close enough to the surface to be dangerous. Broadly habitable plains elsewhere.

Vespine’s Cradle — mountain range forming the northern border of the Vespur Plains. Acts as weather barrier against Varkaan’s highland climate. Creates the chokepoint geography that made Caer Seryth’s position strategically inevitable. Name origin ambiguous — either derived from Vespur Plains or connected to a historical figure named Vespine. Scholars disagree.

Theralion City — built into and on top of marble coastal cliffs above the Palecrest Sea. The city is inseparable from its geology. Approached from the water it presents as white stone rising directly from the sea. The Renavon reaches the coast nearby. Access from the waterline to the city’s sea gates is a two-hour climb. The cliffs are the walls. The sea gates are the chokepoint.

Old Thenbury — port settlement at the base of Theralion’s cliffs. Predates the capital. The “Old” prefix means it was there before Theralion existed. Fishing, trade, dockwork, sailors, merchants. Culturally maritime rather than plains-oriented. Technically part of Theralion. Practically its own world. The people there have the quiet weathered sense of something that was there first and will be there last.

The Renavon — primary river, flows west through the Vespur Plains, empties into the Palecrest Sea near Theralion City. The economic spine of Halreth’s inland trade.

Amos’ Fingers — the Renavon’s delta, several leagues from the coast where the river spreads into multiple channels before meeting the sea. Named for Amos, deity of Judgment and Protection. Historical site of repeated military engagements between Halreth and rival kingdoms. Strategically contested because whoever controls the delta controls river access to the Halreth interior.

The Orella — secondary river running along the southern face of Vespine’s Cradle, colder and faster, feeding mill towns and fishing settlements before curving southwest to the sea.

The Chimera River — eastern river draining the marshy eastern tip of the plains. Behaves differently across its length — fast and cold in the upper stretch, slow and silty in the middle, brackish and unreliable near Shadowfen. Does not reach the sea. Dissolves into the Shadowfen marshlands. Named for what it is — a river that can’t decide what it wants to be.

Halreth’s first king saw the leylines as a weapon that would cause more harm than good if harnessed — like a nuclear deterrent. This decision became Halreth’s political identity. The kingdom that chose restraint while sitting on the continent’s most powerful arcane resource. Two centuries later most Halrethians have inherited the philosophy without fully understanding its origin.

CALTHERAN

Caltheran is the Noxus/Demacia equivalent to Varkaan — neighboring kingdoms that are mirror images of each other in the worst way. Longest contested border on the continent. Centuries of wars over the territory between them, particularly the northwestern coastal strip where the Restoration leyline current makes the land fertile.

Aedric Spire sits dead center in Caltheran. Not positioned for trade or water access — positioned for arcane access. The Echoa Archive sits on the most magically saturated ground in Caltheran where leyline branches from the Vespur Plains push northward. Athens is the cultural model. A civilization that made knowledge its primary currency and built its seat of power on sacred ground. Cultural inspiration: ancient Greece.

Glintgate is in southern Caltheran near Vespine’s Cradle — first major city travelers hit coming through the passes from Halreth. The Aldren originates near the Cradle here. Not a capital — the commercial engine doing the economic work Aedric Spire doesn’t bother with.

Hudria — major city on the Caltheran/Varkaan border originally belonging to Varkaan, now Caltheran territory following border shifts. Original keep of Uthred and Johanna Halvyr. Site of the War of Copper Kings, 1013 A.M. House Farryn governs it. Sits a few leagues south of Caltheran’s northern coast on the Aldren. Near the edge where the Restoration leyline current’s influence fades into less leyline-touched territory.

Marisport on the northern coast — Caltheran’s only maritime access, facing the Ebonmaw. Not a thriving port. Nobody sails the Ebonmaw beyond the coastal shelf. Exists primarily for coastal trade between the three northern kingdoms. Has the specific character of a port city that knows the sea it faces is not going anywhere good.

Caltheran’s northern coastline is jagged, ugly, and almost uninhabitable. Sandwiched between Varkaan’s luscious northwestern coast and Naaren’s geologically strange eastern bend. The worst piece of northern coastline on the continent. The historical wars over Hudria weren’t about Caltheran’s coast — they were about position and the Restoration leyline current’s edge.

The Aldren — river originating near Vespine’s Cradle, running northwest through Caltheran past Glintgate, crossing into Varkaan, emptying into the Ebonmaw. The Old Road follows its corridor north through ancient Varkaan/Caltheran battlegrounds. Gravewrens haunt its middle stretch where the killing has been thickest across centuries. Named before the current border configuration existed — predates both kingdoms’ claims on it.

The Singing Frost hits Hudria badly. Aedric Spire gets a muted version. The Vespur Plains are sheltered behind Vespine’s Cradle.

VARKAAN

Scale — half of Canada. The largest single kingdom on Eòs by considerable margin. Governing it requires systems, not just force. Cultural inspiration: Ottoman Empire with Norse surface layer.

Druhmir Hold sits at the plateau edge where highland terrain descends into the interior — not in the harshest highland territory, not on the most fertile coast, but at the threshold between worlds. Ottoman Constantinople parallel. The Iron Guilds sitting there control the flow of goods, ore, and people between Varkaan’s radically different internal regions. Everything passes through the threshold city.

The Sol’telarī — arid interior plateau and desert region produced by the highland rain shadow effect. Name derived from German “taler,” Arabic “ṭalari,” and “Sol” meaning sun and one of the gods. Arabic encoded. Contains a black sand region — House Al-Khem’s territory, named for volcanic mineral deposits. River Kýe runs through the desert, named by Iskari settlers before the kingdom existed.

Iskari substrate — Varkaan’s nomadic desert population descended from pre-kingdom Iskari settlers who were there before civilizations claimed the area. Iskari cultural influence embedded throughout the Sol’telarī. House Al-Khem’s name derives from this heritage — Al is Arabic definite article, Khem is ancient Egyptian for black land. Multiple historical territorial wars. Varkaan was built partially on contested land. The official history does not reflect this accurately.

Varkaan’s highland interior is riddled with large lakes and hundreds of smaller ones fed by snowmelt. Abundant freshwater. Fishing culture alongside mining culture in highland settlements.

Varkaan’s northwestern coast is luscious and fertile — site of centuries of wars with Caltheran. The fertility comes from a moderate Restoration leyline current running through the area. The border wars aren’t just about land. They’re about a leyline branch that neither kingdom has ever formally identified as the actual source of the contested territory’s value.

The Buffer Zone — northwesternmost Varkaan past the Skyplate Highlands. Uninhabited wilderness. Era of Collapse ruins throughout. The Marshall’s keep sits on the Ebonmaw coastline — Order of the Wyrm connected, not yet written. Climatically isolated by the Skyplate Highland magic resistance deflecting the annual cold front.

NAAREN

Cultural inspiration: Byzantine Empire. Republic in name, effective autocracy in practice. High Chancellor holds personal executive authority through constitutional forms that everyone observes in form and nobody respects in practice. The Cipher’s Guild predates and outlasts individual Chancellors — it’s the institutional backbone, not the Chancellor.

Genetic magic control policy is legislation embedded in republican law, not a monarch’s decree. Harder to dismantle because it isn’t one person’s will.

Keth’var Bastion sits on sorcerer-altered elevated ground — the terrain itself was transmuted by early post-Merge mages during the Era of Collapse. The Myrn runs under the city through the transmuted terrain. All commerce funnels through the lower city at the river passage. Total economic control through geographic chokepoint. The sorcery that altered the ground dates to 0–280 A.M. The original method is either lost or archived somewhere in Keth’var.

The Myrn — primary river, splits Naaren roughly in half, runs under Keth’var Bastion, terminates in a saltwater inland lake near the eastern coast. The Chimera River feeds the marshland system from the west and connects to the same terminal lake. Why the lake is saltwater is unknown and actively disputed — possible Selenic Deep underground connection, historical marine incursion, or volcanic ring water table influence.

Naaren’s eastern coastline is Hawaiian in character — dramatic volcanic-formed dark rock meeting the Selenic Deep. Ships hug the coast in established trade lanes rather than venturing into the Deep. Nightshade Port positioned to surveil every vessel moving through the lane — it’s an intelligence node, not primarily a trade port.

Northeastern volcanic zone — past the Ashen Coast’s grey bluffs, the ground starts steaming. Hot springs, geysers, some toxic vents. Unique fauna evolved around geothermal heat rather than solar warmth. The cold front deflecting east from the Skyplate Highlands annually collides with this geothermal activity — cold air meeting ground heat, persistent thermal fog, mineral-glinting grey ash sand. DIRECTOR’S NOTES — THE TWO MOONS
New Canon Established — Not Yet in Project Files

Eōtria has two moons. Named Luuna and Sol after the gods.

One moon is intact and normal. One is broken — its pieces visibly floating away from the main body, observable from the ground with the naked eye. Think Elder Scrolls moons — the fragmentation is that visible and that dramatic. The broken moon shines more brightly than the intact one despite being shattered.

The broken moon’s greater brightness is unexplained by conventional understanding. A destroyed thing giving more light than a whole one. Nobody in the general population has a satisfying answer for this. The Iskari religion does.

Iskari religious doctrine holds that the broken moon is the body of one of the old gods — a dead divine being whose remains hang in the sky. The brightness is the god’s essence still radiating even in death. Whether this is literally true in the canon’s cosmology is unresolved and should remain so — it is what the Iskari believe, not confirmed fact.

The mythology takes inspiration from Norse cosmology’s wolves — Sköll and Hati, the wolves who chase the sun and moon across the sky. In Eōtria’s version this framework applies to both moons. Two celestial bodies, one intact, one destroyed, both named after gods. The wolf mythology implies pursuit, inevitability, and eventual consumption — the intact moon being chased toward the same fate as the broken one is a reading available to anyone who knows the myth.

Writing implications — standing rule:

Every night scene must account for both moons. The broken moon shines brighter, meaning nights in Eōtria are lit differently than a single-moon world. The light from the broken moon is not clean — it comes from a fragmented source, meaning it produces unusual shadow patterns where the pieces scatter light at slightly different angles. A full night scene with both moons visible is a specific visual that no Earth-based reader has seen. Use it deliberately.

The cultural weight of the broken moon varies by kingdom and tradition. Iskar treats it as sacred remains. Other kingdoms may treat it as omen, as myth, as simple astronomy, or as something they have stopped looking at because looking at it raises questions nobody wants to answer. Vel’Era’s old-ways tradition almost certainly has a primordial interpretation that predates the Iskari religious framing. What Vel’Era’s Druids know about the broken moon is an open thread.

The broken moon’s pieces floating visibly away from the main body means the fragmentation is ongoing — or at least appears ongoing from the ground. Whether the moon is actively breaking apart further or whether it broke catastrophically at a fixed point in history and what’s visible is simply the settled aftermath is unknown in-world and should remain ambiguous.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

## Vel’Era:
QUEENSWICK
Southwest coast of Vel’Era, Shardwater Reach approach

Queenswick began as a fortified harbor installation under Furaha, third queen of Vel’Era, commissioned to give Furaha’s Fist a permanent home on the southwestern coast. The military installation came first — breakwaters cut into the rocky coastline, drydocks carved from the cliffside, barracks built above the tideline where the Shardwater’s spray couldn’t rot the timber. The civilian settlement followed within a generation, as shipwrights, provisioners, rope-makers, and the families of naval personnel built permanent lives around the fleet’s needs.

The name derived colloquially — sailors and dockworkers calling it the Queen’s port, the Queen’s wick, until the shorthand formalized. No single woman is honored by the name. The queen it implies is Furaha herself, which means Queenswick and Furaha’s Fist are twin monuments to the same monarch — the port and the navy inseparable in name and function.

The military installation and civilian town share the harbor. The navy owns the western docks and the fortified inner basin. The civilian settlement occupies the eastern slopes above the water, growing up the coastal cliff face in terraced rows. The two communities are functionally distinct but economically bound — the town feeds and supplies the fleet, the fleet is the reason the town exists. Tension between naval command and civilian governance is structural and ongoing.

Queenswick is the closest inhabited point to the Shardwater Reach’s outer reefs. Experienced navigators live here. So do people who have lost family to the reefs.

TYRANU’S WATCH
Interior Vel’Era, boundary edge of the Hollow Crown’s forbidden zone

Founded by Tyranu, the first Silver Warden of Vel’Era, as a permanent boundary post against the entity now known in folk legend as the Hollow Crown. What began as a warden outpost — a handful of trained operatives maintaining a perimeter — became a permanent settlement across generations, as the practical reality of indefinite containment required infrastructure, supply chains, and eventually families.

The town takes its name from its founder. Colloquially residents call it the Watch, and that name carries its own weight — every person born here understands that the settlement exists because something must be watched.

The public knowledge: the zone beyond the Watch’s boundary is cursed ground. The stories disagree on what happened there. Common versions involve a queen who made a bargain she couldn’t keep, or a battle that tainted the soil, or simply that the old forest remembers something the living have forgotten. Children are told not to go near the boundary markers. They are not told why beyond that. The markers themselves are carved stone posts, old, worn, reset periodically by people the town sees leave at dawn and return by midday.

The private knowledge, held by senior Silver Wardens and transmitted only to the crown: the Hollow Crown is a corrupted Druid. It does not sleep. It does not leave willingly. Without the Watch’s active containment, it would roam.

The Silver Wardens are a crown institution — they answer to the monarchy of Vel’Era directly, bypassing noble houses and civic governance entirely. Their authority at the Watch is absolute. They recruit from across the world, selecting candidates for disposition rather than origin, and train them at the Watch itself before assigning postings. Most Wardens do not discuss what they do in specific terms. The order’s existence is not secret — Vel’Era’s government acknowledges them — but their purpose is not publicly detailed. To outsiders they read as a specialized crown guard. That misreading is maintained deliberately.

Tyranu herself is remembered differently by different audiences. To the public she is a figure from old history, vague enough to be mythologized. To the Silver Wardens she is the institution’s founding logic — the reason the order exists and the standard against which every Warden measures their own tenure. Tyranu’s Vein, Vel’Era’s primary river, carries her name. The kingdom’s lifeblood named for the woman who kept something catastrophic contained. That honor was not accidental.

THE QUNTHREN — THE GHUVMI ISLANDS
Southern and southwestern archipelago of Vel’Era

Eleven islands scattered across the southern and southwestern coastal approach to Vel’Era. Official designation: the Qunthren. Colloquial name, dominant in common usage across all four continents: the Ghuvmi Islands, named for the explorer and settler Ghuvmi, a woman of the third era who charted the archipelago and established the first permanent settlements across its habitable landmasses.

Sovereignty: eight islands held by Vel’Era, including Aleisha’s Rest. One island in the northeastern cluster belongs to Naaren. Two islands in the western reach belong to Halreth. The borders are old, established by treaty rather than conquest, and generally stable — though Vel’Era’s naval dominance through Furaha’s Fist means the distinction between Vel’Era’s islands and the others is enforced more firmly than the others can reciprocate.

Most islands are inhabited. The Shardwater Reach’s southwestern islands are the exception — shattered reefs, jagged tides, and siren lore make permanent settlement impractical on the outermost rocks, though temporary fishing camps exist on the larger ones during favorable seasons.

The Qunthren’s strategic importance is economic before it is military. The primary trade routes between Aethryn, Iskar, and Eòs pass through or near the archipelago. Goods moving between continents move through Ghuvmi waters. Vel’Era does not tax passage aggressively — the kingdom understands that choking the route would invite coalition pressure it doesn’t need. But the option to close or complicate those routes is a standing diplomatic instrument, and every kingdom trading with Aethryn or Iskar understands that Vel’Era holds that lever.

Furaha’s Fist patrols the Qunthren. Queenswick is the naval hub from which that patrol operates. The civilian populations of the inhabited islands are Vel’Era’s, culturally and politically, with the Naaren and Halreth islands maintaining their own governance under their respective crowns — small enough that the arrangement is manageable, politically sensitive enough that it has never fully normalized.

Ghuvmi herself has no monument in the archipelago. She has the name of eleven islands. That was considered sufficient.

## Caltheran:

AEDRIC SPIRE — Updated Entry
Center of Caltheran, Echoa Archive

Aedric Spire was built for position and arcane access, not trade or water. The Echoa Archive sits on the most magically saturated ground in Caltheran — where leyline branches from the Vespur Plains push northward through the kingdom’s interior and concentrate beneath this specific patch of earth. The city didn’t grow here because it made practical sense. It grew here because the Council’s predecessors understood what was beneath the ground and built their seat of power on top of it. Athens is the model. Sacred ground first. City second.

The original walled Spire is still there — the Archive at its center, the Council’s sanctum operating from somewhere inside it that doesn’t appear on public maps. The Spire’s walls are old enough that the city has long since outgrown them. What surrounds them now is two centuries of organic expansion that has calcified into its own distinct urban identity — the lower city, which the residents of both halves simply call the Outer.

The Outer grew the way cities grow when nobody planned them: provisioners first, then housing for provisioners, then the institutions that follow where housing goes. Research stations and observatories ring the outer walls, positioned for clear sightlines and arcane access to the leyline currents that pulse beneath the ground. Libraries and minor academies cluster in the northeastern quarter, the ambient magical saturation useful for certain preservation and study work. The Artisan’s Quay runs along the western stretch of the Outer — magically lit, open arcane craftsmanship on public display, the kind of cultural density that hits a first-time arrival like a physical thing.

The divide between upper and lower isn’t formally acknowledged but everyone operates by it. The walled Spire is Council territory — institutions, old money, the political machinery of Caltheran’s oligarchy. The Outer is where the city actually lives. The relationship between them is the specific tension of a place where power is centralized and prosperity is diffuse. The Outer feeds the Spire. The Spire governs the Outer. Neither is fully comfortable with the arrangement.

Known locations within: the Echoa Archive, the Gilded Compass inn (Outer, western quarter near the Artisan’s Quay), the Council’s secret sanctum inside the Archive itself.

Northern Caltheran, Aldren River, near Varkaan border. House Farryn governing seat.

Hudria is older than Caltheran’s current political structure. It was Varkaan’s first. Then it was contested. Then it changed hands enough times that the city’s identity split down the middle and never fully healed — the Varkaan half still in the architecture, the northern quarter’s stonework, the military infrastructure that predates any Caltheran administration. The Caltheran half layered over it in the subsequent centuries like a second skin that fits imperfectly.

It is the original keep of Uthred and Johanna Halvyr — the physical site where the founders of Johanna’s Promise began. That weight is in the stones. The current governing house, House Farryn, holds the seat because Han Farryn was the last man standing at the end of the War of Copper Kings in 1013 A.M. Not because he was the most capable of the seven claimants. Because the other six died. Salyon Pevlium taught that discrepancy to a lecture hall and called it history’s shape. It is also Hudria’s shape.

The Aldren runs through the city’s eastern edge before continuing north toward the Ebonmaw. The river here runs fast and cold — highland melt coming down from the Varkaan ranges. The Singing Frost hits Hudria badly at the winter solstice, the evocation charge rolling down from the Varkaan highlands finding no geographic shelter here the way it finds Vespine’s Cradle further south.

The fallen Caltheran houses — Blackwood, Stonehaven, the others at the ENAA — do not consider House Farryn legitimate. Farryn governs the most historically significant city in Caltheran and shares none of their displacement. That grievance runs quietly under every diplomatic interaction between the diaspora houses and the city’s administration.

LENTORUM
Southern Aldren corridor, between Glintgate and Aedric Spire

Lentorum predates the Echoa Archive and possibly predates Caltheran’s current governing structure in its recognizable form. It is a river city in the oldest sense — built where the Aldren widens into a navigable section before narrowing again toward Glintgate’s estuary, positioned because the water was there first and everything else followed.

The city’s character is accumulation rather than design. Centuries of slow growth have produced something that works without being elegant — markets that started as single stalls and expanded into permanent districts, administrative buildings constructed over the foundations of whatever came before them, a bridge across the Aldren that has been rebuilt four times and carries the architectural evidence of all four versions simultaneously.

Lentorum is where the Aldren’s commercial traffic pauses between Glintgate’s runewright industry and the capital’s political machinery. Merchants who need to move goods between the two stop here. Information moves the same way. The city is not politically significant in itself but is functionally indispensable, which is a different kind of power that Lentorum’s older merchant families understand clearly and the Council in Aedric Spire finds slightly annoying.

NUVOLIA
Northern Aldren corridor, between Aedric Spire and Hudria, near the contested border zone

Nuvolia exists because the road between Aedric Spire and Hudria is too long for a single march and the terrain too open to leave ungoverned. It began as a military waystation on the Aldren’s northern run and became a city the way waystations on contested roads always do — soldiers need provisioning, provisioners need housing, housing needs administration, and administration needs walls.

The military infrastructure is still visible beneath the civic surface. The street grid in Nuvolia’s oldest quarter is garrison-planned — wide enough for formation movement, sightlines maintained between main thoroughfares, the central square positioned for rapid assembly. The city has grown past those origins but not away from them. Every generation or two the border with Varkaan produces enough friction that Nuvolia’s military character becomes relevant again and the Council is reminded why it sits where it does.

The Singing Frost reaches Nuvolia at reduced intensity — far enough south from the highland source that the evocation charge has partially dissipated, but present enough that the winter solstice carries a specific atmospheric quality locals have built small ritual observances around. Not Caltheran’s most magically significant city. Strategically, one of its most important.

BLACKWOOD PASS — Canon Note
Vespine’s Cradle, southern Caltheran, origin of House Blackwood

Blackwood Pass cuts through Vespine’s Cradle on the road between Caer Seryth and Glintgate. The trees in and around the pass are noticeably dark — enormous specimens that predate the road entirely, their canopy dense enough that midday light through the pass arrives grey and filtered. The darkness is the pass’s name and its reputation.

No civic settlement exists at the pass mouth. This is Blackwood territory and has been since before the house migrated its political branch to Aedric Spire. The traditional Blackwoods who remained are nature-bound, primal magic practitioners whose attunement runs stronger and less refined than their urban counterparts — raw where the Spire branch is precise, instinctive where Morgan Blackwood is deliberate. The two branches know each other exist. The relationship is not warm.

The pass sees regular travel on the Caer Seryth to Glintgate road. The Blackwood presence is felt rather than announced. Travelers who know the history move through without stopping. Travelers who don’t know the history sometimes notice they are being watched from the treeline and correctly conclude that stopping would be inadvisable.

## Halreth:
THERALION CITY — Updated Entry
Western coast, Palecrest Sea, marble cliffs

Halreth’s capital is inseparable from its geology. Theralion is built into and on top of marble coastal cliffs above the Palecrest Sea — approached from the water it presents as white stone rising directly from the sea face, the city and the cliffs the same material in different states of arrangement. The Renavon reaches the coast nearby, its delta infrastructure at Amos’ Fingers feeding the port below. Access from the waterline to the city’s sea gates is a two-hour climb. The cliffs are the walls. The sea gates are the chokepoint.

The Temple of Equanimity sits at the city’s highest point — the ideological center of Halreth’s pacifist tradition made architectural. The seat of King Thelranius Remnus IV. A marble city on a marble cliff above a luminous sea, governed by a young king who believes in what the kingdom is supposed to be and is still learning what it actually is.

Old Thenbury sits at the base of the cliffs below — see separate entry. House Llyr’s defensive navy operates from the harbor infrastructure Old Thenbury and Theralion share between them, the fleet positioned on a sea Halreth considers spiritually significant rather than militarily threatening.

OLD THENBURY — Canon Entry
Base of Theralion’s cliffs, Palecrest Sea harbor

Thenbury predates Theralion. The port settlement was here first — fishing, trade, dockwork, the particular culture of people who live at sea level while a capital city looks down at them from the cliffs. Technically part of Theralion. Practically its own world, with its own rhythms, its own social hierarchy, and the maritime indifference of something that was there first and knows it.

House Llyr’s defensive navy quarters here alongside the commercial fleet. The harbor is Old Thenbury’s infrastructure even when Theralion claims it administratively. The Palecrest Sea’s calm luminous water makes this a different kind of port than Queenswick or Nightshade — no aggression in the positioning, no surveillance function. Ships go out, ships come back, the sea cooperates and Halreth is grateful for it.

The two-hour climb to Theralion’s sea gates means Old Thenbury and the capital communicate by messenger rather than proximity. News from the sea reaches Old Thenbury first. How quickly it travels up the cliff depends on who received it and what it contains.

CAER NELHOLLM & NEELIA
Amos’ Fingers, Renavon delta, western Halreth coast

The Renavon spreads into multiple channels several leagues from the coast before meeting the Palecrest Sea. The delta is called Amos’ Fingers — named for the deity of Judgment and Protection, which is either a statement about the god’s presence here or a record of how many times this ground has needed judging and protecting. Both readings are accurate.

Caer Nelhollm is the keep that controls the delta’s primary channel — the fortified installation that whoever holds the interior of Halreth needs to hold. It has changed hands in the past. The history of repeated military engagements that the geography invited is visible in the keep’s construction — layers of repair, different stone in different sections, the architectural record of sieges survived and reconstructions completed. It is not elegant. It is standing.

Neelia is the church settlement grown up beside the keep over generations. Where Caer Nelhollm is military function, Neelia is the community that a sacred site accumulates — pilgrims who stopped at the god’s named ground and didn’t leave, clergy maintaining the delta shrines, the families of the garrison who built homes rather than returning elsewhere. The church that anchors Neelia is dedicated to Amos directly. The congregation understands they are living at a chokepoint and has built their theology around the idea that protection requires presence.

The two communities share the delta without fully merging. The keep’s garrison and the church settlement have different relationships with who comes through the Fingers and why.

AMBERMILL
Mid-plains Renavon, commercial heart of the Vespur Plains interior

The Renavon runs through Ambermill’s center and the city was built to process what the plains produce. Mills — grain mills, lumber mills, the specific industrial infrastructure of a kingdom whose economic power is agricultural — line both banks through the city’s working quarter. The amber color that gives the city its name comes from the grain dust that settles on everything during harvest season, the air itself taking on a warm particulate glow that locals consider beautiful and visitors consider unusual.

Ambermill is the largest inland settlement after Theralion. Everything that moves between the coast and the eastern frontier passes through or near it — goods going west toward the capital, travelers going east toward Johanna’s Promise, the Chroma-Hares that Halreth exports as magical weather indicators bred and traded here in significant numbers. The Runelight Rain falls regularly over Ambermill and the locals have built practical traditions around it — certain agricultural work scheduled around heavy Runelight nights, the grass’s faint luminescence after a strong fall treated as a positive indicator for the following day’s weather.

House Arbor has a commercial presence in Ambermill connecting Greatwood timber operations to the mill infrastructure. The exact relationship between the city’s independent merchant class and House Arbor’s interests is one of those productive tensions that older cities carry without fully resolving.

RENAFORD
Eastern Vespur Plains frontier, Renavon crossing, near the leyline surface zone

Renaford exists because the Renavon has to be crossed somewhere and this is where the crossing is safe. East of Renaford the river becomes unreliable — faster, stranger, the water behavior shifting as the leyline concentration beneath the bedrock pushes closer to the surface. The ford that gave the town its name is the last predictable crossing before the eastern frontier becomes genuinely difficult terrain.

The town knows what it is. Frontier settlements always do. The Runelight Rain here runs longer than it should after heavy falls — the grass holds its glow past dawn on strong nights, and the Chroma-Hares that live on the eastern plain have the most vivid fur-color shifts on the continent, which scholars from Aedric Spire occasionally travel to observe and the locals find faintly tiresome. The leylines are close enough to the surface that certain sensitive individuals feel them as a low persistent pressure behind the eyes. Nobody talks about this in formal company.

The population skews toward people with specific reasons to live at the edge of the safe country — rangers, frontier traders, the families of those who work the Shadowfen border, and a small but permanent community of scholars who study the leyline surface activity despite the Caltheran monopoly on that research. Halreth doesn’t fund the research. It also doesn’t prohibit it.

House Thorne — Halreth’s martial house, responsible for guarding the kingdom’s dangerous borders — maintains a small permanent garrison at Renaford. The garrison is understated for a pacifist kingdom’s frontier post. Whether it is sufficient is a question that hasn’t been tested yet in the current era.

CANON LOCKED — Religion of Eōtria, Kingdom by Kingdom

HALRETH

Halreth’s relationship with the thirteen is the most institutionally developed on Eòs and the most deliberately equalized. The Temple of Equanimity in Theralion is the continent’s largest multi-deity temple — all thirteen patron deities honored under one roof, each with a dedicated altar, each attended by its own order of clergy who operate within the temple’s shared structure. No patron takes precedence. The architecture enforces this — the central nave is neutral ground, the thirteen chapels radiating from it in equal proportion.

This is Halreth’s founding ideology made physical. The first king’s deliberate choice not to weaponize the leylines beneath the Vespur Plains extended to religion — you don’t go to war over gods any more than you go to war over magical advantages you’ve chosen not to exploit. The Temple of Equanimity is both a house of worship and a diplomatic instrument. Pilgrims from every kingdom find their patron deity honored here. That welcome is not incidental.

Individual deity temples exist throughout Halreth beyond Theralion. Uma — Restoration, healing — has the largest independent following outside the capital, her temples embedded in farming communities across the Vespur Plains where healing and seasonal renewal are daily practical concerns. Amos holds particular significance at Caer Nelhollm and Neelia — the delta keep and church settlement are explicitly Amos’s territory, the god of Judgment and Protection honored at the kingdom’s most strategically contested chokepoint.

The Vak’tirī have almost no presence in Halreth. The old faith didn’t survive Halreth’s agricultural civilization intact. Small communities in the eastern frontier near Renaford occasionally maintain practices that scholars identify as Vak’tirī adjacent — probably absorbed from Vel’Era’s border populations over generations — but it isn’t organized or self-identified as old faith worship.

VEL’ERA

Vel’Era is the only kingdom on Eòs where the old faith and the new faith exist in explicit, acknowledged tension rather than one simply having replaced the other. The old-ways tradition — primordial, pre-queendom, connected to the silver forest and the planet-goddess herself — is the majority cultural practice. The twelve-deity Chroma tradition is newer, growing, institutionally backed by the continent’s political structures, and considered by old-ways practitioners to be secondary to what the land itself remembers.

What most old-ways practitioners don’t know — and what the Druids do know — is that their tradition isn’t just older than the twelve-deity system. It predates the Merge. The silver forest is primordial substrate. The old-ways are worship of Eōtria the planet-goddess in her actual form, practiced by people who understand her as a spiritual presence without understanding the cosmological mechanics of what she is. They are more right than they know. The twelve-deity practitioners are not wrong — the thirteen are real forces — but they are worshipping downstream effects rather than the source.

The Vak’tirī have genuine presence in Vel’Era, maintained through the old-ways tradition. Asthœl specifically — the god of Collapse whose shattered body is the broken moon — is the most widely recognized Vak’tirī figure in Vel’Era. Old-ways practitioners read the broken moon as sacred and sorrowful simultaneously. It is not worshipped as actively as the planet-goddess’s presence in the forest, but it is named and acknowledged in ways that other kingdoms don’t maintain.

The Druids hold the deepest knowledge. They understand the Vak’tirī as the cycle that preceded the world’s current form. They do not share this. The Silver Wardens are aware the Druids exist. Whether the Silver Wardens understand the full theological weight of what the Druids maintain is a separate question.

NAAREN

Naaren’s relationship with the thirteen is functional rather than devotional. The republic’s institutional structure leaves no room for religious authority that competes with the Cipher’s Guild’s reach or the Chancellor’s personal power. Temples to the thirteen exist in Keth’var and Zenrenban — particularly Luuna, deity of Illusion and Secrets, whose following in a surveillance state is both ironic and predictable — but clergy operate under the same quiet observation as everything else in Naaren. A temple that becomes too politically significant finds its congregation audited.

The most genuinely practiced religion in Naaren is private rather than institutional. Individual household worship of patron deities — small altars, personal observance, the calendar followed as cultural practice rather than theological commitment. The Cipher’s Guild has no official religion but Luuna’s iconography appears informally throughout its facilities in ways nobody officially acknowledges.

The Vak’tirī are suppressed rather than forgotten in Naaren. The old faith’s presence along the Vel’Era border — where the silver forest’s northern extension pushes into southern Naaren — is treated as a cultural relic that doesn’t require active persecution, just consistent discouragement. The genetic magic control program has theological implications that nobody in the republic’s official apparatus wants a functional religious tradition examining too closely.

The open question flagged in the worldbuilding files: Miraen Vos carries silver hair, which marks a bloodline connected to Eōtria herself. Whether this connection has any relationship to the old faith traditions suppressed within her own republic is an unresolved thread that should remain unresolved.

CALTHERAN

Caltheran’s religious landscape is defined by a conflict that has been running for at least two generations. The Council of Twelve treats Chroma as the divine object — not the thirteen patron deities, but the magic itself, the raw arcane force the Merge released into the world. The Echoa Archive is their temple. Arcane mastery is their theology. The Wardens don’t pray to Galvinus or Amos. They study. Study is the worship.

The independent temples disagree. Caltheran’s clergy — particularly those dedicated to Amos and Terra — maintain that the patron deities are real beings with genuine moral authority, that the Archive’s arcane theology is hubris dressed as scholarship, and that a kingdom governed by twelve men who worship their own knowledge is a kingdom without genuine divine accountability. This argument has been running in Caltheran’s public squares and private courts for decades. The Council has been systematically discrediting, defunding, and occasionally disappearing the more vocal clergy. The temples have been documenting this.

The fallen Caltheran noble houses at the ENAA carry this conflict with them. Several are explicitly aligned with the independent temple tradition — House Norunlight as keepers of history and lore have institutional memory of what the Council has done to religious opposition. The tension between House Blackwood’s political ambitions and the temple tradition is less clean — Morgana Blackwood operates in exactly the kind of morally ambiguous space that makes clean religious allegiance inconvenient.

Chronicle’s Rest — near the Vel’Era border, connected to the Chronomancy Rune corridor — is the most significant religious site in Caltheran that the Council has neither fully controlled nor been able to discredit. Its desertion is officially attributed to scholarly abandonment. The temples have a different account that they share carefully and quietly.

The Vak’tirī have no organized presence in Caltheran. The Council would find the old faith theologically inconvenient — a cosmological framework that predates and supersedes the Chroma the Council worships is not something the Archive wants in circulation.

VARKAAN

Varkaan’s religious practice splits cleanly along its internal cultural divide. The highland Varkaan population — military, Iron Guild, the political class that runs Druhmir Hold — maintains a martial relationship with the thirteen. Amos is the dominant patron of the warrior class. Galvinus for the craftsmen and guild infrastructure. The temples in Druhmir Hold and the major highland settlements are functional, well-funded, and closely connected to guild authority rather than independent clergy.

The Sol’telarī Iskari substrate population maintains the old faith alongside or instead of the thirteen. This is the most significant Vak’tirī worship on Eòs outside Iskar itself. Kye’vathn is the center of it — the seasonal convergence of nomadic tribes produces the largest regular gathering of old faith practitioners on the continent. The Vak’tirī here are not abstract — they are named, storied, present in daily practice. Vorrâ and Asthœl are the most widely invoked in the Sol’telarī. Upheaval is a concept the desert understands. The broken moon is visible from the open black sand plains with a clarity that settled populations under forest cover or behind city walls don’t experience.

The hidden orders maintaining Vak’tirī knowledge operate most openly in the Sol’telarī. House Al-Khem’s ancient lineage almost certainly has keepers embedded within it — a house that predates Varkaan by enough generations to have preserved what Varkaan tried to absorb.

The Windreavers on the ancient battleground corridor are not Vak’tirī but they are old — storm-spirits, sky-gods, beings between earth and heaven that the Varkaan military tradition treats with the specific respect of people who have learned the hard way what disrespect costs. Whether the Windreavers predate the Merge, and what their relationship to the Vak’tirī cycle is, remains unresolved and should stay that way.

JOHANNA’S PROMISE

The imperial capital has every tradition operating simultaneously within its walls. Two centuries of unification have produced a city where a pilgrim can attend a Temple of Uma in the morning, pass an Iskari household shrine to Asthœl at noon, and hear a Caltheran academic argue that all of it is downstream of Chroma by evening. The ENAA’s student body alone represents every kingdom’s tradition — the Iskari delegation, House Arsenia’s Vel’Era old-ways adjacent practices, the Caltheran diaspora houses carrying their temples’ institutional memory.

The official religious position of the High King is non-committal in the way that absolute political power allows — the Halvyr royal line honors the full pantheon without elevated allegiance to any specific patron, which keeps every kingdom’s clergy satisfied enough to remain quiet. Brunor’s founding ideology was paradise for all men, not paradise for the followers of one god.

CONTINENT-WIDE RELIGION SUMMARY:

The dominant faith across Eòs is worship of the thirteen patron deities through individual temple structures — no unified church, each kingdom’s practice shaped by its culture and political structure. Halreth is the most religiously balanced. Naaren is the most surveilled. Caltheran is the most actively contested. Varkaan is the most internally divided. Vel’Era is the only kingdom where old faith and new faith coexist in named tension.

The Vak’tirī old faith survives most strongly in: the Sol’telarī of Varkaan, Vel’Era’s old-ways tradition, and the Iskari communities on the Ashen Coast and Naaren’s borders. On the continent of Iskar itself it is the dominant religion. On Aethryn it persists in specific scholarly and spiritual communities.

The cosmological truth — Vaïve and Zât as the dead First Gods, the eight Vak’tirī completing the cycle, Eōtria and Solsyrr as the still-present divine beings, Ubil as Vaïve’s product and the source of all Chroma — is known in full by almost nobody alive. The Druids of Vel’Era hold the deepest portion of it. Certain Iskari hidden orders hold fragments. The rest of the world worships the downstream effects of a dead god’s entropy and calls it faith.

The new gods are real but not comprehended bythe the world, and they rarely manifest as physical presences in the world or take on an avatar. The Stranger isn’t a metaphor for death. He is the patron of the Necromancy pillar made present. The thirteen exist as genuine entities who can and do appear in the material world, not merely as theological constructs. 

## Achievements:

HALRETH — ACHIEVEMENTS

AGRICULTURE & FOOD

Halreth feeds a significant portion of Eòs. This is the kingdom’s most consequential achievement and its most deliberate one — the Vespur Plains’ exceptional fertility isn’t an accident of geography that Halreth stumbled into. It was a choice. The first king understood what the leyline concentration beneath the plains could do if weaponized. He chose not to weaponize it. The agricultural surplus that resulted is what two centuries of Halreth diplomacy has been built on. You don’t go to war with the kingdom that feeds you. Halreth has known this since its founding and has never stopped knowing it.

The Vespur Plains produce grain, livestock, and orchard fruit in quantities no other kingdom on Eòs can match. Halreth’s grain goes north to Varkaan and east to Naaren. Its livestock — particularly the Windcattle whose bone and fat conduct enchantments — are traded continent-wide. The Chroma-Hares bred on the eastern plains near Renaford are exported as magical weather indicators, the most reliable arcane forecasting tool available to mages working without formal Divination training.

The leyline-touched soil produces specific agricultural anomalies that Halreth farmers have built practice around rather than studied academically. Crops planted during Runelight Rain germinate faster. Certain grain varieties grown near the leyline surface zone in the eastern plains develop faint luminescence at night that locals consider a quality indicator and outsiders find unsettling. The Orella’s mill towns along Vespine’s Cradle’s southern face process grain from the northern plains edge where the Cradle’s shadow produces cooler, distinct growing conditions — a different crop profile than the open plains.

How it’s honored: The Harvest Vow is Halreth’s most significant agricultural festival — an annual event where farmers, merchants, and the crown formally acknowledge the land’s yield. In its traditional form it includes offerings to Terra, patron of Life and Transmutation, at local temples and a public accounting of the year’s surplus administered by House Arbor’s stewards in coordination with crown representatives. The Greatwood’s timber is not harvested without ceremony — House Arbor maintains the ritual calendar that governs when cutting is permissible and what must be replanted in return.

CULTURAL FESTIVALS & TRADITIONS

Paxville exists specifically to host the Peace Weeks — Halreth’s most significant cultural institution. The Peace Weeks are debates, spectacle, and public discourse held annually, drawing participants from every kingdom. Halreth doesn’t have an army that frightens its neighbors into behavior. It has a festival that gives them a reason to talk instead of fight. The Peace Weeks are soft power in its most concentrated form.

The festival structure: formal debate on contested political questions between kingdom representatives, public oratory competitions, theatrical performances commissioned and judged by House Serene’s cultural committee, and the specific tradition of the Open Table — a feast where any participant regardless of kingdom or rank may sit and be heard on any grievance, with House Quillan’s barristers present to record what is said. Nothing said at the Open Table is legally binding. Nothing said at the Open Table is ever forgotten by the people who recorded it.

House Serene’s salons operate year-round in Theralion and function as the cultural engine between Peace Weeks. The finest poets, musicians, and painters on the continent rotate through Halreth specifically because House Serene pays well and asks only for the work. No political commissions. No propaganda. The salon tradition is one of the few spaces in Eòs where art exists for its own purposes rather than institutional ones. This has made Halreth’s cultural output disproportionately influential relative to its military weight.

House Weaver’s enchanted textiles are one of Halreth’s most widely distributed cultural exports — robes imbued with warmth, resilience, or minor glamour worn by nobility across every kingdom. The weaving tradition predates the house’s ennoblement. The craftsmanship is old. The magic in the cloth is applied through techniques the house guards carefully.

How it’s honored — CORRECTED: The Peace Weeks are constitutionally protected — no Halreth monarch can cancel them unilaterally. They ran continuously from their founding until the Kymancy War, when they were suspended for the full duration of the conflict — approximately fifty years. Every person alive at their restoration in 1036 A.M. had grown up in a world without them. The generation that remembered them was dead. Their restoration at the war’s end was one of the founding acts of the Age of Peace and carries that weight to the present day. A festival that had to be rediscovered rather than simply resumed.

ARTS & ARCHITECTURE

Theralion City is Halreth’s most significant architectural achievement — white marble rising from sea cliffs, the geology inseparable from the construction. The city didn’t impose itself on the landscape. It grew from it. This is the aesthetic principle that runs through Halreth’s built environment: work with what the land offers rather than against it. The Temple of Equanimity at Theralion’s highest point is the fullest expression of this — the interior designed so that light from the Palecrest Sea enters through western windows at specific hours and illuminates different deity chapels at different times of day. The building manages time and meaning simultaneously.

The Vyrglass Serpent — the translucent leviathan said to inhabit Lake Vyr — appears on Halreth’s royal crest. Halreth chose as its heraldic symbol not a weapon, not a crown, not a martial beast, but a creature of uncertain existence associated with truth, clarity, and potential madness. That choice says something specific about what Halreth’s founders considered worth representing.

House Azure’s sea-sagas and cartographic art document the Palecrest Sea coast in detail no other record matches, even the cartography guilds in Naaren. The collaboration between Halreth’s maritime culture in Old Thenbury and Vel’Era’s literary tradition has produced the continent’s most accurate coastal records, maintained at Bridgestone Pavilion’s floating archives.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

CANON LOCKED — Vel’Era Achievements

LITERATURE & ORAL TRADITION

Vel’Era’s most significant cultural achievement isn’t a monument or an institution — it’s a body of recorded memory that no other kingdom on Eòs can match in depth or age. The sea-sagas are the most visible expression of this. House Azure produces the continent’s finest cartographic chronicles — not maps alone, but narrative documents that embed the coastline’s history into the geographic record. A House Azure sea-saga doesn’t just describe where the reefs are. It tells you who died on them, why, what was salvaged, and what the water looked like in the hour before. The distinction between literature and cartography doesn’t exist in Vel’Era the way it exists elsewhere.

The old-ways oral tradition runs beneath and alongside the written record. The Druids hold the deepest layer — cosmological memory that predates the queendom, predates the silver forest’s documentation, predates the Merge itself. That knowledge isn’t written because writing it would require explaining it, and explanation requires a context that most readers don’t have. The old-ways practitioners below the Druidic tier maintain a different layer — ancestral story, ritual instruction, the kind of knowledge that lives in recitation rather than text. The Glade of Echoes exists specifically for this — the whispering forest enclave where ancestral voices are preserved in the wood itself.

The tension between written literature and oral tradition maps directly onto the culture war between the twelve-deity Chroma tradition and the old ways. The Chroma tradition favors written texts — codified theology, published scholarship, institutional libraries. The old ways treat writing as insufficient for certain categories of knowledge. Neither is wrong. The knowledge they each hold is genuinely different in kind.

How it’s honored: The naming convention itself is the deepest honoring of literary tradition — Vel’Era names everything after people, specifically women. Rivers, the navy, institutions. The landscape is a library. Tyranu’s Vein isn’t a geographical feature with a person’s name attached to it. It is the record of Tyranu, carried forward in the daily act of calling the river what it is. A cartographer from another kingdom sees a river. A Vel’Eran sees a name they’ve spoken since childhood, and knows — or ought to know — what that name means.

TRADE

Vel’Era’s trade position is structural rather than entrepreneurial. The Qunthren — the Ghuvmi Islands — sit at the intersection of every major trade route between Aethryn, Iskar, and Eòs. Goods moving between continents pass through or near Ghuvmi waters. Vel’Era doesn’t manufacture the continent’s most sought commodities. It sits between the people who do.

House Coralwind’s enchanted coral alchemy is the kingdom’s most distinctive trade export — potions and arcane focuses derived from marine life, coveted across every kingdom. The cultivation and processing techniques are proprietary. House Coralwind has maintained that advantage for generations. The sunstone quarries controlled by House Solara supply the naval navigation and arcane construct industries — sunstone is a component in Furaha’s Fist’s weather-tracking technology and in the Great Lighthouses maintained by House Spire along Vel’Era’s treacherous southern coastline.

The Great Lighthouses are worth naming as an achievement in themselves. House Spire maintains massive arcane beacons along the southern coast — the first line of detection for Chroma typhoons forming in the Veiled Expanse, and the navigational infrastructure that makes the Qunthren route viable for heavy cargo vessels in difficult conditions. Without the lighthouses, the southern trade corridor closes. Vel’Era built that infrastructure and maintains it at considerable cost. The trade routes passing through the Qunthren exist partly because House Spire makes them safe enough to use.

Furaha’s Fist’s Divination mage and Artifice weather-tracking network is the secondary infrastructure — it detects typhoon formation before it reaches the lighthouses’ visual range. The combination of arcane early warning and physical navigation beacons means Vel’Era has the most sophisticated maritime safety system on the planet, operated by a navy named for offense rather than defense. The kingdom that gives the continent its storm warnings is also the kingdom whose navy is named a fist.

How it’s honored: Trade achievement in Vel’Era is honored through the naming of ships rather than festivals. Vel’Eran vessels carry the names of women from the queendom’s history — naval engagements, merchants who opened new routes, cartographers whose maps made a passage viable. The fleet is a floating record. House Tideborne manages the hereditary admiralty with the understanding that the names on the hulls are obligations as much as honors.

ARTS & ARCHITECTURE

Silvanawr is the achievement. The capital built among the silver-leaved arboreal spires — city and forest inseparable, the architecture grown from or around living wood in ways that the old-ways tradition considers a continuation of the primordial substrate rather than an imposition on it. The twelve-deity practitioners who operate within the city see beautiful arcane construction. The old-ways practitioners see the forest accepting the city rather than tolerating it.

The original Brokilon quality — ancient, impenetrable, deeply primordial — is what Vel’Era was before civilization layered over it. Silvanawr is what happened when the civilization grew slowly enough and carefully enough that the primordial didn’t reject it. The tension between what the forest was and what the city became is visible in the architecture’s contradictions — Chroma-touched crystal alongside uncut living wood, formal temple structures beside groves that nobody has touched in centuries because touching them felt inadvisable.

House Azure’s paintings represent the continent’s finest realistic coastal documentation. Not impressionistic, not idealized — what the Shardwater Reach actually looks like at noon in autumn when the reefs break the surface. What the Mouth of Endurr’s fog looks like at dawn. The technical precision serves the documentary function. It’s art in the service of record, which Vel’Era considers a higher calling than art in the service of beauty.

CANON LOCKED — Caltheran Achievements

ARCANE INNOVATION & SCHOLARSHIP

The Echoa Archive is Caltheran’s defining achievement and the continent’s most significant academic institution outside the ENAA itself. Built on the most magically saturated ground in Caltheran where leyline branches push northward through the kingdom’s interior, it sits at the intersection of position and purpose — the right building in the right place, doing the right work. Or it was. The Archive’s original function was preservation and active research. What the Council of Twelve has made of it in the current era — a sanctum for arcane theology, a containment site for dangerous specimens, a political instrument — is the corruption of a genuine achievement.

The runewright tradition operating out of Glintgate is Caltheran’s most practically significant contribution to Eòs. Every kingdom uses rune infrastructure — warding systems, arcane construction, the Abjuration defenses built around major cities. Caltheran’s runewright guilds trained the practitioners who built most of it. The craft crossed the Selenic Deep with the Aethryn refugees 500 years ago and was refined on leyline-saturated ground for generations until it became something distinct from its Aethryn origins. Caltheran runewrights are not simply practicing imported technique. They are practicing a tradition that adapted to a specific magical environment and developed accordingly.

The Artisan’s Quay in Aedric Spire is the public face of this — open displays of arcane craftsmanship, magically lit, a city that treats innovation as spectacle rather than concealment. Aeraa Wynvale’s reaction arriving at Glintgate captures what outsiders experience: overwhelming sensory shock. A world of dazzling civilization. The gap between Caltheran’s public arcane culture and what most of Eòs is accustomed to is jarring. That gap is itself an achievement.

Runehold Abbey as a clifftop monastic scriptorium is the secondary institution — where rune theory is preserved, debated, and transmitted in scholarly rather than commercial form. The monks who work there are not entirely comfortable with the Council’s direction of the Archive. That tension is generational and ongoing.

How it’s honored: The runewright guilds maintain an apprenticeship system that is one of the most rigorous on the continent. Mastery certification from a Caltheran guild carries weight in every kingdom — it implies not just technical competence but a specific theoretical grounding in leyline interaction that practitioners from other traditions don’t share. The guilds honor their lineage by maintaining that standard even as the Council tries to subordinate them to arcane theology. That resistance is itself a form of honoring.

CANON LOCKED — Naaren Achievements

ARCANE INNOVATION — APPLIED INTELLIGENCE

Where Caltheran’s arcane achievement is scholarship and craft, Naaren’s is application and infrastructure. The distinction matters. Caltheran builds knowledge. Naaren builds systems that deploy it.

House Cogswright’s arcane automatons guard the republic and operate its cities — mechanical Artifice constructs at a scale no other kingdom on Eòs has matched. This is not ornamental. The automaton infrastructure is Naaren’s answer to the manpower problem created by a surveillance state that needs to watch everything simultaneously. Guards don’t sleep. Guards don’t talk. Guards don’t develop inconvenient loyalties. The automaton tradition is a political technology as much as a magical one.

Keth’var Bastion itself is an engineering achievement — sorcerer-altered elevated ground, the Myrn running beneath it, a chokepoint that gives the capital total economic control over Naaren’s interior trade. The ground alteration dates to the Era of Collapse and the method is either lost or archived and not discussed. Building a capital on transformed terrain, with a river routed beneath it, is infrastructure on a scale that required institutional knowledge, sustained effort, and a willingness to reshape the land itself as a political instrument.

The Crystalline University in Zenrenban produces theoretical magical research that feeds into the republic’s intelligence apparatus — though most scholars working there believe it feeds into academic journals. The gap between what the research is used for and what researchers think it’s used for is itself an achievement of institutional design. Naaren built a university that functions as an intelligence asset while maintaining the appearance of pure scholarship. That requires sustained, sophisticated management that has held for generations.

How it’s honored: Naaren honors achievement through classification rather than celebration. A significant development in automaton design doesn’t get a parade. It gets integrated into the republic’s infrastructure, documented in the relevant guild archive, and assigned a security level. The Cipher’s Guild maintains records of Naaren’s most significant intelligence and arcane achievements in a registry that is itself classified. Most Naaren citizens have no idea what their kingdom has actually accomplished. This is considered appropriate.

CANON LOCKED — Varkaan Achievements

MILITARY & ENGINEERING

Varkaan’s most significant achievement is one it didn’t build — it endured. The Skyplate Highlands running east to west across the kingdom’s northern territory are magic-resistant terrain containing Era of Collapse ruins including The Shell — massive buried siege engines from the earliest post-Merge period. Varkaan didn’t create the Skyplate Highlands. But it built a civilization on terrain that would have destroyed a less militarily disciplined culture, and it used that terrain as both a defensive asset and a weather system that shapes the entire upper continent.

The Iron Guilds are the genuine achievement. Druhmir Hold as the seat of the Iron Guilds is not just a political designation — it means Varkaan controls the most sophisticated weapons and armor manufacturing infrastructure on Eòs. House Joric’s forge heritage, the soulsteel production at the Emberforge Village, the stormstone extraction at Greyhall Mines, etherium and rune-core metal at Ironvein — this is a vertically integrated military-industrial complex that produces materials no other kingdom can fully replicate. The Iron Guilds’ certification system means Varkaan-forged equipment carries a quality guarantee that is backed by generations of consistent craft.

The highland lake district provides the freshwater abundance that makes this scale of industrial production sustainable — hundreds of lakes fed by snowmelt, fishing culture alongside mining culture, a water supply that doesn’t depend on river access the way every other kingdom’s production infrastructure does. This is a geographic advantage Varkaan has built into its industrial design rather than taking for granted.

Caer Torund surviving intact on the Ebonmaw coastline — maintained, garrisoned, operational — while the surrounding Buffer Zone is uninhabited ruins is a military achievement of a specific kind. The keep hasn’t been attacked. It’s been held against an atmosphere. Whatever the northern exposure produces in the people stationed there, the institution has kept functioning anyway. That’s a particular category of endurance.

Arts — Secondary:

Varkaan’s artistic tradition is martial in aesthetic and memorial in function. The battle-carved reliefs in Druhmir Hold’s older structures record engagements in stone rather than text — a tradition that predates the Iron Guilds and reflects a culture that trusted permanence over prose. Sol’telarī Iskari artistic tradition runs in parallel — bone charms, sand-pattern ritual art, the oral traditions of nomadic tribes that Kye’vathn preserves in its seasonal convergences. The two traditions don’t synthesize. They coexist with the specific tension of a kingdom that built itself on top of someone else’s land and has never fully reckoned with that.

How it’s honored: The Iron Guilds’ oath-swearing at Ironcross is the most significant ritual of achievement-honoring in Varkaan. A new master craftsman doesn’t just complete an apprenticeship. They travel to Ironcross and swear. The oath is specific — to the craft, to the guild, to the standard. Not to the king. Not to Druhmir Hold. To the work itself. That distinction has political implications that periodically become uncomfortable for Varkaan’s ruling class and the guilds have never changed it.

Achievements Pass — Complete across all five kingdoms.

## Politics

CANON LOCKED — Halreth Political Profile

GOVERNMENT STRUCTURE

Halreth is a divine monarchy in the Egyptian model. The Remnus dynasty has held the seat for centuries without interruption and shows no sign of relinquishing it. This is not because Halreth lacks capable noble houses. It is because the houses were never designed to challenge the crown — they were designed to serve it. House Arbor manages agricultural infrastructure. House Serene manages culture. House Quillan manages law. House Weaver manages craft. House Azure manages maritime record and cartography. Each holds genuine expertise and genuine institutional authority within their domain. None holds political weight outside it. The distinction matters. They are the machinery of the kingdom. The crown is the hand that operates it.

This is not feudal. Feudal systems distribute power downward through land and military obligation and periodically discover that the barons have more swords than the king. Halreth’s model concentrates power upward through function. The houses administer. They do not govern. Thelranius Remnus IV sits at the apex of a system that has never seriously threatened to topple him because it was not built to.

THE CROWN’S REAL POWER

There are three layers to Halreth’s power and most kingdoms only see the first one.

The first is food. Halreth feeds a significant portion of Eòs. The Vespur Plains produce exceptional yield. You do not go to war with the kingdom that feeds you, and you do not apply serious diplomatic pressure to it either, because the calculation of what happens to your civilian population if the grain shipments slow is too uncomfortable to complete. Halreth has never weaponised this openly. It has never needed to. The threat doesn’t require articulation. Every crown in Eòs already knows what it means.

The second is the Peace Weeks. Paxville hosts them annually. Every kingdom sends representatives. The Open Table puts every grievance on record. House Quillan’s barristers write it down. Nothing said is legally binding. Everything said is remembered. Halreth doesn’t need to act on that information in real time. The accumulated record of what every kingdom’s representatives have said across generations of Open Tables, combined with what they chose not to say, is a political intelligence archive of extraordinary depth. That the Peace Weeks were constitutionally locked into law — unable to be cancelled by any future monarch unilaterally — is not a constraint on the crown. It is the crown protecting its most sophisticated instrument from its own potential future weakness.

The third layer is known only to the reigning monarch.

THE LEYLINE SECRET

The Vespur Plains sit above the deepest concentration of leyline activity on Eòs. The founding king understood what that meant. He made a choice — not weakness, a deliberate decision — that harnessing a continental weapon would cost more than it could ever buy. That reasoning is recorded. The current king has read it.

What passes from monarch to monarch, sealed from every other living person including the queen of Halreth, is this: the leylines beneath the plains can be accessed. The spells this access enables are not available to any other kingdom on Eòs. The Caltheran Council with all its arcane theology doesn’t know this is possible. Naaren’s Crystalline University hasn’t theorized it. The Echoa Archive has no record of it. It is the most significant military secret on the continent and it has never been used because every Remnus monarch who has inherited it has made the same choice as the first king — that a weapon of that magnitude, once deployed, changes what Halreth is permanently.

Thelranius Remnus IV knows. He sits with it. He presents to the continent as a diplomatic man with anxious fingers at summit tables, and that presentation is not entirely false — he genuinely prefers the Peace Weeks to the alternative. But there is a difference between preferring peace and being incapable of war. The other crowns have never correctly understood which kind of peaceful Halreth is.

THEA REMNUS AT THE ENAA

Thelranius sent a Remnus bloodline heir to the Eos National Arcane Academy in 1239 A.M. The continent reads this as Halreth honoring a diplomatic tradition, participating in the continent’s premier institution, perhaps seeking alliance connections for a young royal.

That reading is incomplete. A Remnus inside the ENAA is Halreth’s eyes at the most politically charged arcane institution on the continent, in neutral territory, during a period of surface peace that Thelranius is experienced enough to know is not stable. Whatever Thea learns, observes, and reports — intentionally or through the normal operation of a sharp young woman navigating institutional politics — goes back to the crown. Not as espionage. As the ordinary function of a dynasty that has survived centuries by understanding its environment before its environment understands it.

Thea may or may not know the leyline secret yet. That knowledge passes at coronation, or at the moment the current monarch judges the heir ready for it. Whether Thelranius has made that judgment about his daughter is between the two of them.

EXTERNAL RELATIONSHIPS

Johanna’s Promise: Halreth funds it. The Promise sits at the dead center of Eòs at the base of Vespine’s Cradle, commanding the southern mouth of the only viable passes from Varkaan and Caltheran into the Vespur Plains. Funding the Promise is not charity. It is the most cost-effective border security purchase on the continent.

Caltheran: The relationship is functionally dependent and diplomatically cool. Halreth needs Caltheran’s runewrights. Caltheran needs Halreth’s grain. Neither particularly likes the other. The border through Vespine’s Cradle is stable because destabilising it is expensive for both parties.

Naaren: Historically contentious over the Shadowfen and the Chimera River corridor. The leyline surface activity near the Halreth-Naaren border is a dormant problem. Naaren has never formally claimed Shadowfen. Halreth has never formally ceded it. The marshland sits between them and both prefer it that way.

Varkaan: Distant and wary. The Singing Frost reminds Halreth annually that its northern neighbor commands weather systems. Halreth reminds Varkaan annually through trade that the Iron Guilds eat Vespur grain.

Vel’Era: The warmest relationship among the five kingdoms. House Azure and Vel’Era’s literary tradition have collaborated for generations. The sea-sagas. The coastal record. The floating archives at Bridgestone Pavilion. Two kingdoms that value record-keeping and long memory find natural common ground.

FAULT LINES

The knowledge gap between the crown and the queen is the most structurally dangerous element in Halreth’s internal politics. Not because the current queen is a threat — but because a secret that size, held by one person, is one death away from being lost. If Thelranius dies before passing the knowledge on, Halreth’s most significant strategic asset becomes inaccessible until someone rediscovers it, if they ever do. Every Remnus monarch has presumably solved this problem. The solution is worth knowing.

The Peace Weeks’ constitutional protection cuts both ways. No monarch can cancel them. But the agenda of who participates, who gets the floor, and what the Open Table records is controlled by the crown through House Quillan. The protection is genuine. The neutrality of the administration is not required to be.

The founding king’s reasoning is preserved. But it is one document, in one place, passed to one person at a time. If a Remnus monarch ever reads it and disagrees — decides the weapon is worth using — the entire political identity Halreth has built across centuries rests on a single heir’s judgment call.

CANON LOCKED — Vel’Era Political Profile (Complete)

GOVERNMENT STRUCTURE

Vel’Era is a queendom. Not a kingdom with a female ruler — a queendom, a distinction the Arsenia dynasty has maintained since Parisia, the first queen, founded it. The title is constitutional. Male succession has never occurred and the mechanisms for it don’t exist in Vel’Era’s founding documents. Whether this was deliberate philosophy or practical circumstance on Parisia’s part, the result is a political culture where the crown and the concept of female authority are inseparable at an institutional level.

The noble houses operate beneath the monarchy in functional rather than political roles. House Tideborne holds the hereditary admiralty of Furaha’s Fist. House Spire maintains the Great Lighthouses. House Coralwind controls the alchemy trade. House Azure manages cartographic and literary record. House Solara controls the sunstone quarries. House Shanty, newest and lowest, holds its title through demonstrated naval loyalty. None of these houses check the crown. They serve the domains the crown assigns them and derive their status from performing those functions well.

The Silver Wardens answer to the monarchy directly, bypassing noble houses and civic governance entirely. They are the crown’s most sensitive instrument — their public purpose is the containment of whatever inhabits the forbidden zone near Tyranu’s Watch, their actual scope is considerably broader. A crown institution that recruits for disposition rather than origin, trains in isolation, and doesn’t explain itself to the houses is an intelligence and enforcement capability that exists outside the normal channels of governance.

Vel’Era is matriarchal in the specific sense that its power structure has never successfully been challenged along gender lines because the challenge would require dismantling the founding architecture of the state itself.

THE RIFTMARK AND SUCCESSION

The Enchantment Riftmark passes within the Arsenia bloodline at the bearer’s death. Eōtria decides the attunement. The mark cannot be voluntarily transferred.

Queen Lysandria Arsenia V holds it now. Elara does not.

This is the single most significant political fact in Vel’Era and nobody discusses it openly. The mark is not merely a magical inheritance — in Vel’Era’s religious and political tradition, it is the visible sign of divine sanction. The queen who bears the Enchantment mark rules with something the other crowns can only claim through bloodline legitimacy or military force. Lysandria V rules with proof. The gods, or the planet itself, chose her house. The mark says so.

Elara attends the ENAA in 1239 A.M. as heir to a queendom whose defining symbol of legitimate rule is biologically inaccessible to her until her mother dies. Both women know this. The court knows this. The other kingdoms know this. Nobody says it because saying it plainly would be either an act of treason or an act of cruelty depending on who said it and to whom.

What this produces in practice: Lysandria V’s political authority is effectively absolute for as long as she lives. Not because she is tyrannical — she is a queen who knows more than she reveals, a woman who traces map borders carefully and speaks in implications. But because the structural condition of her rule means no internal faction can build a credible challenge around Elara without appearing to wish the queen dead. The mark is protection Lysandria didn’t ask for and cannot put down.

Elara at the ENAA is a long position. She is being educated in the broader political world she will one day rule. What she learns there — about the other kingdoms, about the ENAA’s internal factions, about the continent’s fault lines — returns to Silvanawr eventually. Whether Elara understands herself as an intelligence asset or simply as a student is a question her mother has probably not answered directly.

THE CULTURE WAR

Old-ways versus twelve-deity tradition. Primordial versus Chroma. This is Vel’Era’s persistent internal fault line and it runs beneath every institutional relationship in the kingdom.

The old-ways tradition holds the deep knowledge — cosmological memory predating the queendom, the Druids at its root, oral transmission that the written record cannot contain. The twelve-deity Chroma tradition favors codified theology, institutional libraries, published scholarship. Both are real. Both serve genuine functions. The tension between them is not which is true — both are true — but which has the authority to define Vel’Era’s identity going forward.

Lysandria V holds both simultaneously, the way the crown must. The silver-leaved arboreal spires of Silvanawr hold both simultaneously, Chroma-touched crystal alongside uncut living wood that nobody has touched in centuries because touching it felt inadvisable. The tension is not resolved. It is managed, and the management is part of what the crown does.

THE MORRANDIS QUESTION — CORRECTED

Lysandria Arsenia II has met Elaine Morrandis. Once. At the tournament hosted at Caer Seryth. She waited for the right moment — after a personal disruption had knocked Elaine’s composure sideways — and sat beside her at the royal viewing stand and made her offer directly.

Political marriage. For Elaine or Phoebe. The framing was reunion, family, blood calling to blood. The mechanism was absorption — House Morrandis folded back into House Arsenia, the surviving heirs placed in the Vel’Eran succession line, Lysandria’s heir problem solved with two women who were already proven capable.

Elaine cited her oath to Johanna’s Promise and declined.

The conversation ended with a veiled threat about the precarity of a mercenary’s life.

The stalemate that followed is not neutral. It is the specific tension of a refused acquisition, held between two women who both understood exactly what had been said. Lysandria knows Elaine’s capabilities, her position at the Promise, her Riftmark status — Naaren’s delegation made that public before the summit. She also knows the refusal was principled rather than hostile, which is the kind of information a queen files carefully.

Whether Lysandria has made a second approach, whether the marriage proposal has been revised or withdrawn, whether the veiled threat has been actioned through channels Elaine hasn’t identified yet — these are open questions. What is not open: the distance the original entry implied does not exist. These two women have sat beside each other. The queen made her move. The commander said no. Everything since has been downstream of that afternoon.

By Book 6, the Morrandis line is the royal line of Vel’Era — Elara Arsenia is a Morrandis descendant through Phoebe. The house destroyed in a political purge on Vel’Eran soil became the throne. The world has a specific sense of humor about this.

HOUSE PEVLIUM

House Pevlium occupies a position in Vel’Era’s power structure that standard categories don’t fully capture. One of the most powerful and famous mage bloodlines in the queendom — a Pevlium wing exists in the Grand Library, the grandmother of the current generation held three arcane pillars simultaneously, and a Pevlium uncle sits on the Vel’Eran arcane council in an advisory capacity that gives the house direct institutional reach into the queen’s most significant governing body.

Their base is Aleisha’s Rest — a fog-covered island off Vel’Era’s coast, named for the house’s founding figure. The island hosts a manor beside an endorheic lake: enclosed water, no outlet, self-contained. The geography mirrors something about how House Pevlium operates — substantial, somewhat removed from the mainland’s institutional politics, self-sufficient in a way that island aristocracy develops when it has enough resources to not need the continent constantly.

They are major traders. Island positioning gives House Pevlium natural maritime commercial access, and a house with that level of arcane expertise running trading operations has specific advantages in an economy where arcane certification adds premium to goods and services.

Salyon Trivani Pevlium holds a ducal title — the highest non-royal noble rank in Vel’Era’s hierarchy. The family sent Kai to be warded at House Harringold in Johanna’s Promise at age eleven. A house that holds court sway, advisory position on the arcane council, and a ducal title places a child at the continent’s political center with a specific calculation about what that child will become.

In Vel’Era’s internal power picture, House Pevlium sits in a tier of its own — less immediately wealthy than House Solara in liquid terms, more institutionally powerful in court terms, and more arcane-prestige-rich than any other house except the Arsenia royal line itself.

EXTERNAL RELATIONSHIPS

Halreth: The warmest bilateral relationship on the continent. House Azure and Vel’Era’s literary tradition. The sea-sagas. The floating archives at Bridgestone Pavilion. Two kingdoms that value long memory and careful record. The warmth is genuine. It is also useful, and both crowns understand that.

Naaren: The forest border with no natural dividing line — political boundary only, the silver trees bleeding northward into Naaren’s deep woodland without asking permission. Relations are cool. Vel’Era’s spiritual tradition and Naaren’s genetic control program represent incompatible philosophies of what magic is and who gets to have it. The border holds because neither kingdom wants the cost of the alternative.

Caltheran: Formally correct, functionally competitive. The Council of Twelve’s claim that the Echoa Archive represents divine inheritance grates against Vel’Era’s old-ways tradition, which holds the deeper cosmological memory and knows the claim is false. The Twilight Bastion border has held. The ideological competition has not resolved.

The Promise: Vel’Era participated in the summit that produced nothing. The ink here is fading. Maps are expensive to redraw. Lysandria told Uthred she would not spend political capital on a threat that hadn’t materialized within her borders. She was telling the truth about her position.

FAULT LINES

The succession gap is permanent until Lysandria V dies. Any faction that wants Elara to rule sooner than nature allows is structurally incentivized toward regicide, which means Lysandria’s security apparatus treats everyone with that interest as a potential threat regardless of stated loyalty.

The Morrandis survivors represented a dormant succession complication in Book 1. By Book 6 the Morrandis line is the throne. That resolution is itself a fault line — a house that was supposed to be destroyed now rules the queendom. Whoever ordered the original purge, and whatever their reasons, has not had those reasons addressed. They have simply been outlasted.

The Hollow Crown in the forbidden zone is a corrupted Druid. What corrupted it — Order of the Wyrm, Throng contact, something older — is unresolved and should remain so. The Silver Wardens contain it. If containment ever fails, it fails on Vel’Era’s interior, not its coast.

CANON LOCKED — Varkaan Political Profile

GOVERNMENT STRUCTURE

Varkaan is a militaristic monarchy — the largest kingdom on Eòs by a considerable margin, governing a territory the size of half a continent through a combination of crown authority, noble house hierarchy, and the Iron Guilds. The crown sits at Druhmir Hold. The aristocracy beneath it is stratified and martial: House Daruun at the apex, House Tal’kor in permanent rivalry with them, House Joric holding indispensable industrial power, lesser houses beneath them managing territory, extraction, and warfare.

The kingdom’s political character is shaped by what it was built on. Varkaan did not inherit stable ground. It built a civilization on the Skyplate Highlands — magic-resistant, climatically brutal, geologically ancient — and on the Sol’telarī desert interior where Iskari nomads had lived for centuries before a kingdom was declared over their heads. The result is a state that has never fully resolved the question of who Varkaan actually belongs to, and has managed that unresolved question through force for as long as it has existed.

THE IRON GUILDS

The crown’s most significant structural problem is one it inherited and cannot solve.

The Iron Guilds swear their founding oath to the craft. Not to the king. Not to Druhmir Hold. To the work itself. This distinction has political implications that every Varkaan monarch has periodically found uncomfortable and none has successfully changed.

The Guilds’ economic power is not symbolic. They control the most sophisticated weapons and armor manufacturing infrastructure on Eòs. Every kingdom’s military runs on Varkaan-forged equipment. The soulsteel production at the Emberforge Village, the stormstone at Greyhall Mines, the etherium and rune-core metal at Ironvein — this is a vertically integrated supply chain that produces materials no other kingdom can fully replicate. Varkaan’s currency and material base flow through Guild infrastructure to a degree the crown cannot simply absorb or redirect.

Previous kings have attempted subordination. They failed. The Guilds’ political leverage, Artifice technology, and protective warding systems make direct confrontation prohibitively costly. The crown has learned to tolerate the oath because the alternative is a conflict it cannot win against an institution it cannot function without.

What this produces in practice: Varkaan’s military is the most powerful on the continent and the crown does not fully control the industrial base that sustains it. A king who wants to go to war needs the Guilds cooperative. A Guild that disagrees with a campaign can apply friction at the supply level without ever drawing a blade. The current unnamed king understands this. Every king understands it by the time they’ve held the seat long enough.

HALVRIC DARUUN — THE BOOK 1 KING

Halvric Daruun was forty years old during the events of Book 1 — tall, velvet-clad, brilliant mind, questionable methods. Former ally of Uthred Halvyr. Attuned to Evocation. His royal armor was ebony steel said to be impenetrable. His crown was golden, five-pronged, a half-cage at the front.

He is dead by 1239 A.M. What his reign produced — the relationships, the precedents, the decisions that became policy — informs the political inheritance the current king sits on. The Varkaan that exists in Book 6 is partly Halvric’s architecture. The character of that architecture: brilliant and expedient. The combination produced results and left problems downstream.

THE CURRENT KING — UNNAMED

The king who holds Varkaan’s throne in 1239 A.M. has not yet been named. What is established about his political situation:

He is attempting to get his council to agree to a purge of the Iskari people.

This is the most significant active political crisis in Varkaan and one of the most significant on the continent, whether the other kingdoms are aware of it yet or not. The Iskari inhabit a substantial portion of Varkaan’s interior — the Sol’telarī desert, the River Kýe corridor, Kye’vathn as their cultural convergence point. Their presence predates the kingdom. Their traditions are not Varkaan traditions and they have never been absorbed into Varkaan’s political identity — two cultures coexisting with the specific tension of a kingdom that built itself on top of someone else’s land and has never fully reckoned with that.

The purge attempt is not yet canon-confirmed as having succeeded or failed. What is established: the king is seeking council agreement, which means he does not yet have it, which means Varkaan’s aristocracy is divided on the question. That division is itself significant. A council fractured over whether to commit atrocity is a council that cannot present unified political will on anything else either.

If the purge proceeds: the Sol’telarī becomes an insurgency zone. Iskari refugees move south through Caltheran and west toward Halreth. The Kymancy rift corridor near Shadowfen — already arcane-hostile — receives a population of displaced people with no political protection. The continent notices. The Order of the Wyrm, which has operated quietly in Varkaan’s ungoverned northern margins, gains a destabilized kingdom to work within.

If the purge fails at council: the king’s authority is publicly damaged. House Daruun, which commands Varkaan’s military forces, will have been forced to declare a position on the question. Whatever that position is, it has consequences for the Daruun-crown relationship and for Valerius Daruun sitting at the ENAA, who is now heir to a house that voted on whether to exterminate a people.

CANON LOCKED — Caltheran Political Profile

GOVERNMENT STRUCTURE

Caltheran is an oligarchy. The Council of Twelve governs from a secret sanctum within the Echoa Archive in Aedric Spire. Twelve Wardens and an Archwarden — the Archwarden as primary voice, final say in practice though not in theory. The Twelve present unity to the continent: identical Caltheran robes, coordinated public positions, a face of collective authority. Behind that face the usual mechanics of institutional power operate — alliances, resentments, competing agendas, and the specific dynamic of eleven people who believe something completely that their Archwarden knows is false.

The government’s legitimacy rests entirely on its theological claim. Caltheran’s authority derives not from military force, not from hereditary bloodline, not from democratic mandate — but from the assertion that the Echoa Archive and the runewright tradition represent divine inheritance. That the gods of Chroma chose Caltheran as steward of arcane knowledge. That the Council of Twelve administers sacred trust.

This claim is the foundation of everything. Which means it is also the crack running through everything.

THE ARCHIVE AND THE LIE

The Echoa Archive was built on the most magically saturated ground in Caltheran — leyline branches from the Vespur Plains pushing northward through the kingdom’s interior. It was built by Aethryn refugees who arrived around 400 A.M. after being turned away by Naaren, carrying with them the runewright tradition developed over generations on Aethryn soil. That tradition was roughly five hundred years old when it arrived on Eòs. It was refugee knowledge. Survival knowledge. The accumulated craft of a displaced people rebuilding in new ground.

The Council of Twelve calls it divine inheritance.

Eleven of the Twelve believe this. They were inducted into an institution that taught them the theology, surrounded them with the Archive’s accumulated weight, and gave them the specific confidence of people whose entire social reality confirms what they were told is true. The belief is genuine. That makes it more dangerous, not less — eleven people who will defend the foundation without knowing what they’re actually defending.

Rhen Tauvor, Archwarden during the events of Book 1, knows. How he came to know, whether it was discovered or inherited from a prior Archwarden as deliberate institutional secret, is unresolved. What is established: he holds the real history and has not shared it. He declared the Wyrshade a blessing and a puzzle to be studied. He ordered its vivisection. A man who knows his institution’s legitimacy is constructed fraud and is still capable of that decision is a specific category of dangerous — not a true believer making a catastrophic error of hubris, but a pragmatist who has decided the lie is worth maintaining and acts accordingly.

By 1239 A.M. Rhen Tauvor is gone. A new Archwarden holds the seat. What that Archwarden has been told about the Archive’s real history — whether the knowledge passed to them as it apparently passed to Tauvor — is not yet established. What is established is what they are doing with the Neorxn question.

The true royal line of Caltheran is House Neorxn. They were believed extinct. They are not. Wilhiem is the last heir — Riftmarked of the Spiral Pillar, aware of his status, carrying it as a burden and a secret, his life organized around protecting Jenova Halvyr and waiting for the moment his birthright becomes actionable.

Historical references to House Neorxn’s significance deprioritized, contextualized as minor legacy, allowed to fade. The name not scrubbed violently — that would leave evidence of scrubbing — but made to seem irrelevant through accumulated institutional neglect. Miraen Vos is an Order asset running a kingdom. The Caltheran Archwarden is an Order asset running a theological state. The continent’s most significant arcane institution continues under Order-adjacent management. And Wilhiem — Riftmarked of Creation itself, the Spiral Pillar, the rune most connected to the Great Pillar of Magic — cannot rally anyone to his name because his name has been made to mean nothing.

This is not improvised. Someone understood exactly what Wilhiem represents and planned accordingly.

Both maintain oath structures that predate and outlast the political arrangements around them. The Caltheran runewright guilds swear to the craft and to the standard. Not to the Archwarden.

WHAT IT IS

Johanna’s Promise is a sovereign imperial capital city-state. It began as a ruined fortress claimed by two hundred exhausted veterans who had nowhere else to go. That origin is still in its bones. THE CHANCELLOR — MECHANICS

The genetic magic control program is the clearest expression of how this works. It is legislation. It is embedded in republican law. THE GENETIC PROGRAM — POLITICAL DIMENSIONS

She is the variable the Order has been tracking for two centuries and has not yet eliminated. That they have not done so is either a decision or a failure. The distinction matters.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
## Economics:
CANON LOCKED — Eòs Economics: Continental Overview

THE CURRENCY SYSTEM

The High Kingdom mints the continental standard. Three denominations, all bearing arcane certification marks applied by the merchant guilds alongside the High Kingdom brand — the dual authentication is what makes counterfeiting prohibitively difficult and what gives the currency its cross-kingdom legitimacy.

The Glimgold is the largest denomination — a large, bright coin that can be broken into four quarter-pieces, each worth twenty-five Silver Seals. Both sides bear a Rune Drake engraving, different drakes used across different coin variants. Glimgold transactions happen at the level of noble contracts, kingdom-level commerce, and major guild agreements. Most people who handle one are either very wealthy or handling it on someone else’s behalf.

The Silver Seal cannot be broken. One side bears one of the five kingdom insignias — a Varkaan Seal, a Halreth Seal, a Naaren Seal, a Vel’Era Seal, a Caltheran Seal — identifying where the coin was minted. The other side bears the Halvyr stamp, establishing it as continental currency regardless of origin. Guild regulated. Worth fifty Copper Carps. The design embeds the political arrangement of the High Kingdom into the physical object people handle every day — five kingdoms on one face, one throne on the other.

The Copper Carp has a carp head on one side and a depiction of the continent of Eòs on the other. It is the coin most people on Eòs actually use most of the time. Markets, taverns, ferry crossings, daily wages. The fish head is considered good luck by sailors across every coast on the continent, which is either deliberate design from the original mint or folk culture that attached itself to the image and stayed.

Full denomination scale: 1 Glimgold = 4 quarter-pieces = 100 Silver Seals = 5,000 Copper Carps.

Every kingdom mints its own local currency alongside the continental standard. Local coins circulate within kingdom borders and are accepted at exchange rates that fluctuate with political relationships and trade balances. High Kingdom coin is accepted everywhere without negotiation. Local coin requires a conversation. This distinction — universal versus conditional acceptance — is itself a form of soft power the High Kingdom exercises simply by existing as the monetary standard.

The High Kingdom’s treasury in Johanna’s Promise holds accumulated tribute from five kingdoms across two centuries of peace. Its true depth is known only to Berthold. It backs the currency in practice if not always in formal policy — the Glimgold’s value is ultimately downstream of the throne’s credibility, and the throne’s credibility is downstream of two hundred years of the Halvyr name not being attached to a betrayal.

TRADE REGULATION

The High Kingdom actively regulates continental trade. Tariffs, trade laws, royal merchant charters — the formal architecture exists. But the merchant guilds hold effective power over which laws are set and how they are enforced, which means the High Kingdom’s regulatory authority is real and simultaneously contested at every point where guild interests and crown interests diverge.

The practical result: continental trade law is negotiated rather than simply decreed. The High King sets the framework. The guilds fill it with specifics that favor their operations. Neither can function without the other — the crown needs the guilds’ logistical infrastructure and cross-kingdom reach, the guilds need the crown’s legal authority and military protection for trade routes. Two centuries of peace have calcified this arrangement into something that looks like stability and functions like a permanent argument that both sides have agreed not to resolve.

The Merchant Guilds of note: the Halrethian Merchant’s Guild with its Theralion City base and Palecrest Sea coastal operations, the Iron Guilds of Varkaan with their material monopoly, the Caltheran runewright guilds operating out of Glintgate, Vel’Era’s House Coralwind and House Tideborne managing the Qunthren trade routes, and Naaren’s House Sterling managing banking and financing functions across the republic. Each operates within the continental framework while maintaining the specific leverage their kingdom’s geography and resources provide.

JOHANNA’S PROMISE — HUB OF HUBS

Every kingdom has a primary trade hub. Johanna’s Promise is where they all converge.

Its geographic position makes this inevitable rather than designed. Dead center of Eòs. Equal road distance to all five kingdoms. The passes through Vespine’s Cradle are the primary northern entry points. The Shadowfen corridor connects to Naaren in the east. The western road runs to Halreth. The southern road through the middle of the continent reaches Vel’Era. Every major trade route on the continent passes through or near the city because the city sits where they naturally cross.

What this means economically: Johanna’s Promise takes a transaction cut on a significant proportion of continental commerce simply by being where it is. Not through tariffs alone — through the infrastructure of a city that has had two centuries to build the warehouses, banking houses, exchange offices, guild halls, and hospitality infrastructure that major trade requires. Merchants don’t route through the Promise because the law requires it. They route through it because the alternatives are less efficient and the city has everything they need in one place.

The ENAA adds a secondary economic layer that most analyses miss. The academy draws the aristocratic and Riftmarked scions of every kingdom. Those families send money. They maintain residences. They employ servants. They patronize merchants. An institution that draws the children of five kingdoms’ nobility generates enormous indirect economic activity in the city around it. The academy is an economic engine dressed as an educational institution.

WEALTH COMPARISON — THE CONTINENTAL PICTURE

Halreth is the wealthiest kingdom on Eòs in 1239 A.M. and has been for most of the Age of Peace. This is not visible in the way Varkaan’s military dominance or Caltheran’s arcane sophistication is visible. Halreth’s wealth is agricultural, cumulative, and structurally embedded in the continent’s dependency on its grain supply. You cannot eat weapons. You cannot eat rune theory. Two centuries of feeding a continent that was rebuilding from fifty years of war compounds into a level of accumulated agricultural wealth that no other kingdom’s economy can match.

The Peace Weeks draw spending from every kingdom annually. House Serene’s cultural patronage circulates money through arts and performance networks. House Quillan’s legal services generate revenue from every kingdom’s nobility seeking neutral arbitration. Halreth collects quietly and continuously from institutions most kingdoms don’t think of as economic instruments.

Varkaan is second by raw material output. The Iron Guilds’ monopoly on soulsteel, stormstone, and rune-core metal means Varkaan sells things no other kingdom can produce. Every army on the continent buys Varkaan-forged equipment. The material wealth is real and substantial. But the Iron Guilds retain their own treasury independent of the crown, which means Varkaan’s state wealth is lower than its total economic output suggests. The king is wealthy. The guilds are wealthier. The distinction matters for what the crown can actually deploy.

Vel’Era is third. The Qunthren trade position — sitting at the intersection of routes between Aethryn, Iskar, and Eòs — generates consistent transit revenue without requiring Vel’Era to manufacture the goods being moved. House Coralwind’s alchemy exports and House Solara’s sunstone trade add specialized high-value commodity streams. Furaha’s Fist’s weather tracking and lighthouse infrastructure make the southern trade corridor viable, which means Vel’Era effectively charges admission to one of the continent’s most important shipping lanes through the cost of the safety infrastructure it maintains.

Caltheran is fourth. The runewright guilds generate significant revenue — Caltheran certification carries continental weight and the guild apprenticeship system produces a continuous stream of certified practitioners whose skills are purchased by every kingdom. But the Council of Twelve’s arcane theology focus has redirected resources away from practical economic development toward institutional prestige. Aedric Spire is magnificent. Glintgate does the actual economic work. The gap between the capital’s appearance and the kingdom’s actual financial position is wider than it looks from outside.

Naaren is the poorest kingdom on the continent in 1239 A.M. and has been since the Kymancy War’s conclusion.

The post-war settlement stripped their military. Two centuries of deliberate isolationism stripped their trade relationships. The genetic control program concentrates magical ability in compliant bloodlines rather than economically productive ones. The automaton infrastructure that guards the republic and operates its cities was built and is maintained at enormous ongoing cost — Artifice constructs require materials, maintenance, and the institutional knowledge of House Cogswright, none of which is cheap. The Cipher’s Guild’s intelligence operations are expensive to run and generate no commercial revenue.

What Naaren has is institutional sophistication, surveillance capability, and the specific leverage of an intelligence state — information others will pay for, extracted from sources others don’t know are being read. This is real economic value. It does not feed the civilian population the way grain does or equipment sales do. Naaren in 1239 A.M. is a kingdom that has invested two centuries of post-war recovery into control systems rather than prosperity, and its civilian standard of living reflects that choice.

The irony the files establish: Naaren’s most economically productive asset in the current era may be the Runescribe League, which operates under their authority and generates intelligence revenue from every kingdom on the continent. Most of that revenue flows upward through the Order’s chain rather than into Naaren’s civilian economy. Naaren’s population is poor. The institutions that govern them are not.

CONTINENTAL TRADE ROUTES — SUMMARY

The primary land routes radiate from Johanna’s Promise outward. The northern road runs alongside the Aldren through ancient battlegrounds toward Varkaan — the Old Road, with its Gravewrens and its weight of Fifth Era history. The western road follows the Vespur Plains toward Theralion City. The eastern road threads through the Shadowfen corridor toward Naaren. The southern road runs through Vel’Era’s forest border toward Silvanawr.

The sea routes divide between the Palecrest Sea on Halreth’s western coast and the Qunthren passage through Vel’Era’s southern waters. The Selenic Deep on Naaren’s eastern coast is used for coastal trade only — no vessel ventures into the deep waters within living memory. The Ebonmaw on Caltheran’s northern coast is similarly constrained — coastal trade between the three northern kingdoms and nothing beyond the shelf.

The Qunthren passage is the continent’s connection to Aethryn and Iskar and is the highest-value trade corridor on the planet. Vel’Era controls access to it through the lighthouse infrastructure. What moves through the Qunthren — and who profits from it — is the economic question that underpins Vel’Era’s entire foreign policy.

THE ECONOMIC FAULT LINES

Halreth’s grain dependency is the continent’s most significant economic vulnerability. Five kingdoms and the High Kingdom capital eat Halreth’s food. A drought, a blight, a deliberate withholding — any of these produces a continental food crisis within a single growing season. Halreth has never weaponized this. The political cost of doing so would be enormous. But the theoretical leverage exists and every crown that has read its own economic situation understands it.

The Iron Guilds’ dual treasury — their wealth running parallel to Varkaan’s crown wealth — means the continent’s primary weapons manufacturer is financially independent of the kingdom nominally governing it. If the guilds and the Varkaan crown ever reach irreconcilable conflict, the guild’s ability to redirect material supply has continental implications.

Naaren’s civilian poverty alongside its institutional sophistication is a structural pressure that two centuries of isolation have suppressed but not resolved. A population that is poor relative to every neighboring kingdom, governed by institutions that serve Order-influenced objectives rather than civilian prosperity, is a population that produces internal pressure the republic’s surveillance architecture is specifically designed to contain. Whether that architecture can hold indefinitely is the question the current unnamed Chancellor has presumably inherited and not answered.

### Individual economy: 

CANON LOCKED — Halreth Economic Profile

PRIMARY REVENUE STREAMS

Halreth’s wealth is agricultural at its root and diversified at its surface. The Vespur Plains are the engine — exceptional crop yield across the kingdom’s heartland, fertile ground that produces grain, livestock, and orchard produce at a scale that feeds a significant portion of Eòs. House Arbor manages the Greatwood’s timber on a ritual calendar that governs when cutting is permissible and what must be replanted, which means Halreth’s forestry output is sustainable rather than extractive — slower than a kingdom that simply cuts, but perpetual in a way that extractive forestry never is. Two centuries of that discipline has produced a timber reserve that other kingdoms cannot replicate quickly.

The Palecrest Sea coastline is Halreth’s second major revenue source. House Llyr commands the western coast and oversees all maritime trade. The Renavon delta — Amos’ Fingers — is the commercial infrastructure that connects the inland grain supply to the coastal shipping lanes. Goods move from the Vespur Plains down the Renavon, through the delta, and into the Palecrest trade network that runs along the western coast of Eòs. Halreth’s position on the Palecrest gives it access to Vel’Era’s southwestern trade routes and indirect connection to the Qunthren passage without requiring Halreth to maintain the expense of a deep-water navy.

The tertiary revenue streams are cultural and legal rather than material. The Peace Weeks at Paxville draw continental spending annually — accommodation, food, transport, and the specific commerce that forms around a festival that every kingdom’s aristocracy attends. House Quillan’s legal services generate consistent revenue from every kingdom’s nobility seeking neutral arbitration on treaty disputes and inheritance questions. Halreth is the preferred venue for sensitive negotiations because its neutrality is credible — and credibility, in this case, is a commodity that pays in Glimgold.

House Weaver’s enchanted textiles are Halreth’s highest-value craft export. Robes imbued with warmth, resilience, or minor glamour, coveted by nobility across every kingdom. The weaving tradition predates the house’s ennoblement and the techniques are closely guarded. A House Weaver robe at a Varkaan noble’s table is a status marker that functions simultaneously as luxury good, magical utility item, and implicit advertisement for Halreth’s craftsmanship.

House Serene’s cultural output — poets, musicians, painters rotating through Theralion’s salons — creates a softer revenue stream through patronage and artistic commission that circulates Glimgold through the kingdom’s professional creative class. Art as an economic instrument is easy to underestimate until you count how much continental nobility spends on it annually.

INTERNAL WEALTH DISTRIBUTION

Halreth’s wealth distributes more broadly than any other kingdom on Eòs with the partial exception of Johanna’s Promise. This is structural rather than ideological — agricultural economies require a functioning rural population, which means Halreth cannot afford the extreme wealth stratification that a purely extractive or military economy might produce and still maintain the output it depends on.

The Remnus crown sits at the apex. The Egyptian model established in the Politics pass holds here — houses administer domains assigned by the crown and derive wealth from performing those functions, but the crown’s position as the gravitational center of Halreth’s institutional life means the largest proportion of the kingdom’s accumulated wealth flows through Theralion City and its administrative infrastructure.

Below the crown, House Llyr on the western coast and House Arbor in the Greatwood are the wealthiest houses by land value and resource control respectively. House Quillan’s wealth is liquid rather than landed — accumulated legal fees and archival contracts rather than territory. House Weaver and House Serene operate at a prosperous professional level that would be considered wealthy in any other kingdom and is considered comfortable in Halreth’s context.

The rural population of the Vespur Plains is the economic base and is treated accordingly. Farmers, shepherds, orchard workers, mill towns along the Orella — this population produces Halreth’s foundational wealth and maintains a standard of living that is meaningfully above subsistence. Not prosperous by Theralion standards. Not desperate either. The agricultural economy requires enough rural stability to function, and Halreth has understood this since the founding king made his decision about the leylines.

Old Thenbury at the base of Theralion’s cliffs is its own economic culture — maritime rather than agricultural, fishing and trade and dockwork, the specific prosperity of a port town that was there before the capital and has never entirely acknowledged the capital’s authority over it. Old Thenbury merchants operate in a commercial register that the Theralion nobility finds slightly vulgar and completely necessary.

THE LEYLINE QUESTION — ECONOMIC IMPLICATIONS

The leylines beneath the Vespur Plains produce exceptional agricultural yield. The common understanding is either that they run deep enough below the bedrock that surface exposure remains below harmful threshold, or that something in the plains’ composition buffers the effect.

What is known only to the Remnus crown: the leylines can be accessed. The spells this access enables are not available to any other kingdom. The economic implication that every Remnus monarch has sat with and chosen not to act on: a properly weaponized leyline tap could produce agricultural output, arcane power generation, or direct combat capability at a scale that would collapse the continental economic balance. Halreth’s wealth would shift from largest-kingdom-in-a-competitive-system to sole-economic-hegemon-in-a-destroyed-one.

The founding king’s reasoning was that harnessing a continental weapon would cost more than it could ever buy. His successors have agreed. The wealth Halreth has accumulated through patient agricultural dominance over two centuries — without touching the leylines — makes the point. The restraint is the strategy. And the restraint is one monarch’s death away from being revisited by someone who reads the founding document and reaches a different conclusion.

TRADE RELATIONSHIPS

Halreth’s trade relationships are the continent’s most universally positive, which is what happens when you feed everyone. Even kingdoms with historically tense political relationships with Halreth — Naaren’s Shadowfen disputes, Varkaan’s periodic border friction — maintain cordial commercial relationships because the alternative is disrupting food supply chains their civilian populations depend on.

The Halrethian Merchant’s Guild operates across the continent with the specific confidence of an institution that knows its product is non-negotiable. Their corridors run through Bridgestone Pavilion — the embassy canal-bridge city that also hosts Vel’Era’s floating maritime archives — making it a commercial and diplomatic hub simultaneously. Dovelea Market operates as a treaty town with amnesty provisions, which means it attracts the specific commerce that benefits from neutral ground: sensitive transactions, cross-kingdom contracts, merchants who need a location outside any single kingdom’s jurisdiction.

The relationship with Johanna’s Promise is the most strategically significant single trade link in Halreth’s portfolio. The Promise feeds from the Vespur Plains and Halreth funds the Promise. That bilateral arrangement means the continent’s most credible neutral military force is economically dependent on its wealthiest kingdom — a dependency that Halreth has never leveraged overtly and which constitutes a permanent background pressure on the Promise’s actual neutrality.

ECONOMIC FAULT LINES

The grain dependency leverage cuts both ways. Halreth’s wealth is contingent on the Vespur Plains continuing to produce. A sustained blight, a Chroma weather event severe enough to damage multiple harvests, a leyline instability event at the eastern tip near Shadowfen where the leylines surface — any of these damages Halreth’s economic position faster than any military threat could. The kingdom that feeds everyone is also the kingdom that loses the most if the food supply fails.

The Peace Weeks’ constitutional protection is an economic as well as political lock. The festival cannot be cancelled by any Halreth monarch unilaterally. This is good for Halreth’s long-term soft power and its consistent revenue from continental attendance. It also means a future king who wants to use the Peace Weeks as economic leverage — threatening cancellation to extract concessions — cannot. The founding decision to lock it into law removed a potential tool from every future crown.

House Llyr’s maritime trade dominance on the Palecrest creates a concentration risk. One house controls all western coastal trade. If House Llyr’s leadership makes poor decisions, enters irreconcilable conflict with the crown, or is compromised by external pressure — Naaren’s intelligence operations, Order influence, commercial rivals from Vel’Era’s Tideborne house — Halreth’s entire maritime trade apparatus is affected through a single institutional chokepoint.

The leyline secret is the economic fault line that nobody outside the Remnus bloodline can see. Its existence as potential rather than deployed power means it doesn’t currently distort the continental economy. The day it does — if it ever does — the economic landscape of Eòs changes in a single season in ways that none of the other kingdoms’ planning has accounted for.


CANON LOCKED — Varkaan Economic Profile

PRIMARY REVENUE STREAMS

Varkaan’s economy is extractive and industrial at its core. The kingdom sits on top of some of the most valuable raw material deposits on Eòs and has spent centuries building the infrastructure to process and sell them. This is not agricultural wealth that compounds slowly. This is the specific, concentrated wealth that comes from controlling materials other kingdoms cannot produce and desperately need.

The Iron Guilds are the primary engine. Druhmir Hold as their seat is not simply an administrative designation — it means the most sophisticated weapons and armor manufacturing infrastructure on the continent operates from Varkaan’s capital. The material supply chain is vertically integrated in a way no other kingdom has managed to replicate: Ironvein extracts etherium and rune-core metal, Greyhall Mines produces stormstone, the Emberforge Village specializes in soulsteel production, and the Guild’s master craftsmen in Druhmir Hold process all of it into finished product that carries certification no other forge on the continent can match.

Every kingdom’s military runs on Varkaan-forged equipment. This is not preference — it is practical necessity. Varkaan-certified armor is demonstrably superior in arcane resistance and material durability to anything produced elsewhere. The Guild’s certification system guarantees quality backed by generations of consistent craft, and the guarantee is credible because it has held long enough that kingdoms have built their military procurement around it. Halreth buys it. Caltheran buys it. Vel’Era buys it. Even Naaren — stripped of offensive military capacity by the post-war settlement — purchases Varkaan-forged defensive equipment for its automaton infrastructure through carefully worded procurement contracts that technically don’t violate the treaty’s terms.

The highland lake district provides the freshwater abundance that makes this scale of industrial production sustainable. Hundreds of lakes fed by snowmelt support both the mining culture and the smithing operations without the water access problems that constrain industrial production in other kingdoms. This is a geographic advantage Varkaan built into its industrial design rather than taking for granted.

House Al-Khem’s desert pharmaceutical trade is the second major revenue stream and one that operates almost entirely independently of the Iron Guilds. The Sol’telarī interior produces rare flora and fauna that exist nowhere else on Eòs — geographically isolated by the highland rain shadow effect, chemically distinct from anything in the temperate zones, and medically and alchemically significant in ways that House Al-Khem has spent generations cataloguing and monetizing. Everything from potent healing salves to neurotoxins used by Varkaan’s military intelligence apparatus flows through their desert operations. Their desert territory also sits in direct proximity to Iskari ancestral ground, which means their monopoly on Sol’telarī desert products is complicated by the Iskari crisis the current king is attempting to resolve through purge.

House Sunder’s construction and quarrying operation is the third stream — less glamorous than weapons or alchemy but structurally significant. Varkaan’s imposing keep infrastructure, the fortress towns that guard highland passes, the defensive positions that make Varkaan’s highland terrain effectively ungovernable by outside forces — all of it is House Sunder’s work. They export construction expertise and quarried highland stone to kingdoms building fortifications, which is consistent steady revenue rather than spectacular but never absent.

INTERNAL WEALTH DISTRIBUTION

Varkaan’s wealth distributes in a way that produces maximum internal tension. The kingdom generates enormous economic output. That output concentrates in specific institutional pockets rather than spreading through the population in any way that resembles equity.

The Iron Guilds hold the largest concentrated wealth pool in Varkaan and one of the largest on the continent — separate from and parallel to the crown treasury, maintained by an institution that swears to the craft rather than the king. The Guild masters at Druhmir Hold are personally wealthier than most of Varkaan’s noble houses. Their institutional treasury funds ongoing research, master apprenticeships, infrastructure maintenance, and the specific political operations the Guilds deploy whenever a Varkaan crown tests the boundary of their independence.

The crown’s treasury is substantial but structurally limited by the Guild parallel. The unnamed current king commands significant resources through tribute, taxation of non-Guild commerce, and military contract revenue from the Promise and other external contracts. He does not command the Guild treasury and cannot access it except through cooperation. This means Varkaan’s political leadership has less liquid wealth than its total economic output suggests.

House Daruun and House Tal’kor hold the next tier — noble house wealth accumulated through military prestige, land grants, and the specific financial arrangements that come with commanding Varkaan’s military forces. Their wealth is real and substantial and secondary to both the Guilds and the crown.

House Al-Khem sits in an unusual position — significant independent wealth derived from desert trade, operating at geographic and cultural remove from Druhmir Hold, and currently facing existential question about what happens to their desert territory if the current king’s purge proposal succeeds and the Iskari who share that territory are removed.

The general Varkaan population below the noble and guild tier lives in conditions that range from the functional prosperity of mining towns and fishing villages around the highland lakes to the harsh subsistence of highland frontier communities and the Sol’telarī desert fringe. The Iskari population in the interior exists entirely outside the formal wealth distribution — economically marginal by design, integrated into the desert economy around River Kýe and Kye’vathn in ways the kingdom uses but does not formally account for.

THE GUILD-CROWN SPLIT — ECONOMIC SPECIFICS

The dual treasury is Varkaan’s most significant internal economic structure and the one that most shapes its external relationships.

When the current king needs to fund a military campaign, a major infrastructure project, or a political operation like the Iskari purge he is attempting to push through council, he draws on the crown treasury. The Guild treasury is not available to him. If the campaign requires Guild-produced materials — which every Varkaan military operation does — he must negotiate procurement terms with the Guilds as a commercial counterparty rather than simply directing production as sovereign.

This means Varkaan’s king is always in the position of being a significant customer of his own kingdom’s most powerful economic institution. The Guilds provide material on contract terms they set. The crown pays. The arrangement functions smoothly when Guild and crown interests align, which they often do. It becomes politically charged the moment they diverge — and the Iskari purge question has the potential to diverge them significantly, because the purge affects the Sol’telarī population that includes labor the mining operations near the desert fringe depend on.

TRADE RELATIONSHIPS

Varkaan’s trade relationships are structurally simple: every kingdom buys its weapons and armor, which means every kingdom has an ongoing commercial relationship with the Iron Guilds whether they like it or not. The political relationships between Varkaan and its neighbors range from cool to historically contested. The commercial relationships are all functional because the product is irreplaceable.

The relationship with Caltheran is the most commercially complex. The Aldren corridor — the most historically contested border on the continent — is also the primary trade route through which Varkaan’s goods move south. Hudria, the border city that has changed hands enough times that its identity is genuinely split between two cultures, serves as the commercial hinge point for this corridor. Materials move south through Hudria into Caltheran’s internal market. Caltheran’s runewright expertise moves north through the same corridor, often applied directly to Guild-produced metal in collaborative projects that neither kingdom particularly wants to acknowledge as cooperative.

The relationship with Halreth via Ironcross is the most straightforwardly transactional. The Guilds eat Vespur grain. Halreth buys Varkaan steel. The waystation at Ironcross is where these flows intersect, and the oath-swearing ceremonies that mark new Guild masters happen here rather than in Druhmir Hold — a deliberate choice that keeps the Guild’s most significant ritual on the trade route rather than in the capital, reinforcing that the Guild’s identity is commercial and craft-based rather than administratively tied to the crown.

Ebonreach on the Vel’Era border serves as a caravan market — more informal, more diverse in its commerce, less Guild-dominated than Ironcross. It handles the specific trade flows that don’t move through formal Guild channels: House Al-Khem’s desert products, minor house commerce, and the cross-border informal economy that develops wherever two kingdoms share a long border and the road between them is well-traveled.

ECONOMIC FAULT LINES

The Iskari purge question is the most immediately dangerous economic fault line in Varkaan in 1239 A.M. House Al-Khem’s desert pharmaceutical monopoly depends on Sol’telarī ecology that Iskari communities have maintained and managed for generations — not formally, not through any arrangement the kingdom acknowledges, but through the practical reality that people who have lived in a desert for centuries know how to sustain it. A purge removes that population and introduces the specific agricultural instability of removing the people who understood the land’s requirements. House Al-Khem presumably knows this. Whether they have said so in council is a question the files haven’t answered.

The mining labor question runs parallel. Some Sol’telarī fringe mining operations use Iskari labor — not enslaved, not formally contracted, but present in the economy in the ways marginal populations always are. Removing them disrupts production patterns the Guild’s supply chain depends on, which is the kind of consideration that turns Guild masters into quiet political opponents without them needing to make any public statement.

The dual treasury is the structural fault line that never fully closes. Every generation of Varkaan’s crown inherits the stalemate. The current king has the additional pressure of a council fractured over the purge question and an internal crisis that is consuming political attention he cannot simultaneously spend on managing the Guild relationship. If the purge proceeds over Guild objections, the crown and the Guilds enter a period of commercial friction at exactly the moment the kingdom can least afford reduced production capacity.

The Ebonmaw northern coast is an economic dead zone — coastal trade only, nothing beyond the shelf, Caer Torund maintained against the atmospheric exposure of the Buffer Zone. There is no economic development in Varkaan’s northernmost territory and there will not be until whatever the Ebonmaw contains becomes either safe or irrelevant. Every king inherits that dead zone as a permanent line on the map where Varkaan’s economic reach stops.

Appreciated. Vel’Era is the interesting one economically — it’s the only kingdom whose wealth is fundamentally positional rather than productive. They don’t manufacture the continent’s most valuable goods. They sit between the people who do.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

Ha. Elara Arsenia is a Morrandis descendant through Phoebe, which makes House Morrandis the current royal line of Vel’Era by the time Book 6 rolls around. The house that was destroyed in a political purge is now the throne. The world has a sense of humor.

CANON LOCKED — Caltheran Economic Profile

PRIMARY REVENUE STREAMS

Caltheran’s economy runs on knowledge as commercial product. Not knowledge as philosophy, not knowledge as theology — knowledge as a service that other kingdoms need and pay for. The runewright tradition is the engine. The Echoa Archive is the prestige infrastructure that surrounds and partially obscures that engine from outside view.

The runewright guilds operating out of Glintgate are Caltheran’s most practically significant economic institution. Every kingdom on Eòs uses rune infrastructure — warding systems, arcane construction, the Abjuration defenses built around major cities, the navigational constructs built into Vel’Era’s lighthouse network, the containment fields that protect Naaren’s more volatile Artifice experiments. Caltheran’s runewright guilds trained the practitioners who built most of it. The craft crossed the Selenic Deep with the Aethryn refugees five hundred years ago and was refined on leyline-saturated ground for generations until it became something distinct from its origins — not imported technique but a tradition that adapted to a specific magical environment and developed accordingly.

Caltheran runewright certification carries continental weight. It implies not just technical competence but a specific theoretical grounding in leyline interaction that practitioners from other traditions don’t share. Kingdoms buy that certification through apprenticeship fees, practitioner contracts, and the ongoing maintenance agreements that arcane infrastructure requires. A ward built by a Caltheran-certified runewright carries a quality guarantee backed by generations of consistent craft. The kingdom’s primary export is expertise, and expertise has no supply constraint the way material goods do — it scales through the apprenticeship system without requiring more raw material.

Glintgate as the commercial engine deserves specific attention. The city sits at a river estuary with access to the Vespine’s Cradle passes to the south and the Aldren corridor to the north. It is where goods enter Caltheran from Halreth, where the runewright guilds operate their commercial front, and where the artisan culture that gives Caltheran its public reputation is most visible. The Artisan’s Quay in Aedric Spire is the spectacular display — magically lit, overwhelming in sensory impact, a city that treats innovation as spectacle. Glintgate is where the actual commercial transactions happen.

Marisport on the northern Ebonmaw coast is Caltheran’s only maritime access and a secondary revenue source. Nobody sails the Ebonmaw beyond the coastal shelf — the water beyond is not navigable in any commercially meaningful sense. Marisport exists for coastal trade between the three northern kingdoms: Caltheran, Varkaan, and the occasional Naaren vessel trading through the Ashen Coast corridor. It is limited commerce rather than significant commerce, but it is present and it gives Caltheran a northern trade lane that doesn’t depend on road access through the Aldren corridor.

Runehold Abbey’s monastic scriptorium generates a smaller but consistent revenue stream through manuscript production, rune theory scholarship, and the specific archival services that kingdoms pay for when they need historical rune records authenticated or ancient warding systems diagnosed. The monks are not wealthy individually. The institution accumulates fees over generations.

THE ECHOA ARCHIVE — ECONOMIC REALITY VERSUS PERCEPTION

The Echoa Archive is Caltheran’s most significant prestige asset and its most economically complicated one.

The Archive’s public function is the preservation and study of arcane knowledge. Its reputation as the continent’s most significant academic institution outside the ENAA draws scholars, researchers, and institutional delegations from every kingdom. That traffic generates direct revenue — access fees, research permits, accommodation in the capital, the commerce that forms around any institution that requires prolonged residence.

The Archive’s actual function under the Council of Twelve has shifted substantially from preservation and active research toward arcane theology, political instrument, and containment site for dangerous specimens. The Wyrshade vivisection decision in Book 1 is the clearest expression of this — not research in any productive sense, but the specific hubris of an institution that has confused the accumulation of dangerous things with understanding them.

The economic consequence of this shift: the Archive generates prestige revenue based on a reputation it built when it was doing genuine scholarship. It is currently spending that reputation on theological projects and political maneuvering that produce no commercial output. The gap between what the Archive costs to maintain and what it generates in direct commercial return has been widening for generations. The Council of Twelve sustains it through institutional prestige and the runewright guild fees that effectively subsidize the capital’s ambitions.

This is the central tension in Caltheran’s economy: Glintgate generates the money, Aedric Spire spends it. The Glintgate guilds resist Council subordination specifically because they understand this dynamic and resent being commercially exploited to fund theological projects they have no stake in.

INTERNAL WEALTH DISTRIBUTION

Caltheran’s wealth distributes through an unusual structure for a kingdom — there is no monarchy to concentrate it at the apex, and the Council of Twelve is a collective institution rather than a hereditary one. This should theoretically produce more distributed wealth than a monarchical system. In practice it produces a different kind of concentration.

The Council of Twelve controls the Echoa Archive and through it the institutional authority over Caltheran’s arcane certification system. They do not personally own the runewright guilds but they can influence certification standards, access to the Archive’s leyline-saturated research facilities, and the specific regulatory environment in which the guilds operate. Twelve people governing through institutional leverage rather than hereditary right still concentrate significant economic power — it is just less visible than a crown.

The runewright guild masters at Glintgate represent the second wealth tier — prosperous, institutionally independent to a significant degree, and currently in the specific tension of resisting Council subordination while depending on the Council’s institutional prestige for the continental credibility that makes their certification valuable. They are wealthy enough to resist but not wealthy enough to be fully independent. The guilds fund the resistance through collective action rather than individual wealth.

Below the guild masters, the artisan class in Aedric Spire and Glintgate lives in the specific prosperity of skilled professionals whose work is in continental demand. Arcane artisans, certified runewrights, apprentices in the final stages of their training — this is a professional class that earns more than almost any equivalent class in Naaren and less than the equivalent in Halreth’s agricultural middle tier.

House Furrow — masterless servants, their keeps now ruins, intimate knowledge of fortifications they can no longer protect — represents the specific poverty of a house whose function was eliminated by historical change. They are the economic casualty of Caltheran’s political disruption, present at the ENAA as the last institutional remnant of a service function that no longer has anything to serve.

THE CALTHERAN DIASPORA — ECONOMIC DIMENSION

The fallen houses of Caltheran attending the ENAA represent an economic phenomenon as much as a political one. Houses Neorxn, Blackwood, Furrow, and others displaced by the Council’s ascendancy maintain their economic operations — however reduced — from outside their ancestral territory.

The Blackwood cadet branch situation is the most economically significant. The internal schism between Morgan’s uncle and Morgana over who leads the Caltheran diaspora is partly ideological and partly a dispute over who controls what remains of the house’s commercial infrastructure. A house whose ancestral keeps are ruins but whose commercial and legal networks survived the political disruption still has economic weight — it is weight denominated in relationships and contracts rather than territory.

What this means for Caltheran’s economy proper: commercial networks that used to flow through ancestral Caltheran noble house channels now flow around them. The diaspora houses have either reconstituted their commercial operations in new contexts or lost those operations entirely. The Council benefits from the former — networks that predate their authority continuing to generate economic activity in Caltheran’s commercial environment — and faces no structural obligation to the houses that built those networks.

TRADE RELATIONSHIPS

Caltheran’s primary trade relationship is with every kingdom that uses rune infrastructure, which is every kingdom. The runewright guild services move outward from Glintgate in all directions — south through Vespine’s Cradle into Halreth, north through the Aldren corridor toward Varkaan, east toward Naaren through the border region, south-southeast through Twilight Bastion toward Vel’Era.

The relationship with Halreth is functionally dependent in both directions. Halreth sends grain north. Caltheran sends certified runewrights south. Neither kingdom likes the arrangement enough to celebrate it and neither can easily replace it.

The relationship with Varkaan through the Aldren corridor is the most historically contested and commercially significant bilateral trade relationship on the continent. Varkaan-forged materials move south. Caltheran’s runewright expertise moves north — often applied directly to Guild-produced metal in collaborative projects that neither kingdom acknowledges publicly as cooperative. Hudria as the commercial hinge point for this corridor has changed hands enough times that its merchant class has developed a pragmatic relationship with both cultures simultaneously, which makes it the most commercially sophisticated border city on the continent.

The Runescribe League’s connection to Caltheran’s institutional channels provides a secondary economic layer that operates largely outside Caltheran’s formal commercial accounting. Intelligence as a commodity, archival access as a service, the specific value of being able to monitor what moves through the continent’s most significant arcane institution — these generate revenue that flows through the League rather than through Caltheran’s treasury. The Council’s use of the League to monitor inconvenient people is itself a cost rather than a revenue source, paid in access and cooperation rather than Glimgold.

ECONOMIC FAULT LINES

The Glintgate-Aedric Spire split is the fault line that has been widening for generations and has not yet broken. The guilds generate the revenue. The Council spends it on institutional prestige. The guilds resist subordination. The Council applies regulatory pressure. Neither side has escalated to open conflict because the mutual dependency holds — but the dependency is becoming asymmetric. The guilds have been expanding their continental client base for two centuries. The Council’s prestige is declining as the theological redirect becomes more visible to outside observers. The break, when it comes, will favor Glintgate.

The Neorxn erasure campaign is an economic risk disguised as a political operation. When Wilhiem surfaces and his claim is understood — when the continent grasps that the true royal line of Caltheran is alive and that the Council of Twelve are frauds sitting on a throne that isn’t theirs — the institutional legitimacy that the runewright certification system depends on is suddenly unmoored. Caltheran certification carries continental weight because it is backed by the Archive’s authority. If the Archive’s authority is retroactively delegitimized, the certification system requires a new institutional anchor. The guilds have the craft tradition to survive this. The Council does not.

The Archive’s reputation for responsible management of dangerous arcane material died in the moment Rhen Tauvor called the Wyrshade a blessing.

PRIMARY REVENUE STREAMS

Vel’Era’s economy is built on position, infrastructure, and proprietary knowledge — in that order. It is the only kingdom on Eòs whose primary wealth derives not from what it produces but from where it sits and what it has built to make that position commercially exploitable.

Goods moving between continents pass through or near Vel’Era’s waters. The kingdom doesn’t manufacture those goods. It doesn’t extract them. The Great Lighthouses are worth dwelling on as an economic instrument. Without the lighthouses the southern trade corridor closes. Vel’Era built that infrastructure and maintains it at considerable ongoing cost, which means the lighthouse fees are not profit extraction but cost recovery plus margin — which other kingdoms accept because the alternative is losing access to the most significant intercontinental trade route on the planet.

Furaha’s Fist’s Divination mage and Artifice weather-tracking network is the secondary infrastructure layer. It detects typhoon formation before it reaches the lighthouses’ visual range, giving Vel’Era the most sophisticated maritime early warning system on the planet. This capability has dual economic function: it protects Vel’Era’s own coastal infrastructure from storm damage, and it produces meteorological intelligence that other kingdoms’ navies and merchant fleets pay for because knowing a Chroma typhoon is forming four days before it arrives is worth significant Glimgold to anyone with ships on the southern waters.

House Solara’s sunstone quarries are Vel’Era’s most significant extractive industry and the closest thing the queendom has to Varkaan’s material monopoly. Sunstone is a component in Furaha’s Fist’s weather-tracking technology, in the Great Lighthouses themselves, and in arcane navigation constructs used by merchant fleets across the continent. House Solara controls the quarries and the processing expertise. Their wealth makes them the most powerful house in Vel’Era after the royal line — powerful enough to be a quiet rival to the throne in a way no other Vel’Era house approaches. The files note this tension explicitly.

House Coralwind’s enchanted coral alchemy is the premium export commodity. Potions and arcane focuses derived from marine life — cultivation and processing techniques proprietary, the advantage maintained for generations. These are high-value, low-volume goods that move through luxury commercial channels rather than bulk trade. A House Coralwind focus purchased by a Caltheran archwarden or a Varkaan battle-mage costs significantly more than anything from House Al-Khem’s desert pharmaceutical line, and the market for them is specifically the wealthiest and most magically sophisticated buyers on the continent.

House Azure’s sea-sagas and cartographic work generate a revenue stream that most economic analyses dismiss as cultural rather than commercial. This is incorrect. The most accurate coastal records on the continent are Vel’Era’s. They are also the records other kingdoms’ navies pay to access. The collaboration between House Azure’s literary tradition and Halreth’s maritime culture at Bridgestone Pavilion produces navigational documentation that has genuine commercial value to every maritime trading house operating in Vel’Era’s waters or approaching from the Palecrest Sea. Cartography is a commercial product. Vel’Era produces the best of it.

INTERNAL WEALTH DISTRIBUTION

Vel’Era’s wealth distributes along a clear hierarchy that maps directly to proximity to the Qunthren trade and the naval infrastructure that enables it.

House Arsenia at the apex concentrates royal wealth through the standard mechanisms — taxation, tribute, naval contract revenue, and the specific premium the crown receives as nominal owner of the lighthouse and weather-tracking infrastructure even though House Spire operates it and House Tideborne commands the fleet that enforces access fees.

House Solara is the significant anomaly. The files describe them as a quiet rival for influence, which is the appropriate framing — they are not politically hostile to the crown but they are financially capable of independence in a way no other Vel’Era house matches. Queen Lysandria V manages this with the specific attention of a monarch who knows which house in her kingdom could theoretically fund a challenge if it chose to.

Below Solara, Houses Tideborne, Coralwind, and Spire operate at a tier of substantial but institutionally bound wealth — their prosperity is tied to their function within the naval and trade infrastructure, which means it is real and significant and not easily separable from the queen’s authority over that infrastructure.

CANON LOCKED — Naaren Economic Profile

THE STARTING POINT

Every other kingdom’s economic entry begins with what it has. Naaren’s begins with what was taken.

The Kymancy War ended in 1036 A.M. The post-war settlement stripped and minimized Naaren’s military in the Japan model — a defensive force only, no offensive capability, the kind of restrictions that take generations to feel like geography rather than imposed terms. Two hundred and three years later, in 1239 A.M., most Naaren citizens alive have never known a different arrangement. The restrictions feel permanent because they have outlasted everyone who remembers them being imposed.

What the settlement stripped was not just military capacity. It stripped the economic infrastructure that military capacity had sustained — the supply chains, the industrial operations, the trade relationships that had developed around Naaren’s wartime position. A kingdom that spent fifty years fighting a continental war develops commercial relationships around that war. When the war ends in defeat and the military is dismantled, those relationships collapse with it. Naaren spent its first post-war generation not rebuilding toward prosperity but managing the collapse of a war economy without a replacement.

Two centuries of isolation completed what the settlement started. A kingdom that doesn’t trade internationally doesn’t develop the commercial relationships that generate surplus wealth. It generates enough to sustain itself — barely, in some regions — and nothing beyond that.

PRIMARY REVENUE STREAMS

The honest answer is that Naaren’s civilian revenue streams are inadequate relative to its institutional costs. What the republic generates commercially cannot sustain what it spends on governance, surveillance, and automaton infrastructure. The gap is bridged through mechanisms the civilian population doesn’t fully see.

Geothermal extraction in the northeastern thermal zone is the most significant natural resource operation. The Halt settlement runs extraction of geothermal minerals — unusual compounds produced by the specific volcanic activity in the thermal zone that have medical, alchemical, and arcane applications not easily replicated elsewhere. House Menlarian’s patents and magi-technical innovations are downstream of this — the Crystalline University at Zenrenban studies the thermal zone’s unique fauna and the compounds it produces, and those studies occasionally produce commercially valuable findings. The research pipeline from Zenrenban to marketable product is slow and often diverted by the Cipher’s Guild harvesting findings for intelligence purposes before they reach commercial application.

Nightshade Port’s coastal surveillance lane generates intelligence revenue that doesn’t appear in Naaren’s commercial accounting but is real. Every vessel passing through the Selenic Deep’s coastal lane moves within Nightshade’s surveillance range. What moves, who carries it, what cargo manifests show and what they actually contain — this information is worth Glimgold to parties who want to know what their rivals are shipping. The Cipher’s Guild sells this intelligence selectively, routing the revenue through channels that don’t surface in the official treasury’s visible ledgers.

The Shadowfen timber and medicinal herb operations, which Naaren claimed treaty rights over in the Book 1 summit, represent a historically contested natural resource. Naaren harvested Shadowfen for six years before the Kymancy Rift’s proximity made it impractical. The treaty claim exists not because the resource is currently economically productive but because maintaining the legal claim preserves a future option. When the Rift situation stabilizes — if it stabilizes — Shadowfen’s timber and medicinal botanical resources become commercially viable again and Naaren wants the legal architecture already in place.

House Sterling is Naaren’s most economically sophisticated institution and the one that generates the most liquid wealth. Banking. Contract financing. The vaults that fund other houses’ ambitions. House Sterling holds no armies and few lands, but their commercial reach extends into every kingdom on the continent through financing arrangements that other kingdoms’ merchant classes and noble houses depend on. Their wealth is real, substantial, and almost entirely invisible to outside observers who don’t know to look for it. Sterling doesn’t advertise. Sterling holds paper on people who would prefer Sterling not discuss what it holds.

House Cogswright’s automaton infrastructure generates maintenance and installation contracts that produce consistent revenue — but most of that revenue is internal, paid by the republic itself for services the republic requires. Cogswright doesn’t export automatons at commercial scale. The technology is too militarily significant and the treaty restrictions on military capability too carefully monitored. Domestically, Cogswright is the most economically productive major house in Naaren. Internationally it is invisible in the way that classified military technology is always invisible.

INTERNAL WEALTH DISTRIBUTION

Naaren’s internal wealth distribution is the starkest on the continent. The gap between the institutional class and the civilian population is not a matter of degree — it is a matter of kind.

The Cipher’s Guild at the apex of the institutional hierarchy controls Naaren’s intelligence revenue streams and the surveillance infrastructure that generates them. Guild senior operatives live materially well by any standard. Their wealth is operationally functional rather than conspicuously displayed — they maintain the standard of living required for their work, not more. Display is a surveillance risk in Naaren.

The Chancellor’s government tier below the Guild operates on allocated resources rather than accumulated wealth. Individual Wardens and administrative functionaries are comfortable. They are not wealthy. The republic does not generate surplus that reaches this level in personal rather than institutional form.

House Sterling, House Cogswright, House Heimdall, and House Menlarian represent the noble house tier — each prosperous within their specific domain, each constrained by the republic’s tendency to redirect commercially valuable intellectual output into institutional rather than commercial channels.

The civilian population below these tiers lives in conditions that range from functional subsistence in the agricultural zones south of Keth’var to the specific hardship of communities in the geothermal zone where working conditions carry documented health costs that the Halt’s research operations document with interest and the extraction operations continue regardless.

The Ashen Coast at Naaren’s northeastern extreme is the economic periphery made visible. Technically Naaren. Practically ungoverned. No guild presence, no mage institutions, no noble houses with holdings worth mentioning that far out. People who live there have more in common with Caltheran’s rural fringe than with anything coming out of Keth’var. They fish. Strange things occasionally come up in nets. The republic’s administrative reach ends long before it arrives there.

This is what Naaren’s civilian poverty looks like at its most extreme — the people farthest from Keth’var have been effectively abandoned by a governance structure too concerned with its own institutional survival to extend functional administration to its own borders.

THE GENETIC PROGRAM — ECONOMIC DIMENSIONS

The genetic program controlling who may produce children with magical ability has economic implications that operate below the surface of any commercial accounting.

Magical ability is an economic asset in Eòs. A mage commands premium wages, premium service fees, premium everything. A republic that concentrates magical ability in bloodlines it considers compliant and useful is making a decision about where economic premium flows — toward the institutional class and away from the general population. Over two centuries, this compounds. The gap between Naaren’s magically capable institutional class and its non-magical civilian population is not just political. It is economic, and it was designed to be.

The enforcement mechanism is built into republican law rather than decree, which means it cannot be dismantled by a single Chancellor’s decision. Whatever the current unnamed Chancellor’s private views on the program, they govern within a legal framework that pre-commits the republic to these economic distributions before any individual decision-maker sits down.

THE INTELLIGENCE ECONOMY — WHAT IT ACTUALLY IS

Naaren’s real economy — the one that sustains the gap between institutional cost and commercial revenue — is intelligence.

The Runescribe League operating under Naaren’s authority through the pre-existing Order connection generates intelligence revenue from across the continent. Cipher’s Guild sleeper agents embedded in every kingdom produce information that parties in those kingdoms pay for. Nightshade Port’s surveillance of the Selenic Deep lane generates shipping intelligence that maritime commercial interests purchase. The Crystalline University’s classified research findings, harvested before they reach publication, are sold to parties who want the advantage of knowledge their competitors don’t have.

None of this appears in Naaren’s official commercial accounting. None of it is taxed through the normal mechanisms that fund the republic’s civilian services. It flows through the Guild’s channels, funds the Guild’s operations, and sustains the institutional apparatus that maintains Naaren’s actual power while the civilian population lives below the continental poverty average.

This is the economic truth the continental overview established: Naaren’s population is poor. The institutions that govern them are not. The intelligence economy that sustains those institutions actively works against the transparency that would allow anyone to see the gap clearly.

TRADE RELATIONSHIPS

Naaren’s international trade relationships are limited by deliberate isolationism and constrained by the post-war treaty restrictions. What exists is carefully managed.

The relationship with Caltheran through the Ashen Coast corridor is the most active — coastal shipping between Naaren’s northeastern ports and Caltheran’s northern coastline, operating at modest commercial scale. Border trade through the Shadowfen and Chimera River corridor with Halreth exists but is contested by the same treaty disputes that the Book 1 summit made explicit.

The relationship with Johanna’s Promise is the most strategically significant single bilateral arrangement Naaren maintains. Naaren has an operative embedded at Caer Seryth. Naaren contested the Vigil expansion through procedural warfare. Naaren claimed treaty rights over Shadowfen specifically to prevent the Promise from establishing a permanent military presence in territory Naaren considers within its sphere. Every Naaren interaction with the Promise is a move in an ongoing negotiation about whether the continent’s most significant neutral military force operates as Naaren’s constraint or Naaren’s buffer.

There is no warm relationship in Naaren’s external trade portfolio. Isolation is not a commercial posture. It is a political one that has produced commercial consequences Naaren has never fully reckoned with.

ECONOMIC FAULT LINES

The institutional-civilian gap is the primary structural fault line. A population that is the poorest on the continent, governed by institutions that divert commercially valuable outputs to Order-influenced channels rather than civilian services, is a population that produces internal pressure the surveillance apparatus is specifically designed to suppress. The apparatus has held for two centuries. The pressure has been accumulating for two centuries. Whether the architecture can hold indefinitely is the question the files leave open because it is genuinely open.

The intelligence economy dependency is the second fault line. Naaren’s institutional sustainability depends on revenue streams that require secrecy to function. The moment Naaren’s intelligence operations are exposed at scale — the sleeper network, the Runescribe League connection, the classified research harvesting — the revenue those operations generate collapses. The republic’s ability to sustain its institutional infrastructure without those revenues is not established in the files because it has never been tested.

The automaton maintenance cost is the slow fault line. House Cogswright’s infrastructure is enormously expensive to maintain. The materials require procurement. The expertise requires institutional transmission. Two centuries of operating automaton infrastructure at the scale Naaren deploys it has accumulated maintenance obligations that compound. A kingdom with a weak civilian economy sustaining an expensive technological surveillance and defense infrastructure on intelligence revenue is not a sustainable long-term equilibrium. It is a balance that works until it doesn’t.

Aeraa Wynvale is a fault line of a specific kind. A Cipher’s Guild operative who became a double agent for Johanna’s Promise, who now has both institutional knowledge of the Guild’s operation at Caer Seryth and personal loyalty to people inside the Promise — she is the individual-scale version of the problem Naaren’s intelligence economy cannot fully solve. Assets with genuine human connections become unreliable. The Guild trains against this. It cannot eliminate it. She is also a failed experiment as not all genetic mutations using pathogens are fully successful and leave lasting impacts.

## Social structure
### Johanna’s Promise Social Structure

THE SHAPE OF IT

The city of Johanna’s Promise began as a ruined fortress claimed by two hundred exhausted veterans. Caer Seryth is still there — but it is the ENAA’s base, not the royal seat. Lune designed it this way deliberately. When she founded the academy at the close of the Fifth Era, Johanna’s Promise was still an active military company. She built the academy to connect directly to Caer Seryth via drawbridge — the old fortress becoming the physical foundation of the new institution, military architecture repurposed for academic function, the company’s legacy literally underfoot every time a student crosses from the dormitory grounds to the lecture halls.

The royal keep is separate. Berthold’s court operates from a different structure in a different part of the city — the seat of the High Kingdom’s governance, distinct from the academy and from the original fortress. The court and the ENAA exist in the same city but not in the same space. Students do not wander into the throne room. Courtiers do not sit in on lectures. The separation is architectural and deliberate — the academy trains the continent’s future. The court manages its present. Lune understood that those two functions require different air.

The city grew around both in concentric rings across two centuries. The innermost ring is the oldest — court infrastructure and the ENAA grounds occupying different quarters of the original urban core. The middle rings are the institutional and commercial layer — merchant houses, guild offices, warehouses, the banking infrastructure, the residences of the professional class. The outer rings are where most of the population actually lives — sprawling, dense, built faster than planned, the specific urban character of a city that has been growing faster than anyone can organise.

This is not King’s Landing in its decadence. This is King’s Landing in its ambition — a city that was founded on an ideal and has spent two centuries discovering what ideals cost when they have to feed a million people.

THE COURT

At the apex: High King Berthold Alaric Halvyr and Queen Annelise. Their court operates from the original Caer Seryth keep, which has been expanded, renovated, and layered over by successive generations of Halvyr rule but retains its military architecture beneath the diplomatic surface. The throne room where Berthold reinforced Jenova’s deception is inside a fortress. The diplomatic language is spoken inside walls designed for siege.

The court’s social composition is continental rather than national. Unlike every other kingdom’s ruling apparatus — which is staffed by that kingdom’s houses and reflects that kingdom’s cultural identity — Johanna’s Promise draws its court from all five kingdoms. The tribute structure means every kingdom’s aristocracy has a financial and political relationship with the High Throne, which means every kingdom’s houses maintain a presence in the capital. Ambassadorial residences. Merchant representations. Noble families maintaining city estates for the specific purpose of being close to power. A Vel’Eran noble and a Varkaan noble who would never share a corridor in their home kingdoms share a district in Johanna’s Promise.

This produces the continent’s most politically complex social environment. The court at Silvanawr is Vel’Eran. The court at Druhmir Hold is Varkaan. The court at Johanna’s Promise is everyone simultaneously, which means every interaction carries the full weight of every bilateral relationship, every historical grievance, every economic dependency that exists between the five kingdoms. A dinner at the High King’s table is a diplomatic event whether anyone intends it to be, because the people at the table carry their kingdoms’ positions in their names.

Berthold manages this by being cold. His political calculus prioritises succession mechanics over personal warmth, and that flatness produces a court culture where emotion is a liability and precision is currency. The people who thrive in Berthold’s court are the people who can calculate five kingdoms’ interests simultaneously while smiling at the correct person. The people who don’t thrive leave, or are managed, or become the kind of court functionary who survives by being useful rather than visible.

THE ENAA — SOCIAL ECOSYSTEM

The Eos National Arcane Academy is the most socially complex institution on the continent. Not because it is the most prestigious — though it is. Because it admits everyone.

The Sorting Sigil places students. Not bloodline. Not attunement. Not house standing. The Sigil was created by Lune Jiè herself — built using primordial magic, not Chroma — and it reads something in the individual that no other mechanism on the continent can replicate. A princess and a farmer’s son stand before it and receive the same categorical judgment. Admission requires arrival and acceptance before the deadline. There is a population limit — the academy has physical capacity constraints — but that limit has not yet been met, partly because Caer Seryth’s military infrastructure serves as secondary living space when the dormitory villages fill. The result: an institution that is functionally open to anyone who can get there. Commoners attend. Non-attuned students attend. People without house affiliation, without noble standing, without the specific preparation that aristocratic families provide — they attend, and they are sorted, and they walk the same halls as Jenova Halvyr.

What they experience in those halls is different.

The primary social dividing line is kingdom of origin. A Varkaan student and a Naaren student carry the weight of the Kymancy War, the Accords of Faelmr, two centuries of structural adversity between their kingdoms, into every shared lecture hall and dining table. Kingdom identity is the thing students arrive with. It is the first thing other students read — accent, dress, house name, the specific cultural markers that declare where you come from before you’ve said a word. The Iskari delegation’s arrival produced political fallout that Kai watched from the mess hall. Trevane’s argument for mercy in Clayrr’s debate carried the weight of his Iskari identity in a room under curfew because of an Iskari student. Kingdom is the floor beneath everything else.

House loyalty creates the second layer. The Sorting Sigil places students across kingdom lines — a Varkaan heir in the Raven’s Watch, a Halreth princess in the White Bears, a Vel’Eran duke’s son in the Raven’s Watch beside a Naaren minor house scholar. The house becomes a second identity that doesn’t replace kingdom origin but runs alongside it. Students who share a house share dormitory villages, share common rooms, share the specific social infrastructure that proximity and daily contact produce. The Raven’s Watch bonds that form between Kai and Sera and Elara transcend the fact that he is Vel’Eran and she is Halrethian and the princess is from the queendom. The bonds are real. The kingdom identities that underlie them do not disappear. Both layers operate simultaneously, and which one surfaces in a given moment depends on what the moment demands.

THE FIVE HOUSES AS SOCIAL STRATA

The five houses are not equal in the social hierarchy of the ENAA, regardless of what the founding philosophy claims.

The Raven’s Watch — named for Elaine Morrandis. Spiritualism, emotional control, tactical cunning. The house that produces strategists, intelligence operatives, and the specific kind of leader who wins by thinking rather than striking. Its social position within the ENAA is one of quiet respect — not the loudest house, not the most visibly powerful, but the one that other houses assume is planning something. Jeno Halvyr here. Kris Morrandis here. Elara Arsenia as de facto leader. The house contains the heir to the High Throne, the continent’s most dangerous manipulator, and the future queen of Vel’Era in the same common room. The internal politics are correspondingly lethal.

The Black Dogs — named for High King Brunor. Loyalty, authority, command. The house of overwhelming power. Valerius Daruun here. Veridian Cogswright here. Finnian II here. The Black Dogs’ social position is the bluntest — they are the house that other houses define themselves against. Loud, martial, politically aggressive. Wilhiem Neorxn is in this house, dismissed as a commoner, his true identity as the last heir of Caltheran and Riftmarked of the Spiral Pillar hidden behind a persona that the Black Dogs’ hierarchy treats as insignificant. The social irony is structural: the most important person in the entire academy is the lowest-ranked member of the house that values rank most.

The White Bears — named for Uthred Halvyr. Martial prowess, discipline, strength. The house of commanders. Kaelan Heimdall here. Thea Remnus here. Kaelen Tal’kor here. Morgan Blackwood here. The White Bears’ social position is martial aristocracy — the house that produces the students other houses want commanding their armies. Rhys Halvyr is here, carrying the Halvyr name in a house named for the ancestor the name comes from. The White Bears and the Black Dogs share a martial identity and a rivalry that mirrors the historical tension between Uthred’s discipline and Brunor’s authority.

The Hollow Hearts — named for the Hollow, the legendary mage of the Spiral Pillar. Potential, mystery, spirit. The house of the unexpected. Jupiter Jiè here. Diavon Holden as leader. Jamie Menlarian here. Illya Prism here. The Hollow Hearts’ social position is the most ambiguous — they are the house that contains people who don’t fit neatly elsewhere, which makes them either the most interesting house or the most overlooked depending on who is doing the looking. Diavon’s manipulative leadership has turned the house’s theoretical openness into a practical autocracy. Jupiter, whose volatile power makes her one of the most dangerous people in the academy, was forced into the champion roster by a leader who views her as a weapon rather than a person.

The Silver Bulls — named for Frederick Rowain. Endurance, resilience, quiet strength. The house of character. Hugo Rowain here — Royal Seer, carrying his father’s legacy, deeply unprepared for the political weight of the role. Ashur of the Red Sands here — Iskari heritage in a Varkaan minor house, looked down upon by the “true” nobility. Mason Gable here. The Silver Bulls’ social position is the most honest — they are the house that values what a person is over what a person has. This makes them the house that commoner-born students most often find themselves placed in, and the house that aristocratic students most often underestimate. Petra Sunder delivered Hugo’s message to Kai with the specific competence of someone the Bulls produce — not glamorous, not loud, simply present and reliable when the moment requires it.

THE COMMONER EXPERIENCE

A commoner-born student at the ENAA walks through the same gates, sits before the same Sorting Sigil, and receives the same house placement as a royal heir. This is true and it is also the beginning of a social experience that diverges from the aristocratic one at every subsequent point.

The commoner does not know the unwritten rules. Which common room conversations are actually political negotiations. Which invitations are genuine and which are assessments. Which professors have house affiliations that shape their grading. Which students’ names carry weight that requires specific forms of acknowledgment. The aristocratic students learned this at home, in courts, in the specific informal education that noble families provide as a matter of course. The commoner learns it by making mistakes and observing the consequences.

The commoner is not excluded. The academy does not formally distinguish between noble and common students. The Sorting Sigil does not read bloodline. The distinction is social, cultural, and economic — the commoner arrives with less, knows less of the institutional language, and occupies the specific position of someone who earned admission on individual merit in an institution where most students earned it on inherited access. The playing field is level. The preparation for the playing field is not.

Lyra of Stonebridge — placed in the Silver Bulls as the merit pick for the champion roster — represents this. A commoner whose unbreakable will was what the house valued enough to send into the Gauntlet alongside Hugo Rowain and Torvin Ironhand. She is there because she is exceptional. The aristocratic champions are there because they are expected to be.

Kai occupies a strange position in this landscape. He is not a commoner. He is a Pevlium. But his lack of attunement produces some of the same social effects — the assumption that his contribution is supplementary, the specific patience people show when accounting for his presence in contexts where magic is the default capability. He is an aristocrat experiencing a commoner’s social position from inside a noble house’s name, which gives him a perspective on the ENAA’s class dynamics that neither a true commoner nor a typical noble would have.

THE CITY POPULATION

Below the court and outside the academy walls, Johanna’s Promise is a city of working people.

The growth rate is the defining social fact. Two centuries of peace, five kingdoms funnelling population toward the continental centre, the economic gravity of a city that takes a transaction cut on a significant proportion of continental commerce — the result is a population that has outpaced every infrastructure plan the Halvyr governance has implemented. The city that Brunor’s idealism built is now a city that Berthold’s bureaucracy struggles to maintain.

Sanitation is the largest profession. Not the most prestigious. The largest. The people who manage the waste of the continent’s biggest city are the people the city depends on most fundamentally and acknowledges least. They are the social foundation in the most literal sense — without them the city becomes uninhabitable within weeks. Their social position is the universal position of essential workers in every civilisation that has ever existed: indispensable and invisible.

The merchant class occupies the middle tier — the warehouses, banking houses, exchange offices, guild halls, and hospitality infrastructure that two centuries of continental trade have built. These are not noble families. They are commercial families who have accumulated wealth through the specific advantage of operating at the continental crossroads. A merchant in Johanna’s Promise who facilitates trade between Halreth grain producers and Varkaan military procurement holds more liquid wealth than most minor house scions and less social standing than any of them. The gap between commercial wealth and institutional rank is where the city’s middle-class tension accumulates.

The ENAA adds an economic layer most analyses miss. The academy draws the aristocratic and Riftmarked scions of every kingdom. Those families send money. They maintain residences. They employ servants. They patronise merchants. An institution that draws the children of five kingdoms’ nobility generates enormous indirect economic activity. The servant class that supports the ENAA’s student population is itself a significant demographic — cooks, cleaners, groundskeepers, stable hands, the administrative staff that keeps the dormitory villages functional. These people live in the city. Their employment depends on the academy. Their social position is defined by proximity to an institution they serve but do not participate in.

The outer rings are where the growth problem is most visible. Housing built fast, maintained poorly, the infrastructure of a city that has been adding population faster than it can add capacity. The people in the outer rings are the most recent arrivals and the most economically marginal — labourers, unskilled workers, people drawn to the continental capital by the promise of opportunity and discovering that opportunity in the world’s largest city looks like competition with everyone else who had the same idea. The outer rings of Johanna’s Promise are not slums in the dramatic sense. They are the specific condition of a city that was founded on “paradise for all men” and has spent two centuries discovering what paradise costs when everyone shows up.

THE FOUNDING PHILOSOPHY AND ITS WEIGHT

A paradise for all men. The motto is still taught. Still invoked. Still carved in the marble that has replaced the original fortress stone in the public-facing architecture.

The social structure of Johanna’s Promise in 1239 A.M. is what happens when an ideal meets a population. Brunor meant it. The two centuries since have been a sustained experiment in whether meaning it is sufficient. The court operates above the city. The academy operates within the city. The city operates beneath both. The sanitation workers who keep the sewers running have never been inside Caer Seryth. The students who walk the academy grounds have never been to the outer rings. The High King who governs all of it manages a succession crisis inside a keep while the city outside that keep grows faster than his administration can process.

A paradise for all men. The sanitation worker, the commoner student, the merchant whose wealth exceeds his standing, the Riftmarked celebrity who has never cleaned anything, the servant who makes the dormitory bed that the duke’s son sleeps in — they all live in Brunor’s paradise. Whether they all experience it as one is the question the city has been answering differently for every person who lives there, and the answer has been diverging for two hundred years.

THE PEOPLE WHO DON’T COUNT

Johanna’s Promise’s version is the most visible on the continent because the city is the largest.

They are the people the growth outpaced. The infrastructure that was planned for a smaller population serves the population that exists now by not serving its margins. The outer rings produce the city’s uncounted — people who are technically residents of the continental capital and practically invisible to its governance. Not persecuted. Not targeted. Simply present in numbers the administrative apparatus was not designed to track, living in housing the building codes were not designed to regulate, working in economies the tax infrastructure was not designed to capture.

In a city founded on paradise for all men, the uncounted are the specific proof that “all” has always been aspirational rather than operational. They are not a failure of the founding philosophy. They are the cost of the founding philosophy being taken literally by a population large enough to test whether the philosophy was ever meant to bear the weight.

DIRECTOR’S NOTE — THE SORTING SIGIL (Solsyrr’s Eyes Only)

The Sorting Sigil operates on the laws of causality. Not probability. Not personality assessment. Not talent evaluation. Causality — the fabric of what will happen, not what might.

Lune built it using primordial magic — Eōtria’s own pre-Merge substrate, the same magical foundation the Druids of Vel’Era tend, the same force that predates the thirteen Chroma pillars. An Echowalker of the Jiè clan, carrying volatile and terrifyingly potent raw magic, tapped into the planet’s deepest magical layer and encoded causal law into a mechanism that the academy has used for over a century without understanding what it is.

Every placement the Sigil has ever made was not a recommendation. It was an inevitability. Jenova in the Raven’s Watch. Wilhiem in the Black Dogs. Kai beside Elara and Sera. Jupiter in the Hollow Hearts under Diavon’s manipulation. Every placement was the road the person was always going to walk, identified by an artifact that reads roads rather than travellers.

Nobody alive in 1239 A.M. knows this. The primordial nature of the Sigil, its causal mechanics, and the full scope of what Lune built are lost with her. She left relics and notes — a cache that would explain the Sigil’s true function — but the cache’s location is unknown. The academy treats the Sigil as a sorting mechanism. A magical hat. The most powerful artifact on the continent is being used as furniture.

The narrative implications are loaded. If anyone discovers what the Sigil actually does, the political consequences are staggering — every kingdom would want to control an oracle that reads causal paths. The Order of the Wyrm would want to destroy or capture it. The Riftmarked students it placed could be reread as pieces on a board that was set before they arrived. The question of whether the Sigil placed people where they needed to be or where they were always going to be is the same question, and the answer is both, and the distinction between fate and freedom collapses inside that answer.

The “most powerful artifact” designation is flagged but not locked. Null and the Black Blade operate in different categories — weapons versus oracle. The comparison that holds: Wonder of U / the Idea of Evil. Causality cannot be avoided. All roads lead to it. Lune put that into stone and metal and primordial fire and gave it to a king, and nobody since has understood what she gave him.

Hold this. It is a loaded gun. Fire it when the narrative earns it.
### Naaren Social Structure

THE SHAPE OF IT

Naaren’s social structure is the most deliberately engineered on the continent and the least honest about it. Every other kingdom’s hierarchy grew from something — Vel’Era’s from the queendom’s founding architecture, Halreth’s from the first king’s restraint, Varkaan’s from the military culture that built the highland civilization, Caltheran’s from the theological claim that legitimises the Council. Naaren’s hierarchy was designed. Not in a single act of legislation — no founding document declares the class structure of the Republic of Naaren — but through two centuries of institutional decisions that each made the next one inevitable. The genetic program concentrates magic in approved bloodlines. Magic determines economic access. Economic access determines institutional visibility. Institutional visibility determines who the republic considers a person worth administering. The chain is causal, each link producing the next, and nobody at any point had to declare that the unmagicked are lesser. The system simply ensures they are.

This is Naaren’s most significant social achievement and its most damning one. A kingdom that has built a permanent class hierarchy without ever formally acknowledging that the hierarchy exists.

THE INSTITUTIONAL CLASS

In Keth’var Bastion and to a lesser extent in Zenrenban and Nightshade Port, the institutional class and the civilian population occupy different physical space. Not walls. Not formal segregation in any legal sense. The separation is architectural and economic — the upper city at Keth’var, built on sorcerer-altered elevated ground, houses the Chancellor’s apparatus, the Cipher’s Guild facilities, the Warden offices, the house estates, and the infrastructure that administers the republic. The lower city at the Myrn river passage houses the commerce, the labour, the people who make the city function at the level of daily material reality. The upper city looks down on the lower city. This is not metaphor. The geography was transmuted to ensure it.

A Naaren noble walking from House Heimdall’s estate to a Guild briefing will not pass through the same streets as a fishwife carrying her morning catch to the lower market. Their paths do not cross because the city was built so they wouldn’t. The institutional class in Keth’var lives, works, socialises, and governs in a physical environment that the civilian population enters only when summoned or employed. Servants move between the tiers. Administrators move between the tiers. The people being administered do not, unless the administration requires their presence.

Zenrenban replicates this at a smaller scale — the Crystalline University and its associated research infrastructure occupy elevated ground above the coastal town that provisions and services the institution. Nightshade Port inverts it — the Guild’s surveillance infrastructure is built into the port itself, embedded rather than elevated, because Nightshade’s function is observation of what moves through the coastal lane, and observation requires proximity rather than distance.

THE PERIPHERAL REALITY

Move north and east from Keth’var and the separation dissolves — not because the republic chose egalitarianism, but because the republic chose not to reach that far.

Jenby, the interior road town, is functional rather than stratified. The permanent residents and the travellers passing through share the same infrastructure because the town exists to service the road, and the road does not distinguish between institutional and civilian traffic. The Cipher’s Guild has an observer rotating through the waystation’s administrative staff. That observer eats at the same tavern as the toll collectors. The surveillance is present. The physical separation is not.

The thermal zone settlements — Xenlonfon, the Halt, the Vents — exist outside the institutional-civilian framework almost entirely. Xenlonfon’s spa economy serves Naaren’s nobility and upper merchant class, but the settlement itself is run by people whose discretion is a professional skill rather than an institutional appointment. The Halt’s extraction workers and researchers share infrastructure and nothing else, their relationship defined by the work rather than by class. The Vents has no institutional presence at all. The republic lists a settlement reference number and has not replaced the colloquial name in four census cycles.

The Ashen Coast is the logical terminus of this gradient. Technically Naaren. Practically ungoverned. No guild presence, no mage institutions, no noble houses with holdings worth mentioning that far out. People on the Ashen Coast have more in common with Caltheran’s rural fringe than with anything coming out of Keth’var. The republic’s administrative reach ends long before it arrives there. Renu lives in a fishing village at the northeastern tip of a kingdom that has largely forgotten the tip exists. The people there fish. Strange things occasionally come up in nets. Nobody from the institutional class comes to check.

What this gradient produces is a republic where the social structure is two different things depending on where you stand. In Keth’var you live in a surveillance state with visible class separation, institutional architecture that physically elevates the governing class above the governed, and the Cipher’s Guild watching from positions everyone knows about and nobody challenges. On the Ashen Coast you live in a forgotten corner where the republic is an abstraction that occasionally sends a tax collector and otherwise leaves you alone. Both of these are Naaren. Neither recognises the other as the real one.

THE CIPHER’S GUILD AS SOCIAL FORCE

The civilian population knows what the Guild is. They are not hidden. They are not a secret police operating behind a fiction of non-existence. They are the Republic of Naaren’s intelligence service and everybody knows it.

This is worse than secrecy.

A secret police force can be denied, resisted, exposed. A known intelligence service that operates openly within the daily life of the republic cannot be resisted because resistance requires a target, and the Guild’s visibility makes it clear that the target sees you before you see it. The population of Keth’var lives under observation the way populations live under weather — it is present, it is known, it shapes daily behaviour, and nobody expects it to change because nobody alive remembers a time when it was different.

The social effect is not terror. Terror is expensive and unstable. The social effect is calibration. People in Naaren’s major cities calibrate their behaviour to the Guild’s presence the way people everywhere calibrate to social norms — automatically, unconsciously, without the daily conscious experience of fear. A merchant in Keth’var’s lower city does not think “the Guild is watching” before every transaction. He simply does not make certain transactions, does not say certain things in certain places, does not associate with certain people, because the understanding of what the Guild notices has been absorbed into his social instincts the way any environmental constant gets absorbed. The fear is not active. It is ambient. It has become culture.

This produces a specific Naaren social personality that other kingdoms find unsettling. Naaren’s people are not frightened. They are careful. They are precise in their language. They do not say more than they mean. They do not assume privacy in public spaces. They read rooms before they speak. Other kingdoms call this coldness or elitism. It is neither. It is the behavioural adaptation of a population that has been observed for two centuries and has developed the specific social skills that observation selects for — awareness, restraint, the ability to communicate meaning through what is not said rather than what is. Aeraa Wynvale was raised in this culture. Her ADHD manifests against this backdrop — the hyperfocus and restlessness of a mind that the Guild’s training tried to shape into controlled observation and partially succeeded.

The Guild’s senior operatives occupy a social position that has no equivalent in other kingdoms. They are not nobility. They are not military. They are not clergy. They are the people who know things, and in Naaren, knowing things is the most significant form of power available. A senior Guild operative at a Keth’var social function is treated with the specific deference that other kingdoms reserve for military commanders or religious authorities — not warmth, not admiration, but the careful respect of people who understand that the person they are speaking to could destroy them with information rather than force. The operative’s wealth is not conspicuously displayed because display is itself a surveillance risk. They dress well. They do not dress loudly. The restraint is functional and it signals status to everyone trained to read the signal.

THE NOBLE HOUSES

Naaren’s house system operates within a republic that theoretically does not recognise hereditary privilege. In practice the houses are the republic’s functional aristocracy — they hold domain expertise, institutional relationships, and accumulated wealth that the republican framework depends on while declining to formally acknowledge the dependency.

House Heimdall sits at the apex and knows it costs them. Riftmarked of Conjuration. Descendants of the hero Heimdall. Lord Kaelan inherited the mark through his mother Lady Cerys’s “state-sanctioned retirement” — a phrase the Noble House Registry uses without elaboration, which is itself an elaboration. The state decided when the Conjuration Riftmark would transfer. Not death. Not natural passage. A sanctioned event, managed by the republic, producing the transfer on institutional schedule rather than biological one. What “retirement” means for a Riftmarked woman in a surveillance state that controls magical inheritance as policy is a question the registry leaves for the reader to sit with.

Kaelan at the ENAA is treated with a mixture of fear and suspicion by other students. Not because of anything he has done — because of what his family represents. House Heimdall are Naaren’s “secret gatekeepers.” Conjuration specialists controlling trade routes through temporary gateways, capable of summoning arcane constructs for the republic’s defence. In a kingdom where the Cipher’s Guild is the visible intelligence apparatus, House Heimdall is the visible magical apparatus — the family whose powers serve the state’s strategic interests and whose Riftmark transfers at the state’s discretion. Other students look at Kaelan and see not a person but a system. His social isolation at the ENAA is the social structure of Naaren expressed in the specific loneliness of a young man whose kingdom made him what he is and whose peers hold him accountable for it.

House Cogswright is the industrial counterpart. Where Heimdall summons, Cogswright builds. The arcane automatons that guard the republic. The magi-technical engines that power Naaren’s cities. House Cogswright is the most economically productive major house in the republic and the most institutionally trapped — the treaty restrictions prevent export, the republic consumes their entire output domestically, and the expertise required to maintain the automaton network is proprietary to the house. They cannot leave. They cannot diversify. They are indispensable and imprisoned by the indispensability. Veridian Cogswright at the ENAA carries the weight of a house that builds the machines that watch the people, placed in the Black Dogs — the house of overwhelming power — where the irony of a builder among warriors is presumably not lost on him.

House Sterling holds the money. Lesser house in rank. Continental in reach. Their vaults finance the ambitions of every other house. Their power is the specific power of people who know what other people owe — and in Naaren, where information is the most significant currency, a house that holds financial information on every noble family in the republic holds leverage that transcends its formal ranking. Argent Sterling at the ENAA is in the Hollow Hearts, which is either a comment on the house’s emotional character or a coincidence the sorting mechanism produced. Sterling doesn’t advertise which.

House Menlarian produces the innovations. Scholars, inventors, artificers. Their patents and magi-technical output feed the republic’s institutional apparatus. Jamie Menlarian at the ENAA is in the Hollow Hearts — a brilliant, eccentric scholar of Artifice and history who Kris later coerces into forging documents. A house whose expertise is genuine, whose awareness that the Guild harvests their research before publication is variable, and whose most promising student is vulnerable to exactly the kind of exploitation that Naaren’s social structure produces in people whose value is intellectual and whose protection is institutional.

House Prism studies pure theory at the Crystalline University. They eschew politics for research, which in Naaren means they have chosen the one form of social withdrawal the republic permits — academic isolation that the Guild monitors without interfering because the research occasionally produces useful findings. Illya Prism at the ENAA is in the Hollow Hearts. A house of people who believe knowledge is an end in itself, operating within a republic that treats knowledge as an instrument. The sincerity of House Prism’s scholarly commitment is its social vulnerability — they genuinely don’t seek alliances, which means they have none when they need them.

House Gable builds the cities. Architects and city planners whose designs blend arcane principles with structural efficiency. In a kingdom where the physical architecture of the capital was designed to separate the institutional class from the population, the house that builds the architecture is the house that builds the separation. Whether House Gable understands itself as complicit in the republic’s social engineering or simply as practitioners of excellent design is a question the house has presumably answered internally and not shared externally. Mason Gable at the ENAA is in the Silver Bulls — the house of character and resilience — which places an architect of surveillance infrastructure in a house that values moral endurance. The tension is structural.

House Yldritch appears in the canon through two members — Slovyn Yldritch, the Naaren noble who searched for Renu across four kingdoms before finding her in Naaren’s own forgotten corner, and Xedrun Yldritch, who teaches Soldiery and Equestrian Arts at the ENAA. A noble house with members deployed across the continent in positions of institutional function — searching, teaching, operating at the intersection of Naaren’s interests and other kingdoms’ institutions. Their rank is not yet established in the Noble House Registry. Their presence in both the Renu Arc and the Kai Arc suggests a house whose reach exceeds what its formal position would predict.

THE GENETIC PROGRAM AS SOCIAL ARCHITECTURE

The program does not formally punish the unmagicked. There is no law in Naaren that declares non-magical citizens lesser. There is no statute that restricts their employment, their residence, their marriage options, their access to public services. The program simply ensures that magical ability — the single most significant determinant of economic mobility, institutional access, and social standing on the entire continent — is distributed according to the republic’s institutional priorities rather than according to chance.

The effect is compound. Generation one: the program concentrates attunement in approved bloodlines. Those bloodlines gain the economic premium that attunement commands — higher wages, institutional employment, Guild access, university admission. Generation two: the children of approved bloodlines inherit not just the magical potential but the economic and institutional position their parents’ attunement purchased. The non-approved bloodlines inherit the absence — not poverty necessarily, not persecution, just the specific condition of being non-magical in a world where magic is the primary currency of status. Generation three: the gap has widened enough that the approved bloodlines occupy the institutional class and the non-approved bloodlines occupy the civilian population, and the separation looks natural because nobody alive remembers a time when the distribution was different.

This is what two centuries of the program have produced. Not an underclass in the dramatic sense — not people in chains, not people formally declared inferior. An underclass in the statistical sense. The unmagicked in Naaren are poorer on average, less institutionally visible, less likely to hold positions that interact with the republic’s governance apparatus, and less likely to produce children who will change any of this. They are not oppressed. They are simply excluded from the mechanisms that would allow them to be anything else, and the exclusion happened before they were born.

The unmagicked don’t know they were excluded. They know magic is rare. They know some families produce mages and others don’t. They know the republic manages magical inheritance because the republic has always managed magical inheritance and the alternative — uncontrolled attunement, random manifestation, the chaos of a population where anyone might wake up casting — sounds dangerous when described the way the republic describes it. The program’s most effective product is not the mages it creates. It is the population that believes the program is protection rather than selection.

THE GENERAL POPULATION

Below the houses, below the Guild, below the institutional class — the majority of Naaren’s population lives in conditions that range from functional subsistence to the specific hardship of communities the republic has not bothered to reach.

The Keth’var lower city is the densest civilian concentration. People who work the river passage, the market infrastructure, the service economy that the upper city’s institutional class depends on. They are not destitute. They are employed, housed, fed. They are also observed, calibrated, and separated from the governance that determines their lives by an architectural elevation that was designed to keep them below. The lower city feeds the upper city. The upper city governs the lower city. The exchange is unequal and permanent.

Zenrenban’s civilian population services the Crystalline University. The Halt’s extraction workers cycle through on short rotations because extended exposure to the thermal zone carries documented health costs that the research operations at the same settlement document with professional interest. The extraction operations continue regardless.

The agricultural zones south of Keth’var produce what the republic eats. These communities are the closest thing Naaren has to Halreth’s Vespur Plains population — rural, functionally invisible to the capital’s institutional politics, sustaining the republic through labour the republic does not acknowledge in any form that resembles gratitude. Their relationship to the institutional class is one of provision without reciprocity. They grow the food. The food goes to Keth’var. What comes back is administration and observation.

THE PEOPLE WHO DON’T COUNT

The Ashen Coast. The Vents. The forest-border communities near Vel’Era where the silver trees bleed northward without asking permission and the republic’s administrative reach has thinned to nothing.

These are Naaren’s forgotten. Not persecuted — forgotten. The distinction matters because persecution requires attention, and attention is a resource the republic allocates according to institutional priority. The Ashen Coast has no institutional priority. The people there fish. They exist. They are technically citizens of a republic that has not sent a functional administrative presence to their settlement in living memory. Their relationship to the Republic of Naaren is the relationship of people who share a name on a map and nothing else.

The Vents settlement has no official name. The republic’s administrative records list a reference number. The people who live there chose it because of the distance it creates from everything south — hiding from specific things, hiding from the general condition of being findable. In Naaren, where findability is the fundamental condition of citizenship, choosing to be unfindable is choosing to stop being a citizen in every way that matters. The republic does not formally acknowledge what the Vents is. The people there keep no records. This is both true and policy.

Aeraa Wynvale is what happens when the institutional class produces someone and then deploys them outside the structure that made them. Raised in the Guild’s training programme, sent to Caer Seryth as a spy, turned by Lune into a double agent. She is a product of Naaren’s social structure who has stepped outside it, and her ADHD, her charm, her difficulty connecting emotionally, her desire for control in a life where she was never allowed to have it — these are the psychological products of a social system that shaped a child into an instrument and then discovered the instrument had preferences the programme didn’t account for. She is also a failed genetic experiment. Not all pathogenic interventions are fully successful. The lasting impacts mark her in ways the programme did not intend. Her body carries the republic’s ambitions and its errors simultaneously.

HOW THEY LIVE TOGETHER

They don’t.

That is the honest answer. The institutional class in Keth’var and the fishing communities on the Ashen Coast do not live together in any meaningful sense. They share a republic the way people on different floors of a building share an address — technically accurate, experientially irrelevant. The republic’s social contract is not a contract in the sense that both parties agreed to terms. It is a system that the institutional class designed, the civilian population inherited, and the surveillance apparatus maintains. The population does not rebel because rebellion requires organisation, organisation requires communication, and communication in Naaren happens under observation. The observation doesn’t need to prevent rebellion actively. It prevents the conditions that produce rebellion — private gathering, unmonitored conversation, the specific social trust that allows people to discover they share a grievance.

Two centuries of this have produced a population that is not oppressed in the ways other kingdoms would recognise as oppression. There are no chains. There are no public executions. There is no dramatic cruelty that would give the population a narrative to organise around. There is simply a republic where the people at the top designed the system, the people in the middle service it, the people at the bottom sustain it, and the people at the edges have been forgotten by it. The pressure accumulates without release because the architecture was designed to absorb pressure rather than resolve it.

Whether the architecture can hold indefinitely is the question. The civilian population is the poorest on the continent. The institutional class is among the wealthiest. The gap was designed to be permanent. Designed things, given sufficient time and sufficient pressure, break along the lines they were designed on. Naaren’s social engineers built something durable. They did not build something eternal. The difference between those two things is the fault line the republic sits on.


CANON LOCKED — The Magisters of Kaev’Vor

WHAT THEY ARE

The Magisters of Kaev’Vor are Naaren’s deepest institutional secret. They operate under the authority of the Magistrate, who operates under the Chancellor. Their chain of command is short, insulated, and invisible to every layer of the republic’s governance that is not directly above them. The Wardens do not know. The noble houses — with specific, rare exceptions — do not know. The Cipher’s Guild knows they exist the way the Guild knows everything, but the Guild’s operational relationship to the Magisters is one of parallel function rather than command. Two instruments serving the same republic, each aware of the other, neither fully controlling the other.

Their purpose is the enforcement of the genetic programme at the level the legislation does not reach.

The programme’s legal architecture controls which bloodlines are permitted to produce magical children. The legislation is clean. The statute is embedded in republican law. What the legislation cannot control is Eōtria herself — because Chroma chooses. Attunement is not perfectly heritable. The programme concentrates magical potential in approved bloodlines. It does not eliminate the possibility of spontaneous manifestation in unapproved ones. Children born to families outside the programme’s approved lines occasionally manifest magical ability anyway, because the planet’s magic does not read republican law before deciding who receives it.

The Magisters exist because those children cannot be allowed to remain where they are.

WHAT THEY DO

When a child manifests magical ability in a family the programme did not approve, the Magisters are deployed. The identification mechanisms are not fully established in the files — whether they operate through ambient detection networks, informant systems embedded in the medical infrastructure, or the specific observation practices of a surveillance state that has been looking for exactly this kind of anomaly for two centuries. What is established: they find the children. They are efficient at finding them. The programme has been running long enough that the identification process is institutional routine rather than emergency response.

What happens next is the republic’s most guarded secret and its most indefensible act.

The children are taken. Removed from their families. Inducted into the institutional class — absorbed into approved bloodlines through mechanisms that erase their origins so the programme’s narrative of controlled inheritance remains intact. A child who was born to a farming family south of Keth’var becomes, in the institutional record, a child who was always part of an approved house. The family that lost the child experiences the loss. They do not understand the mechanism that produced it. They do not know where the child went. They do not have institutional recourse because the institution that took the child is the institution they would appeal to.

Those who resist — parents who fight, families who hide, communities that protect — are dealt with. The historical record that exists within the Magisters’ own files includes accounts of infants taken from mothers by force. Mages killed for refusing to comply with induction. Entire families silenced through methods the files do not specify and do not need to, because the pattern is sufficient: people who interfere with the programme’s enforcement disappear from the institutional record the same way natural-born mages disappear from their families. Cleanly. Completely. Without the kind of evidence that produces the kind of questions the republic cannot afford.

The experimentation is the darkest layer. The programme’s pathogenic intervention — the biological mechanism through which attunement is induced or manipulated in subjects — requires refinement. Refinement requires subjects. Natural-born mages taken by the Magisters are not only inducted into high society. Some are studied. Some are subjected to the pathogenic process — a twisted, Chroma-based corruption of a technique whose primordial original exists in Vel’Era’s Druidic tradition, where young girls are transformed into Druids through a primordial pathogen that is biological transformation rather than magical awakening. What the Magisters do is the same process stripped of its spiritual context and rebuilt as institutional technology. The primordial version transforms. The Chroma version forces. The results are not always successful. The failures are not always survivable.

The scale of what has been done across two centuries is not established in the files because establishing it would require the kind of accounting the Magisters do not keep in accessible form. What two hundred years of this programme have produced — how many children taken, how many families destroyed, how many experimental subjects processed — is a number the republic has never calculated because calculating it would require looking at the full picture, and the full picture is the kind of thing that produces the uprising the programme exists to prevent.

AERAA WYNVALE

Aeraa is a product of the Magisters’ programme. Not a child taken from a farming family — a child given willingly by her own father.

Judas Wynvale is a major Naaren noble. House Wynvale in the Fifth Era held standing comparable to House Heimdall — significant, politically connected, institutionally powerful. Judas is attuned. His daughter was not. In a republic where attunement determines everything — economic access, institutional visibility, social standing, the specific currency of being a person the republic considers worth investing in — a non-attuned daughter of a major house is a problem that the house’s status makes more acute rather than less. A commoner’s non-attuned child is simply non-magical. A major house’s non-attuned child is a visible failure of the bloodline the programme was designed to maintain.

Judas gave Aeraa to the Magisters. He wanted her to be a mage. The pathogenic intervention was applied. It was not fully successful. Aeraa manifests abilities — she functions as a spy, she has skills the Guild’s training shaped — but the genetic mutations the pathogens induced left lasting impacts the programme did not intend. She is a partially successful experiment. Her body carries the evidence of what was done to her. She does not know what was done to her. She does not know her father authorised it.

By the time the reader meets Aeraa in Book 1, she is a sixteen-year-old Cipher’s Guild operative at Caer Seryth — bright blond hair, cool amber eyes, ADHD manifesting in hyperfocus and restlessness, trained in a cold elitist spy programme that prioritises obedience and detachment, charming when she wants to be, distrustful of authority, driven by a desire for control in a life where she was never allowed to have it. Every one of these traits reads differently once the Magisters context is known. The ADHD is not incidental. The distrust of authority is not personality. The desire for control is not adolescent rebellion. These are the products of a child whose father gave her to the state to be rebuilt, and the rebuilding was incomplete, and what emerged was a person who is simultaneously exactly what the programme intended and a living record of what the programme costs.

By the time the reader meets Judas at Caer Seryth, he is the Shepherd of the Order of the Wyrm. Polite. Weary. Articulate. A man who does not revel in destruction and views it as necessary rather than pleasurable. A man who looks like a disappointed father rather than a warlord.

He is a disappointed father.

His daughter is on the other side of the wall he is besieging.

Neither knows the full shape of what connects them. The reader will discover it in layers. The revelation, when it comes, will retroactively change every interaction both characters have had — Aeraa’s distrust of institutions that shaped her, Judas’s weary pragmatism about necessary costs, the specific way he told Elaine they would meet again. A man who gave his daughter to the Magisters and then joined the Order of the Wyrm is a man who has been making decisions about acceptable costs for longer than either organisation has employed him. The question the saga will eventually force the reader to answer is whether Judas was always this, or whether what he did to Aeraa is the thing that made him capable of becoming it.

THE OTHER KINGDOMS

They know. Or they suspect strongly enough that the distinction between knowing and suspecting is a courtesy they extend to themselves.

The Kymancy War ended in 1036 A.M. The Accords of Faelmr stripped Naaren’s military. Four kingdoms sat at the treaty table and negotiated terms. The genetic programme was already embedded in Naaren’s republican law by then — legislation, not decree, the kind of policy that survives regime change because it belongs to the state rather than the ruler. The four kingdoms that signed the Accords could have addressed the programme. They could have made its dissolution a condition of the peace. They did not.

Whether this was pragmatic calculation — the programme is Naaren’s internal affair, intervention would destabilise the peace — or something more uncomfortable — the programme produces a Naaren that is magically contained, militarily defanged, and internally focused on its own population rather than on external ambitions — is a question the treaty negotiators presumably answered privately and never committed to paper. The result is a continental order that permits what Naaren does to its own people because what Naaren does to its own people keeps Naaren predictable.

Halreth feeds Naaren. Halreth’s grain crosses the border into a republic that tears children from their mothers. The grain keeps moving. The Peace Weeks keep running. The Open Table at Paxville records what every kingdom’s representatives say and what they choose not to say. What Halreth’s representatives have chosen not to say about Naaren’s programme, across two centuries of Peace Weeks, is itself a record that House Quillan’s barristers have presumably noticed and filed.

The continent’s complicity is not dramatic. It is administrative. It lives in the treaties that were signed without certain clauses, in the trade relationships that continued without certain conditions, in the diplomatic conversations that happened without certain questions. The Magisters of Kaev’Vor operate in shadow. The continent’s decision to let them operate lives in the light, in the public record, in the specific silence of five kingdoms that chose comfort over confrontation and have maintained that choice for two hundred and three years.


### Vel’Era Social Structure

THE SHAPE OF IT

Vel’Era’s social structure is the oldest on Eòs in the sense that matters most — it has never been replaced. Other kingdoms have been conquered, restructured, had their ruling lines broken and rebuilt. Vel’Era traces an unbroken line from Parisia Arsenia to the current queen, and the social architecture that Parisia established has been layered over, adapted, and occasionally challenged but never fundamentally redesigned. What exists in 1239 A.M. is not a system that was imposed. It is a system that grew, slowly, from roots older than any other kingdom’s founding document.

This gives Vel’Era a quality the other kingdoms don’t share: its social hierarchy feels natural to the people living inside it. Not natural in the way that propaganda makes things feel natural — natural in the way that old trees feel natural. The roots are deep. The canopy is thick. The people underneath it have been breathing this air for so long that most of them cannot imagine a different atmosphere. Whether this makes Vel’Era the most stable kingdom on the continent or the most calcified one depends on which direction the pressure comes from.

THE CROWN TIER

House Arsenia sits above everything. The Riftmark of Enchantment passes within the bloodline at the bearer’s death. The queen who carries it rules with something no other monarch on Eòs possesses — visible proof that the divine chose her house. The other crowns rule by bloodline, by force, by institutional authority, by theological claim. Lysandria V rules because the planet itself said so, and the mark on her body is the signature.

Succession is matrilineal at the crown level. The throne passes through the women of the Arsenia line. Male Arsenias exist, hold positions, carry the family name — but the crown goes to the daughter. This is constitutional, not cultural preference. The founding documents specify it. A challenge to matrilineal succession at the crown level would require challenging Parisia’s founding architecture, which would require challenging the legitimacy of every queen who followed her, which would require challenging the Riftmark itself. Nobody has attempted this because nobody who has understood the implications has been willing to accept them.

Elara Arsenia is the heir. She attends the ENAA without the Riftmark — that passes only at her mother’s death. This produces the specific social position of a woman who is simultaneously the most important person in her kingdom’s future and the least empowered person in her kingdom’s present. Everyone at the ENAA knows what Elara will become. Nobody at the ENAA can treat her as what she will become without implying they wish her mother dead. Elara navigates this by being exactly what her mother raised her to be — sharp, controlled, strategic, and willing to wait.

The crown tier includes the queen’s immediate household, the arcane council that advises her, and the Silver Wardens who answer to her directly. These are the people who interact with the queen as a person rather than as an institution. Their social position derives from proximity to the crown rather than from house standing — a Silver Warden of common birth outranks most minor house scions in practical access and institutional authority. This is one of the few places in Vel’Era where the house system’s rigidity bends. It bends because the crown needs it to, not because the system was designed to accommodate flexibility.

THE HOUSE TIERS

Below the crown, Vel’Era’s houses are arranged in a hierarchy that is formally ranked and functionally absolute. Major, lesser, minor. The ranking is not decorative. It determines who speaks first at court, who sits where at the queen’s table, whose children are considered suitable matches for whose children, and whose opinions carry institutional weight in the specific, practical sense of whose letters get read by the arcane council and whose get filed.

The houses operate on mixed inheritance — titles and property can pass through either parent depending on the house’s internal customs. House Pevlium’s ducal title sits with Salyon, a man, which means the house’s internal succession allowed male inheritance at the senior level. House Tideborne’s hereditary admiralty has passed through both men and women across generations depending on which child demonstrated command capability. House Solara’s quarry holdings follow eldest-child inheritance regardless of gender. The crown is matrilineal. The houses are not required to be. This produces a kingdom where the ruling principle is female authority and the practical operation of the nobility beneath it is mixed — which is more politically sophisticated than either a purely matrilineal or a purely egalitarian system, because it allows the crown to embody the queendom’s foundational identity while the houses adapt to the practical realities of which heir is actually competent.

House Solara sits at the top of the non-royal hierarchy and knows it. Their sunstone quarries supply the components that make Furaha’s Fist’s weather-tracking work and the Great Lighthouses function. They are wealthy enough to be financially independent of the crown if they chose to be — and the fact that they have not chosen to be is itself a political calculation that Lysandria manages with the specific attention of a queen who knows which house in her kingdom could theoretically fund a challenge. Solara’s social position is apex-noble: they attend every function, influence every major policy discussion, and maintain the posture of loyal service while possessing the material resources to abandon that posture at a moment’s notice. The court watches them the way the court watches anything that is simultaneously indispensable and potentially dangerous.

House Pevlium occupies a tier that the standard ranking system doesn’t fully capture. They are one of the most powerful mage bloodlines on the continent — the grandmother held three pillars, the uncle advises the arcane council, the name carries weight in every kingdom. Their base is Aleisha’s Rest, an island off the coast, which gives them a specific social character: somewhat removed from the mainland court’s daily politics, self-sufficient in a way that island aristocracy develops when it has the resources to not need the continent constantly, and possessed of the maritime commercial access that makes their trading operation viable. In court terms Pevlium is advisory and commercial rather than territorial. In prestige terms they rival or exceed Solara among the magically informed. Whether a Solara or a Pevlium walks through a door first depends on who is watching and what the watching party values — money and material, or arcane legacy and counsel. The answer varies by room.

Kai’s position within this house is the social structure entry’s sharpest edge. A Pevlium who cannot cast. The ducal son who carries the name without the attunement that made the name what it is. The family sent him to be warded at House Harringold in Johanna’s Promise at age eleven — a placement that reads as education on the surface and reads as something more complicated underneath. A house that holds court sway, an arcane council seat, and a ducal title places a non-attuned child at the continent’s political centre with a specific calculation. Whether that calculation was protective, strategic, or an acknowledgment that Kai’s future lay outside Vel’Era’s mage-aristocracy hierarchy is a question the family has not answered publicly and Kai has not asked directly.

House Tideborne holds the hereditary admiralty of Furaha’s Fist. They are a lesser house in rank and a major house in functional importance — without Tideborne the navy doesn’t have a command tradition, and without the navy the Qunthren trade route doesn’t have a security guarantee. Their social position is the specific one of people whose work everyone depends on and whose rank doesn’t reflect that dependency. Tideborne scions at court functions sit below Solara and Pevlium at the table and above them in the esteem of anyone who has ever been on a ship. Their salt-worn heritage is a mark of pride within the house and a mark of class difference to the houses above them. The court smells like perfume. Tideborne smells like the sea. Both parties are aware of the distinction.

House Coralwind controls the enchanted coral alchemy trade — potions and arcane focuses derived from marine life, proprietary cultivation techniques, high-value low-volume goods that move through luxury channels. They are wealthy in the specific way that a house controlling a premium commodity is wealthy — not land-rich, not population-rich, but capable of purchasing influence at a level that their lesser house ranking doesn’t formally entitle them to. A House Coralwind focus purchased by a Caltheran archwarden costs more than anything in House Al-Khem’s desert pharmaceutical line. The buyers are the continent’s wealthiest and most magically sophisticated individuals. Coralwind knows who their clients are and what those clients’ purchasing habits reveal about their priorities. This is a form of intelligence the house has never formally weaponised and has never needed to.

House Azure is the cultural record. Minor house in rank, irreplaceable in function. They produce the sea-sagas, the coastal cartography, the paintings that document Vel’Era’s coastline with technical precision that serves documentary function rather than aesthetic ambition. Their social position is the one that cultural houses occupy in every kingdom — respected, underfunded relative to their importance, and perpetually aware that the things they preserve are valued by the court only when the court needs them and ignored the rest of the time. Cerulean Azure at the ENAA carries the name of a house that maps the world. Whether the world notices the mapmakers is a different question.

House Shanty is the exception that proves every rule about Vel’Era’s rigid house system. Common sailors. No bloodline, no land, no institutional history. Their founder defended the queen from an assassination attempt and was granted a minor title for it. The older houses look down on them. The crown protects them because the crown remembers what the founder did and because a house that owes everything to the monarchy is a house whose loyalty is structurally guaranteed.

Shanty’s existence does not indicate social mobility. It indicates the opposite — that the only path from commoner to noble in Vel’Era requires saving the queen’s life, and the house that walked that path is still looked down upon generations later. The crack they squeezed through closed behind them. Finn Shanty at the ENAA carries a title that half the student body considers illegitimate. He knows this. His house has known this since the ennoblement ceremony. They serve anyway, because the alternative is proving the older houses right.

House Spire keeps the Great Lighthouses. Reclusive. Duty-bound. The social position of a house that does vital, tireless, thankless work and has built its identity around the thanklessness rather than despite it. Spire doesn’t attend court functions frequently. Spire doesn’t seek alliance marriages aggressively. Spire maintains the beacons that make the Qunthren trade route viable and considers that sufficient. Pharon Spire at the ENAA is the representative of a family that has built its entire social identity around the idea that the work matters more than the recognition. Whether this is genuine humility or a form of institutional pride that has become indistinguishable from humility is a question the house would find irrelevant.

THE OLD-WAYS DIVIDE

The culture war between old-ways and twelve-deity Chroma tradition is not a class line in the formal sense. It does not appear on any ranking document. It does not determine house standing or institutional access. It is also the most significant social divide in Vel’Era beneath the house system itself, and it maps onto geography in ways that produce functional class consequences without anyone having to name them.

The deep forest interior and the areas around Silvanawr are where old-ways practice runs strongest. The capital itself was built among the silver-leaved arboreal spires — city and forest inseparable — and the old-ways tradition treats that integration as the forest accepting the city rather than the city imposing on the forest. The Druids operate invisibly in this zone. The primordial substrate is present and felt. Old-ways herbalists, oral tradition keepers, the practitioners who work with the forest rather than studying it from institutional distance — they concentrate here, in the deep interior where the silver trees are thickest and the Chroma influence is thinnest.

Moving outward from the deep interior toward Vel’Era’s borders — particularly toward the Halreth border in the northwest and the coastal settlements — the twelve-deity Chroma tradition becomes more visible. The coastal towns connected to the Qunthren trade interact with the continent’s institutional structures daily. Merchants, sailors, provisioners — people whose economic lives depend on the continental system rather than the forest — practice the twelve-deity tradition because the continental system runs on it. Temple to Uma in a harbour town. Temple to Galvinus where the shipwrights work. The institutional religion of a world that has moved past the primordial, adopted by people whose livelihoods require participation in that world.

The social consequence is not that old-ways practitioners are lower status. Some are — a deep-forest herbalist living in a subsistence economy is materially poorer than a coastal Chroma-temple merchant. But House Arsenia itself bridges both traditions. The queen knows the Druids exist. The court at Silvanawr maintains both Chroma-touched crystal and uncut living wood. The social consequence is subtler and more pervasive: old-ways practice marks you as interior, as traditional, as connected to the thing Vel’Era was before it became what it is now. Chroma practice marks you as continental, as institutional, as connected to the thing Vel’Era is becoming. Neither is officially lesser. Both are legible, immediately, to anyone who knows what to look for. And everyone in Vel’Era knows what to look for.

The inland-coastal economic divide that the Economics pass established maps imperfectly but meaningfully onto this. Coastal settlements experience Qunthren trade prosperity. Inland populations live in more subsistence-oriented agricultural and silvicultural economies. The old-ways tradition governs the relationship to the forest that the silvicultural economy depends on. The Chroma tradition governs the institutional relationships that the coastal economy depends on. A person’s spiritual practice in Vel’Era is not a private matter in the way it might be in Halreth’s Temple of Equanimity. It is a legible social signal that tells anyone watching where you come from, what economy sustains you, and which version of Vel’Era you believe in.

THE GENERAL POPULATION

Below the house system, Vel’Era’s commoner population distributes along the inland-coastal divide.

The coastal population is the more prosperous tier. Harbour towns, port settlements, provisioning infrastructure for Furaha’s Fist and the merchant fleets that transit the Qunthren. Queenswick — the naval port — has a civilian population whose entire economy exists because the fleet does. Shipwrights, rope-makers, provisioners, the families of naval personnel who built permanent lives around the fleet’s needs. These people are not noble. They are not house-affiliated. They are comfortable, employed, and connected to the continental trade economy in ways that give them material stability and institutional visibility. A skilled shipwright in Queenswick has more practical economic security than most minor house scions in the deep interior.

The inland population is the subsistence tier. Agricultural communities in the silver forest interior, silvicultural workers who harvest timber on the ritual calendar House Arbor — wait. That’s Halreth. Vel’Era’s forestry operates under old-ways Druidic constraint rather than house-managed ritual calendars. The inland communities live with the forest in a relationship that the old-ways tradition governs — when to cut, what to replant, which groves are untouchable, which paths are walked and which are not. This relationship is sustainable. It is also not commercially productive at a level that generates surplus wealth. The inland population produces enough to live. The coastal population produces enough to prosper. The gap between living and prospering is where Vel’Era’s class tension quietly accumulates.

The Qunthren islands add a third population tier — island communities connected to the Vel’Era crown but geographically separated from the mainland social hierarchy. The eight Vel’Era-held islands in the archipelago have their own internal social structures, their own economic relationships with the trade routes passing through their waters, and the specific character of communities that are technically part of a kingdom and practically their own world. Aleisha’s Rest — House Pevlium’s island — is the aristocratic version of this. The fishing communities on the smaller inhabited islands are the commoner version. Both exist at a remove from Silvanawr that makes the mainland social hierarchy feel abstract rather than present.

THE PEOPLE WHO DON’T COUNT

Every kingdom has them. Vel’Era’s version is quieter than most.

They are the people the forest border doesn’t protect. The silver trees bleed northward into Naaren’s territory without asking permission — the border is political, not ecological. People who live in the transition zone between Vel’Era’s deep forest and Naaren’s woodland exist in a jurisdictional gap. Vel’Era’s crown doesn’t administer them effectively. Naaren’s republic doesn’t claim them. They are forest-border people — culturally Vel’Eran, practically ungoverned, surviving on old-ways subsistence practices in territory that two kingdoms nominally share and neither actually manages.

The deep-interior communities nearest the Hollow Crown’s forbidden zone around Tyranu’s Watch are another category of the uncounted. The Silver Wardens maintain the boundary. The settlements nearby understand they exist because something must be watched. But the Wardens recruit for disposition rather than origin, train in isolation, and don’t explain themselves to the houses or to the civilian populations adjacent to their operations. The people who live in the Watch’s shadow live there knowing that the crown has placed something between them and something else, and that the something else is not discussed. Their social position is not poverty in the material sense — it is the specific condition of people whose proximity to a state secret has made them invisible to the state’s normal administrative functions. The crown sees the Wardens. The Wardens see the Hollow Crown. Nobody sees the families who live close enough to hear the Wardens’ dawn patrols and have learned not to ask where they go.

HOW THEY LIVE TOGETHER

Vel’Era’s classes coexist the way old growth forests coexist with the undergrowth beneath them. The canopy houses — Arsenia, Solara, Pevlium — receive the light. The mid-tier houses — Tideborne, Coralwind — grow in the filtered light below, prospering within the space the canopy allows. The minor houses — Azure, Shanty, Spire — occupy the lower layer, performing essential functions from positions that never break through to the upper canopy. The commoner population is the forest floor. The uncounted are the root system — invisible, essential in ways nobody examines, and the first thing to suffer when the soil goes bad.

The old-ways tradition tells Vel’Era that this arrangement is the forest’s will. The Chroma tradition tells Vel’Era that this arrangement is institutional wisdom. Both readings produce the same result: a population that accepts the hierarchy because the hierarchy has been present for longer than anyone can remember and both spiritual traditions confirm its legitimacy through different theological mechanisms.

What holds it together is the queen. Not through force — Vel’Era’s internal military apparatus is the Silver Wardens and Furaha’s Fist, neither of which is designed for domestic population control. Through the Riftmark. Through the visible, undeniable, biologically irrefutable proof that the gods chose this house to rule. Lysandria V does not need to justify her position. The mark justifies it. The social hierarchy that flows downward from her position inherits that justification at every tier — the houses serve the marked queen, the population serves the houses, and the entire structure rests on a foundation of divine sanction that nobody alive has the theological standing to challenge.

When Lysandria dies and the mark passes to Elara, the entire system will hold its breath for the transfer. If it passes cleanly, the hierarchy continues. If something goes wrong — if Eōtria chooses differently, if the bloodline fails, if the mechanics that have held for generations encounter something they weren’t designed for — then Vel’Era’s entire social order loses the foundation it has never needed to question.

That is Vel’Era’s deepest social fault line. Not the culture war. Not the inland-coastal divide. Not House Solara’s quiet wealth. The entire kingdom is one Riftmark transfer away from discovering whether its social contract was ever based on anything more than a divine signature that could be revoked.


###  Caltheran Social Structure

THE SHAPE OF IT

Caltheran’s social structure exists in two places at once. There is Caltheran the kingdom — Aedric Spire, Glintgate, the runewright guilds, the Council of Twelve governing from the Echoa Archive, a population that has grown alongside every other kingdom’s in the two centuries since the Kymancy War. And there is Caltheran the memory — ten fallen houses scattered across the continent, concentrated at the ENAA, carrying the names of institutions that no longer exist in the territory those names came from. Both are Caltheran. Neither is complete without the other. And neither fully acknowledges the other as the real thing.

This split is the defining feature of Caltheran’s social structure and the thing that makes it unlike any other kingdom on Eòs. Varkaan’s social tensions are internal — the Iskari question, the Guild-Crown stalemate. Naaren’s tensions are vertical — institutional class above, civilian population below. Vel’Era’s tensions are cultural — old-ways versus Chroma. Caltheran’s tension is existential. The kingdom that exists inside its borders is not the kingdom its displaced aristocracy remembers, and the kingdom the displaced aristocracy remembers no longer exists inside anyone’s borders.

THE COUNCIL CLASS

The Council of Twelve sits at the apex. Twelve Wardens and an Archwarden governing from a secret sanctum within the Echoa Archive in Aedric Spire. They wear identical Caltheran robes. They present coordinated public positions. They project the face of collective authority — twelve voices speaking as one, the Archwarden as primary voice, final say in practice though not in constitutional theory.

Their social position is theological rather than hereditary. The Council does not rule because they were born to rule. They rule because the Archive exists, the Archive sits on the most magically saturated ground in Caltheran, and the Council claims that the Chroma itself chose Caltheran as steward of arcane knowledge. This is the foundation of everything. It is also, as the Politics entry established, a lie — the Archive was built by Aethryn refugees carrying craft knowledge, not divine inheritance. Eleven of the Twelve believe the lie. The Archwarden knows the truth and has decided the lie is worth maintaining.

The social consequence of theological legitimacy is a governing class that behaves like clergy even when it governs like oligarchs. The Council’s internal culture is academic rather than martial or commercial. They study. They debate. They speak in the register of people who believe their authority derives from proximity to sacred knowledge rather than from the practical mechanisms of taxation and military force. The Artisan’s Quay in Aedric Spire — magically lit, open arcane craftsmanship on public display — is the Council’s social philosophy made visible: knowledge as spectacle, arcane mastery as cultural identity, a city that treats innovation as performance. The upper city within the original walled Spire is Council territory. The Outer — where the city actually lives — feeds the Spire and is governed by it, and neither is fully comfortable with the arrangement.

The divide between upper and lower in Aedric Spire is formally unacknowledged and universally observed. The walled Spire is institutions, old money, the political machinery. The Outer is markets, housing, the artisan culture that gives Caltheran its public reputation. A runewright apprentice in the Outer walks past the Spire’s walls every morning and understands that the walls are not there to keep the weather out. The upper city doesn’t need to enforce the separation because the architecture does it — the same principle as Keth’var Bastion’s elevated ground, achieved through stone instead of sorcery.

THE RUNEWRIGHT GUILDS

Below the Council and functionally independent of it, the guilds at Glintgate are Caltheran’s genuine social counterweight. They are not nobility. They are not Council appointees. They are a professional class whose authority derives from craft competence rather than theological claim, and whose institutional independence has been maintained for generations through the specific leverage of being economically indispensable while politically subordinate.

A Caltheran runewright guild master is one of the most socially interesting positions on the continent. He — or she, the guilds don’t restrict by gender — holds continental certification that carries weight in every kingdom. His expertise is purchased by monarchs, military commanders, and noble houses across Eòs. His economic output funds the capital he doesn’t govern. His professional reputation exceeds his political standing. He sits in Glintgate and watches the Council in Aedric Spire spend the wealth his work generates on theological projects he has no stake in, and his resistance to Council subordination is quiet, institutional, and grounded in the craft itself — he simply refuses to allow arcane practice to be redefined as religious observance.

The guild apprenticeship system is Caltheran’s most genuine form of social mobility. A glassblower’s daughter who demonstrates aptitude for rune theory can enter the apprenticeship track and, if she completes it, emerge with certification that gives her professional standing across the continent. The mobility is real. It is also contained within the guild structure — she becomes a guild artisan, not a political actor. She can prosper. She cannot govern. The guilds produce the wealth. The Council spends it. The relationship is understood by both parties and resented by the one that does the work.

Runehold Abbey sits in quiet tension with both the guilds and the Council. The monks who work there preserve rune theory in scholarly rather than commercial form. They are not entirely comfortable with the Council’s direction of the Archive — whether that discomfort includes awareness that the Archive’s founding was refugee craft rather than divine inheritance is unclear. If it does, that knowledge has been sitting in a clifftop scriptorium for generations, held by people who have not yet decided what to do with it.

THE COMMONER POPULATION

Caltheran has the smallest peasant population of the five kingdoms but it is not a small number. The post-Kymancy War population surge that hit every kingdom hit Caltheran too — two centuries of peace, abundant resources following the devastation, the specific demographic recovery that follows every major war. Death was widespread. The generation after filled the space the dead left. By 1239 A.M. Caltheran’s population is substantial.

The commoner experience in Caltheran is shaped by proximity to the guild economy. The kingdom’s primary economic output is knowledge-as-service — runewright certification, arcane consultation, the artisan culture that Glintgate produces and the Artisan’s Quay in Aedric Spire displays. This means Caltheran’s commoner population is more urban and more professionally oriented than the equivalent in Halreth or Varkaan. There are farmers — the Aldren corridor and the land between Glintgate and Aedric Spire produces food — but the cultural centre of gravity is artisan rather than agricultural. A Caltheran commoner is more likely to be a provisioner servicing a runewright workshop than a farmer tending grain. The economy is built around knowledge work, and the people who support that economy develop the specific social character of service populations in knowledge economies: aware of what the professionals above them do, respectful of the expertise, and quietly conscious that their own contribution — the cooking, the cleaning, the hauling of materials, the maintenance of the infrastructure the knowledge workers depend on — is essential and unrecognised.

Lentorum on the southern Aldren corridor between Glintgate and Aedric Spire is where the commoner Caltheran is most visible. A river city of accumulated centuries, markets that started as single stalls and became permanent districts, a bridge rebuilt four times carrying the evidence of all four versions. The city is not politically significant. It is functionally indispensable — where the Aldren’s commercial traffic pauses, where information moves between the guild engine at Glintgate and the political machinery at Aedric Spire, where the merchant families who understand the difference between significance and indispensability have been operating for generations.

Nuvolia on the northern Aldren corridor between Aedric Spire and Hudria carries a different commoner character — military origin, garrison-planned street grid, a city that periodically remembers its strategic purpose when the Varkaan border produces friction. The commoners here are closer to Varkaan’s frontier populations in disposition than to Glintgate’s artisan service class. They provision. They maintain. They watch the border and understand that the peace is a condition rather than a permanent state.

Marisport on the northern Ebonmaw coast is Caltheran’s most peripheral commoner population. Limited coastal trade, nobody sailing beyond the shelf, a port city that knows the sea it faces is not going anywhere good. The people there have the specific character of a population servicing an economy that was never going to be large — steady, modest, culturally distinct from both the guild culture at Glintgate and the theological culture at Aedric Spire.

THE DIASPORA

Ten houses. Scattered across the continent. Concentrated at the ENAA.

The Noble House Registry calls them the last, desperate hope of a scattered people to reclaim their history, honour, and homeland. That description is accurate and it is also the description the houses would give of themselves, which means it should be held at a slight distance. People who describe their situation as desperate and hopeful simultaneously are people who have built an identity around the condition of displacement, and that identity has been calcifying for as long as the displacement has lasted.

The Council of Twelve betrayed and purged the Neorxn royal line. The noble houses that had served the crown were displaced — not conquered by an external enemy but politically exiled by the institution that replaced their government. They lost their keeps, their territories, their institutional functions. What they kept: their names, their craft traditions, their internal hierarchies, and the specific social cohesion of people who share a grievance large enough to organise around.

The diaspora houses maintain a shared Caltheran national identity that is genuine and strategic simultaneously. The unity is real — the fallen houses consider themselves Caltheran before anything else, they maintain internal social structures and shared cultural practices, and the collective memory of what was lost functions as a political project that every house publicly supports. The unity is also a performance — beneath the banner of Caltheran restoration, ten houses pursue ten agendas. House Blackwood’s ambition is not House Norunlight’s scholarly preservation. House Stonehaven’s mercenary pragmatism is not House Gryphon’s elegiac attachment to a lost craft. The houses wave the same flag and mean different things by it. The flag is not a fiction. The agreement about what the flag represents is.

This produces a diaspora that functions effectively in public and fractures under pressure in private. At the ENAA the Caltheran student contingent presents a united front — they sit together, they advocate for each other in political disputes, they maintain the specific social cohesion of people who share a grievance large enough to organise around. Behind the front, Morgan Blackwood and Morgana Blackwood are fighting a proxy war for control of their house’s direction. House Silverstream’s bitterness about lost wealth sits uncomfortably beside House Furrow’s quiet acceptance of lost function. House Norunlight’s scholars and House Farsight’s rangers share a table and almost nothing else. The unity holds because the alternative — public fragmentation — would destroy the political leverage that is the only instrument the diaspora possesses. The day the leverage is no longer needed, or the day a crisis demands genuine collective sacrifice rather than coordinated performance, the question of whether the unity was ever more than strategic will receive an answer nobody in the diaspora is currently prepared for.

THE TEN HOUSES — SOCIAL LANDSCAPE

Regardless of which identity option holds, the ten houses arrange themselves in a hierarchy that mirrors what they were before the displacement and contradicts what they are now.

House Neorxn is invisible. The true royal line. Believed extinct. Wilhiem attends the ENAA as Wilhiem Neokròl, hidden in the Black Dogs, dismissed as a commoner. His name is being actively erased from historical record by the Caltheran Archwarden under Order of the Wyrm direction. The most important Caltheran on the continent occupies the lowest social position in the diaspora because his existence is a secret. Every other house’s claim to lead the Caltheran restoration is built on the assumption that the Neorxn line is dead. When that assumption breaks, every relationship within the diaspora restructures.

House Blackwood claims the apex by default. With Neorxn believed gone, Lord Blackwood — Morgan’s uncle — postures as the rightful leader of the Caltheran diaspora. The internal schism is the family’s defining social feature: Morgan at the ENAA in the White Bears, his uncle’s chosen representative, ambitious and dangerous. Morgana sent separately by the cadet branch as a check on Morgan’s power, operating in the Raven’s Watch alongside Kai and Elara. Two Blackwoods at the same institution representing two factions of the same house, each claiming to be the true direction of Caltheran’s future. The traditional Blackwoods who remained at Blackwood Pass — nature-bound, primal magic practitioners, raw where the Spire branch is precise — are a third faction nobody in the diaspora talks about because acknowledging them would require acknowledging that the house’s identity split long before the exile.

Morgana’s social position within the diaspora is the most complex. She is the most politically capable person in the Caltheran student contingent, operating as a double agent within Daruun’s faction, managing her cousin Morgan’s recklessness, and carrying the knowledge that her house’s claim to lead the diaspora is built on ambition rather than right. She is the person most likely to succeed at the political game the diaspora is playing and the person most aware that the game may not be worth winning.

House Stonehaven was the shield. Mountain pass guardians. Now solemn warriors without a kingdom to protect, their members serving as elite mercenaries, clinging to a rigid code of honour. Garrick Stonehaven’s death in the Purge is the house’s social condition made literal — a defender who died defending nothing that was his, in a trial designed by an institution that doesn’t care about what his house lost. The Stonehavens at the ENAA carry their grief in their posture. They are the diaspora’s moral weight — the house that reminds every other house what displacement actually cost.

House Halvyr (Caltheran Branch) complicates everything. A cadet branch of the ruling dynasty of the entire continent, choosing to identify as Caltheran rather than as Halvyr. Fiercely independent. Suspicious of their High Kingdom cousins. Rhys Halvyr in the White Bears carries two names simultaneously — one that rules the continent and one that lost its kingdom. His social position is the permanent discomfort of a man who could claim association with the most powerful family on Eòs and instead claims association with a fallen kingdom’s displaced aristocracy. That choice is either profound loyalty or profound stubbornness, and the distinction matters less than the fact that he made it.

House Gryphon is the relic. War-gryphon trainers with no aeries, a craft that is more memory than practice, a living reminder of something beautiful that was destroyed. Alise Gryphon in the Black Dogs is what happens when a house’s defining function no longer exists — she is a hunter and a beastmaster because her family’s tradition encoded those skills, but the specific thing her family was famous for — the war-gryphons, the legendary creatures, the aeries in the mountain passes — is gone. She carries the skills without the context that gave them meaning. The diaspora respects House Gryphon the way people respect relics — with a reverence that is itself a form of distance.

House Norunlight carries the history. Scholars and loremasters who managed the Royal Archives, now preserving their kingdom’s memory in scattered scrolls and oral tradition. Elian Norunlight in the Hollow Hearts is the keeper of a record that the Order of the Wyrm’s Archwarden is actively working to make irrelevant. The erasure campaign targeting the Neorxn name runs through the same archival channels that Norunlight’s institutional memory predates. If any house in the diaspora is positioned to notice that Caltheran’s history is being revised — that records are shifting, that references to House Neorxn are becoming thinner, that the institutional memory they carry contradicts what the Archive now says — it is Norunlight. Whether they have noticed, and what they are doing about it, is an open thread.

House Silverstream carries the loss. Mithril mines, pristine rivers, legendary wealth — all gone. A title without a treasury. Their name is a painful reminder of what they had and do not have. Coralia Silverstream in the Raven’s Watch is a woman whose family name is synonymous with wealth she has never touched. The social experience of inherited grandeur without inherited resources produces a specific kind of person — either bitter, or determined, or both, and the ratio shifts depending on the day.

House Farsight are the wanderers. Rangers and scouts who patrolled the wilderness, now without a wilderness to patrol. Gunther Farsight in the Hollow Hearts is a tracker whose kingdom no longer has borders for him to watch. His skills are intact. His purpose is not. The house has adapted to displacement by leaning into the displacement itself — they wander because wandering is what scouts do when the thing they were scouting for has been taken.

House Ashfield carries the land’s grief. Once-fertile heartlands now blighted and dead. They are the only diaspora house whose loss is ecological rather than political — they didn’t just lose territory, they lost living ground. Terra Ashfield carries what the registry calls “the grief of the land itself, a tangible sorrow that clings to their name.” Whether the blight that killed their heartlands is natural, arcane, or connected to the Council’s theological malpractice is an open question.

House Furrow carries the function. Masterless servants who maintained the great keeps and castles. Their keeps are ruins. Their knowledge of fortifications — how to build them, maintain them, defend them — is intact and useless. Piers Furrow is a steward without a house to steward, which is the diaspora’s condition distilled to its purest form — expertise perfectly preserved for a context that no longer exists.

HOUSE FARRYN — THE INDIFFERENCE

House Farryn governs Hudria. The city where Uthred and Johanna began. The original keep of the founders of the Promise, now held by a house that took the seat because six other claimants died in the War of Copper Kings and Han Farryn was the one who survived.

The diaspora houses do not consider Farryn their enemy. They do not consider Farryn their obstacle. They do not consider Farryn at all. Hudria is a border city. It is not the cultural heart of what was lost. The Echoa Archive is. Aedric Spire is. The walled Spire where the Council sits and the Outer that feeds it — that is the Caltheran the diaspora houses remember and want to reclaim. Hudria is a city that has changed hands enough times that its identity split between Varkaan and Caltheran centuries ago. Farryn governs it competently. The diaspora acknowledges this the way you acknowledge weather — it exists, it doesn’t require a response.

This indifference is itself a social fact. The fallen houses of Caltheran have maintained their identity, their grief, their internal hierarchies, and their political project across generations of exile. They have done so by deciding what matters and what doesn’t. Hudria doesn’t matter. The Spire matters. The Archive matters. The names they carry and the names being erased from the record — those matter. Everything else is geography that belongs to someone who isn’t them.

HOW THEY LIVE TOGETHER

Inside Caltheran’s borders: the Council above, the guilds beside, the commoner population below. The theological class governs from sacred ground. The professional class produces the wealth from commercial ground. The commoner class services both from ground that neither tier looks at directly. It is a cleaner hierarchy than most — fewer layers, less distance between the top and the bottom, the specific compression of a knowledge economy where the professional class is large and the agricultural base is small. A Caltheran commoner is closer to the guild class than a Halreth farmer is to the Remnus crown. The gap is narrower. The gap is still a gap.

Outside Caltheran’s borders: the diaspora carries a portable version of the hierarchy that no longer has a physical home. The houses maintain their ranks — major, lesser, minor — in a system that was defined by territorial function they no longer perform. House Blackwood was the throne’s rival. House Stonehaven guarded the passes. House Gryphon trained the war-gryphons. None of these functions exist in exile. The hierarchy persists anyway because the hierarchy is all they have left. The ranks are memory dressed as structure, and the structure is real enough that Morgan Blackwood and Morgana Blackwood and Wilhiem Neorxn and every other Caltheran student at the ENAA organises their social world around it.

The two Caltherans — the kingdom and the diaspora — live together by not living together. The Council governs the territory. The diaspora carries the identity. The Council’s legitimacy rests on a theological claim the diaspora’s scholars may have the institutional memory to challenge. The diaspora’s political project rests on a royal line the Council is actively erasing. They are two halves of a kingdom that was broken by betrayal, and the betrayal is still the organising principle of both halves’ social structure — the Council maintains power because the betrayal succeeded, and the diaspora maintains identity because they refuse to accept that it did.

When Wilhiem surfaces, both halves will have to answer the same question: what is Caltheran now? The Council will answer that Caltheran is the Archive, the theological claim, the institution that has governed for centuries. The diaspora will answer that Caltheran is the names, the memory, the royal line that was supposed to be dead. Wilhiem himself will have to answer it standing between both, carrying a Riftmark that the Council’s theology cannot explain and a name that the diaspora has been waiting for without knowing it still existed.

That is Caltheran’s deepest social fault line. Not the Council-guild tension. Not the upper-lower divide in Aedric Spire. The question of what the kingdom actually is, held open by a betrayal that is still active in both directions — the Council betraying history by erasing it, and the diaspora betraying the present by refusing to accept it.

That’s a rich combination. Halreth is the kingdom where farmers are genuinely prosperous because the economy is built on what they produce, religion saturates social life with the crown functioning as a religious figure, House Thorne is accepted because the philosophy is mature enough to acknowledge reality, and cultural prestige through the Peace Weeks and salons is the primary social currency. This is the most functional society on Eòs and the one that will be hardest to write without making it sound like propaganda. Let me find the edges.

### Halreth Social Structure

THE SHAPE OF IT

Halreth is the kingdom that works. This is the hardest thing to write about it honestly because a kingdom that works sounds like a kingdom that hasn’t been examined carefully enough. But the Economics pass established that Halreth’s wealth distributes more broadly than any other kingdom on Eòs. The Politics pass established that the Remnus crown concentrates power upward through function rather than feudal obligation. The Religion pass established that the Temple of Equanimity honours all thirteen deities equally under one roof. And the foundational economic fact — Halreth feeds a significant portion of the continent — means the people who produce the kingdom’s wealth are the people the kingdom cannot afford to impoverish.

This is not idealism. It is arithmetic. An agricultural economy requires a functioning rural population. A kingdom that depends on its farmers for the continental leverage that grain provides cannot treat those farmers the way Naaren treats its civilian population, because broken farmers produce broken harvests and broken harvests destroy the diplomatic instrument the entire kingdom is built on. Halreth’s equitable social structure is genuine. It is also structural necessity disguised as virtue, which is either a cynical reading or an honest one depending on whether you believe that necessity and virtue are mutually exclusive.

The founding king understood this. The leyline secret — the weapon beneath the Vespur Plains that every Remnus monarch inherits and chooses not to use — was the first decision. The second decision was what to build instead of a weapon. Thelranius Remnus IV inherited the answer: a kingdom where the people who grow the food are treated well enough to keep growing it, where the cultural institutions that project soft power are funded well enough to keep projecting it, and where the philosophy of restraint has been maintained long enough that most of the population has internalised it as identity rather than policy.

THE CROWN AS RELIGIOUS FIGURE

The Remnus monarchy is not merely a political institution. In Halreth, the crown carries religious weight.

The Temple of Equanimity in Theralion — all thirteen patron deities honoured equally, each with a dedicated altar, each attended by its own order of clergy — is the kingdom’s spiritual centre, and the monarch’s relationship to that temple is not one of patronage but of embodiment. The Remnus monarch is not a priest. The Remnus monarch is the living expression of the principle the temple was built to serve: equanimity itself. Balance between the thirteen. Restraint in the face of power. The choice to honour all rather than elevate one.

This means the crown’s social position in Halreth is qualitatively different from every other kingdom’s. Berthold in Johanna’s Promise is a political figure. Lysandria in Vel’Era is a divine instrument through the Riftmark. The unnamed Varkaan king is a military administrator. Naaren’s Chancellor is an executive. The Council of Twelve are theological oligarchs. Thelranius Remnus IV is something closer to what medieval Europe’s ideal of a Christian king was supposed to be — a ruler whose authority is inseparable from his spiritual function, whose governance is understood by his population as an expression of divine principle rather than merely political competence.

The population looks up at the marble cliffs of Theralion City and sees not just a capital but a sacred site. The crown sits at the Temple’s highest point. The architecture was designed so that light from the Palecrest Sea enters through western windows and illuminates different deity chapels at different times of day. The building manages time and meaning simultaneously, and the monarch who sits within it manages the kingdom within the framework that the building establishes. Religion in Halreth is not a private matter appended to governance. It is the governance’s foundation, the population’s shared language, and the social glue that holds the hierarchy together without the hierarchy needing to enforce itself through the mechanisms other kingdoms require.

This is why Halreth’s farmers are well-treated — not only because the economy requires it, but because the religious culture tells every Halrethian that the people who work the land are performing a sacred function. Terra is the patron of Life and Transmutation. The Harvest Vow is an annual religious event. House Arbor maintains a ritual calendar governing when cutting is permissible in the Greatwood and what must be replanted. The relationship between the population and its labour is sanctified in a way that Naaren’s extraction, Varkaan’s military industrialism, and Caltheran’s knowledge economy never attempt. A Halreth farmer is not a peasant. A Halreth farmer is a participant in a religious economy, and the distinction is felt daily.

THE HOUSE TIERS

Halreth’s houses serve the crown through function. This was established in the Politics pass and it determines the social structure from the top down — the houses are machinery, the crown is the hand. But the machinery in Halreth has a specific character that differs from every other kingdom’s version: the houses do not compete for the crown’s favour because the system was not designed to produce competition. Each house holds a domain. Each domain is necessary. The hierarchy between domains exists but is not the primary organising principle of daily life. A House Arbor ranger and a House Serene poet do not occupy the same social space, but they do not occupy adversarial social space either. They serve different functions within a system that values all functions as contributions to the whole.

House Remnus at the apex. Riftmarked of Abjuration — the defensive mark, protection magic, the pillar most aligned with Halreth’s founding philosophy. Thea Remnus at the ENAA is the bloodline heir carrying a mark that reflects what her kingdom believes: that the highest form of power is the power to protect without attacking. Her social position within the ENAA is diplomat first, princess second — she embodies her kingdom’s ideals of peace through strength and her defensive magic is described as second to none. She is the most respected royal heir at the academy who is not also the most feared, which is a distinction only a Halreth heir could carry.

House Veyne holds the Restoration Riftmark and the healer tradition. Major house. Seraphina’s mother died from a wasting sickness — the tragic irony of a Restoration Riftmarked dying from illness that her own mark should theoretically have addressed. That irony is the house’s social condition in miniature: they are defined by their ability to heal, and the thing that defines them is also the thing that makes their failures most visible. In Halreth’s religious culture, where healing and renewal are sanctified through Uma’s temples across the Vespur Plains, House Veyne’s social position is close to sacred. They are not just a noble house. They are the kingdom’s most visible expression of the principle that life should be preserved.

House Arbor manages the Greatwood and the agricultural infrastructure. Major house. Briar Arbor at the ENAA is a ranger and a hunter — skills that Halreth’s pacifist philosophy accommodates because the Greatwood requires management and the kingdom’s borders require awareness. The ritual calendar that governs forestry — when to cut, what to replant — means House Arbor operates at the intersection of economic function and religious observance. They are stewards in the original sense: people who tend something that was there before them and will be there after them, and the tending is the service.

House Llyr commands the defensive navy and oversees maritime trade on the western coast. Lesser house. The Palecrest Sea is calm and luminous — Halreth’s relationship with its ocean is cooperative rather than adversarial, which gives House Llyr a social character distinct from Vel’Era’s Tideborne. Llyr doesn’t fight the sea. Llyr works with it. Caspian Llyr at the ENAA carries the pragmatism and worldliness of a maritime house in a kingdom that values peace — he understands international treaties and complex negotiations because the sea requires them.

House Thorne guards the dangerous borders. Lesser house. The necessary anomaly in pacifist Halreth — a martial family in a kingdom that defines itself by not being martial. The social treatment of House Thorne is the test of whether Halreth’s philosophy is real or performative, and the answer is that it’s real: Halreth has accepted that even pacifists need guards. Thorne is not stigmatised. Thorne is not treated as the dirty secret that makes the clean philosophy possible. Thorne is integrated — respected for doing work the kingdom acknowledges is necessary, honoured for doing it without the kingdom needing to become something it doesn’t want to be. Bram Thorne at the ENAA carries this integration in his person — a martial student from a pacifist kingdom who does not experience the contradiction as a contradiction because his kingdom resolved it generations ago.

This is genuinely unusual on Eòs. Most kingdoms that espouse one philosophy and require another for survival handle the contradiction through denial, stigma, or compartmentalisation. Halreth handles it through acceptance, and that acceptance is itself the philosophy working correctly — equanimity means holding contradictions without pretending they aren’t there.

House Quillan keeps the law and the history. Lesser house. Scribes, barristers, archives. Their influence is subtle and foundational — they draft treaties, they maintain records, they administer the Open Table at the Peace Weeks where everything said is written down and nothing written is ever forgotten. House Quillan’s social position is the specific one of people whose power is archival rather than active: they do not make the decisions, they record them, and the recording is itself a form of power that most people underestimate until they need something from the archive and discover that Quillan remembers what they said twelve years ago. Orianna Quillan at the ENAA is in the Hollow Hearts.

House Serene manages culture. Minor house in rank, major house in influence. The salons in Theralion host the finest poets, musicians, and painters on the continent — not because House Serene commissions propaganda but because they pay well and ask only for the work. No political commissions. No institutional direction. The salon tradition is one of the few spaces on Eòs where art exists for its own purposes. This makes Halreth’s cultural output disproportionately influential relative to its military weight, and it makes House Serene’s social position the proof of the continental overview’s claim that cultural prestige functions as social currency in Halreth the way military prestige functions in Varkaan. A House Serene invitation to perform at a salon is the Halreth equivalent of a House Daruun invitation to serve in a campaign — the currency is different, the weight is comparable. Lyra Serene at the ENAA carries this.

House Weaver produces the enchanted textiles. Minor house that rose from a guild of artisans — one of the few examples of craft-to-nobility mobility in Halreth’s history, which tells you both that the path exists and that its rarity makes it notable. Their wares are coveted across the continent. A House Weaver robe at a Varkaan noble’s table is a status marker that functions as luxury, utility, and cultural export simultaneously. Penelope Weaver at the ENAA is in the Raven’s Watch.

House Scribe is the administrative backbone. Minor house that earned its place through merit — diplomats, negotiators, historians. Elias Scribe at the ENAA is in the Raven’s Watch, a scholar and strategist whose house’s tradition of quiet administrative competence produced a young man brilliant enough to be selected as a champion and eccentric enough to become obsessed with a cosmic grimoire. House Scribe’s social position in Halreth is the specific one of people who are necessary in the way that foundations are necessary — nobody looks at them, everyone stands on them.

THE PEACE WEEKS AS SOCIAL INSTITUTION

The Peace Weeks at Paxville are where Halreth’s real social power operates. Not the crown’s power — the kingdom’s power, expressed through the specific instrument of a public festival that every other kingdom participates in.

The formal debates. The oratory competitions. The theatrical performances commissioned by House Serene. The Open Table where any participant regardless of kingdom or rank may sit and be heard on any grievance, with House Quillan’s barristers recording everything. The festival is constitutionally protected — no Halreth monarch can cancel it unilaterally. It was suspended during the Kymancy War and restored afterward, which means every person alive at the restoration had grown up in a world without it. A festival that had to be rediscovered rather than simply resumed.

The social function of the Peace Weeks extends beyond diplomacy. Within Halreth, the festival is where the kingdom’s internal social hierarchy is most visible and most permeable. A farmer who attends the Open Table and speaks well is heard by the same barristers who record the words of kings. A poet who performs at the festival stage and moves the audience enters House Serene’s orbit regardless of birth. The Peace Weeks are Halreth’s annual demonstration that cultural participation — speaking, performing, recording, witnessing — is the social currency the kingdom values most. You do not need to be noble to participate. You do not need to be attuned. You need to be present and to have something worth saying.

This is Halreth’s most genuine form of social mobility and it operates on a principle no other kingdom shares: that the ability to speak, to argue, to create, to witness — these are qualifications for social participation in the same way that attunement is a qualification for institutional access elsewhere. Halreth cannot eliminate the shadow hierarchy of magic-as-class that operates across the entire continent. What it has done is build a parallel social economy where the currency is cultural rather than arcane, and that economy is large enough and prestigious enough that participation in it constitutes a genuine alternative to the attunement-based hierarchy.

Not an equal alternative. The Riftmarked still outrank everyone. The houses still hold institutional authority. The crown still governs from sacred marble above the sea. But a Halreth farmer who speaks at the Open Table and is recorded by House Quillan has done something that a Naaren commoner, a Varkaan highland labourer, a Caltheran provisioner, and a Vel’Era inland subsistence worker cannot do in their respective kingdoms: participate in the social life of the kingdom as a voice rather than as a function.

THE GENERAL POPULATION

Halreth’s farmers are prosperous. Not wealthy — prosperous. The distinction matters because prosperity means sufficiency with dignity, not accumulation with ambition. A winery owner in the Vespur Plains, a grain farmer along the Renavon, an orchard keeper in the valley regions near Vespine’s Cradle — these people produce what the kingdom sells to the continent and they receive enough of the return that their standard of living is meaningfully above subsistence. Not by Theralion standards. By the standards of anyone on Eòs who grows food for a living, Halreth’s agricultural population lives well.

The religious culture reinforces this. The Harvest Vow — annual, public, the crown and the farmer formally acknowledging the land’s yield — is not a ceremony the farmer observes from a distance. The farmer participates. The offerings to Terra happen at local temples across the Vespur Plains. House Arbor’s stewards coordinate with crown representatives. The public accounting of the year’s surplus is not an administrative exercise performed above the farmer’s head — it is a shared ritual in which the farmer’s contribution is named, honoured, and recorded.

The Chroma-Hares bred on the eastern plains near Renaford are exported as magical weather indicators. The Windcattle whose bone and fat conduct enchantments are traded continent-wide. The Runelight Rain that falls over the plains — the grass holding faint luminescence until dawn — is treated as ordinary by locals and unsettling by visitors. The specific character of Halreth’s rural population is people who live in a landscape saturated with low-level arcane phenomena and have domesticated their relationship to it. They don’t fear the magic in the soil. They farm it. The leylines run deep beneath the bedrock and the common belief is that something buffers the effect, and the farmers have built their practice around that belief without needing the Caltheran scholars to explain it to them.

Old Thenbury at the base of Theralion’s cliffs is the other pole of Halreth’s commoner experience — maritime rather than agricultural, fishing and trade and dockwork, the specific prosperity of a port town that was there before the capital and has never entirely acknowledged the capital’s authority over it. The two-hour climb from Old Thenbury to Theralion’s sea gates means news from the sea reaches the port first and travels up the cliff at whatever pace the port decides is appropriate. Old Thenbury merchants operate in a commercial register the Theralion nobility finds slightly vulgar and completely necessary. They are Halreth’s most independent commoner population — not rebellious, not disaffected, simply possessed of the specific confidence that comes from being the thing the capital sits on top of.

THE PEOPLE WHO DON’T COUNT

Halreth has fewer of them than any other kingdom, and the ones it has are easier to miss because the kingdom’s general equity makes the edges harder to see.

The eastern frontier near Renaford is where Halreth’s social structure thins. The leylines push closer to the surface as the Vespur Plains narrow eastward. Certain sensitive individuals feel them as a low persistent pressure behind the eyes. Nobody talks about this in formal company. The population here skews toward people with specific reasons to live at the edge of the safe country — rangers, frontier traders, families of those who work the Shadowfen border, scholars studying leyline activity without Caltheran’s institutional backing or Halreth’s institutional funding. House Thorne maintains a small permanent garrison. The garrison is understated for a frontier post. Whether it is sufficient is a question that hasn’t been tested.

The people on Halreth’s eastern frontier are not forgotten the way the Ashen Coast is forgotten in Naaren. They are present in the kingdom’s awareness as a boundary condition — the place where the safe country ends and something else begins. They are administered, garrisoned, and acknowledged. They are also the population most likely to discover, firsthand, what happens when the leylines that everyone believes are safely deep turn out to be closer to the surface than the common belief accounts for.

Small communities near Renaford maintain practices that scholars identify as Vak’tirī adjacent — probably absorbed from Vel’Era’s border populations over generations. It isn’t organised or self-identified as old faith worship. It is the specific spiritual residue of a border population that lives close to something the kingdom’s institutional religion doesn’t fully account for, and has quietly developed its own relationship with what it feels underfoot.

HOW THEY LIVE TOGETHER

Better than anywhere else on Eòs. That is the honest answer and it is also the answer Halreth would give about itself, which means it should be held with the same slight distance as any self-assessment.

The crown governs from sacred ground. The houses serve through function. The professional class operates within domains the houses administer. The farmers produce the wealth and receive enough of the return to maintain dignity. The cultural institutions provide an alternative social currency that operates alongside the attunement-based hierarchy. The religious culture sanctifies the labour that sustains the economy. The Peace Weeks provide an annual mechanism for participation that transcends class. The philosophy of restraint — inherited from a founding king who chose not to weaponise a continent-ending power — has been maintained long enough to feel like nature rather than policy.

What holds it together is the specific combination of economic necessity and religious culture. The farmers are treated well because the economy requires it. The economy requires it because Halreth chose to build its continental leverage on food rather than force. The choice is sanctified by the religious tradition. The religious tradition is embodied by the crown. The crown sits in a temple that manages light and meaning simultaneously. The whole system is circular, self-reinforcing, and remarkably stable.

The fault line is the one the Politics pass identified: it is all one monarch’s death away from changing. The leyline secret passes from monarch to monarch. The restraint is a choice, not a constraint. A future Remnus heir who reads the founding document and reaches a different conclusion — who decides the weapon is worth using — doesn’t just change Halreth’s military posture. They change what Halreth is. The social structure that flows from the philosophy of restraint collapses if the philosophy is abandoned, because the religious culture, the economic model, the cultural institutions, and the population’s internalised identity are all downstream of a decision that has been remade by every monarch and could be unmade by one.

Halreth’s people live well because their kingdom chose well. They live well because the choice has been sustained. They live well, and the thing that sustains their living well is a secret they don’t know about, held by a person they trust, renewed by a judgment call they have no input into. That is Halreth’s social structure at its deepest: a genuinely good kingdom whose goodness depends on a single inherited decision remaining the same forever.


### Varkaan Social Structure

THE SHAPE OF IT

Varkaan is the largest kingdom on Eòs and the one most likely to destroy itself from the inside. Not through weakness — through the specific structural contradictions of a civilisation that was built by conquest on stolen ground, sustained by an industrial base it doesn’t fully control, governed by a military aristocracy whose apex house holds both the crown and a Riftmark that don’t sit in the same person, and is currently debating in council chambers whether to exterminate the people who were there before the kingdom existed.

Every other kingdom’s social structure can be described as a hierarchy. Varkaan’s social structure is better described as a set of competing hierarchies that have been held in productive tension for centuries and are now approaching the limits of what productive tension can sustain. The crown, the Guilds, the highland aristocracy, the desert houses, and the Iskari substrate population each operate according to different organising principles, different loyalties, and different definitions of what Varkaan actually is. They coexist because the alternatives to coexistence have historically been more expensive than the tension. Whether that calculation holds through the purge question is the social structure entry’s central concern.

THE CROWN AND THE MARK

House Daruun is the royal line. They are also Riftmarked of Evocation — direct descendants of the hero Thor, commanding the most powerful military house on the continent. The crown and the Riftmark both belong to the Daruun bloodline. They do not belong to the same Daruun.

The current king — Valerius’s uncle, unnamed in the files — holds the throne. He does not hold the Riftmark. Valerius’s father held the Riftmark. He fell in a border skirmish. The mark passed to Valerius — a hot-headed, arrogant young man sitting at the ENAA, recently marked, heir to the Evocation pillar, and nephew of the king rather than son.

This separation between crown and mark produces a social fracture at the very apex of Varkaan’s hierarchy. The king governs without divine sanction. His nephew carries divine sanction without governing. In Vel’Era, the Riftmark and the crown are constitutionally fused — the mark IS the legitimacy. In Varkaan, the mark and the crown exist in the same bloodline but not in the same person, which means the question of who truly leads House Daruun — the uncle who holds the political authority or the nephew who holds the god-chosen power — is a question the family has not publicly resolved and the rest of the aristocracy is watching very carefully.

Valerius at the ENAA does not experience himself as a political complication. He experiences himself as the Riftmarked of Evocation, heir to Thor’s legacy, the most powerful young mage at the academy. His arrogance is not purely personal — it is the arrogance of a young man who carries a mark that his uncle, the king, does not. Whether Valerius understands the political implications of that asymmetry is a question his behaviour at the ENAA suggests he has not seriously considered. The people around him — Morgan Blackwood in the White Bears, Kaelan Heimdall, the faculty — have considered it. That gap between Valerius’s self-understanding and everyone else’s assessment of his position is itself a social fact about what it means to be a Daruun in 1239 A.M.

THE IRON GUILDS AS PARALLEL SOCIETY

The Guilds are not part of Varkaan’s noble hierarchy. They exist alongside it, within it, beneath it, and above it depending on which metric you use. This is the most unusual social arrangement on the continent and the one most resistant to clean description.

The founding oath is to the craft. Not to the king. Not to Druhmir Hold. To the work itself. A Guild master swears at Ironcross — the strategic waypoint and oath-swearing crossroads positioned on the trade route between Druhmir Hold and Halreth — not in the capital, not before the crown. The oath ceremony is held on the road rather than in the throne room because the Guild’s identity is commercial and craft-based rather than administratively tied to the crown. This is not an accident. It is a statement the crown has tried to change and the Guilds have never permitted.

The Guild masters at Druhmir Hold are personally wealthier than most of Varkaan’s noble houses. Their institutional treasury is separate from and parallel to the crown treasury. The crown cannot access it except through negotiation. When the king needs Varkaan-forged equipment for a military campaign — which every Varkaan military operation does — he negotiates procurement terms with the Guilds as a commercial counterparty rather than simply directing production as sovereign. The king of the largest kingdom on Eòs is, in this specific transaction, a customer.

The social consequence is a population within Varkaan that answers to the Guild rather than to the crown. Smiths, miners, smelters, apprentices, the entire vertical supply chain from Ironvein’s etherium extraction through Greyhall’s stormstone operations through the Emberforge Village’s soulsteel production to the master workshops at Druhmir Hold — these people are Guild people. They live in Guild housing, train under Guild masters, swear Guild oaths, and carry Guild certification that has more practical value in their daily lives than any noble title the crown could grant them. A certified Guild smith can walk into any kingdom on Eòs, present his certification, and receive employment at premium rates. A minor Varkaan noble with no Guild connection can walk into Caltheran and receive polite indifference.

The Guild population is not a social class in the traditional sense. It is a parallel society — with its own hierarchy (apprentice, journeyman, master, master of masters), its own values (craft quality above all, the oath above politics), its own wealth distribution (Guild masters at the top, apprentices at the bottom, the vertical structure mirroring the noble hierarchy without sharing any of its personnel), and its own relationship to the crown (cooperative when interests align, friction when they don’t, never subordinate). A blacksmith’s son who masters the forge becomes a Guild master regardless of his father’s house. That mobility is real. It operates entirely within the Guild structure and does not translate to the noble hierarchy. The two systems run in parallel. They intersect at the economic level. They do not merge.

THE HIGHLAND ARISTOCRACY

Below the Daruun crown, Varkaan’s noble houses are stratified along martial lines. This is the most conventionally hierarchical portion of Varkaan’s social structure and the one that most resembles what the other kingdoms have — a ranked system of houses holding authority through bloodline, military prestige, and functional domain.

House Tal’kor is the permanent second. Cavalry legions, master strategists, a centuries-old rivalry with Daruun that the crown has historically managed by keeping both useful and neither supreme. Where Daruun is the hammer, Tal’kor is the manoeuvre. Kaelen Tal’kor at the ENAA is in the White Bears — the house of discipline and command — carrying the weight of a family that has been powerful enough to rival the crown’s own house for generations and has never been permitted to surpass it. The Tal’kor social position is the specific frustration of people who are excellent at what they do and structurally prevented from being the best at it.

House Joric forges the steel. Their power is industrial rather than martial but it is not lesser. Without Joric, House Daruun’s armies carry inferior equipment. The interdependence is understood by both houses. House Joric occupies the social position of the indispensable specialist — not the most prestigious, not the most politically prominent, but the house whose absence would collapse the entire military architecture. Bastion Joric at the ENAA is in the White Bears. An engineer and a pragmatist. The social character of a house that builds the things warriors use and has learned to value the building over the using.

House Holden produces Varkaan’s most ferocious frontline commanders. Fiery temperament. Win at any cost. Diavon Holden at the ENAA leads the Hollow Hearts — a manipulative leader who has turned a house named for potential and mystery into a personal instrument. The Holden social character is what Varkaan’s martial culture looks like at its most extreme — courage valued indistinguishable from recklessness, ferocity valued indistinguishable from cruelty, a house whose reputation is respected and feared in the same breath. That Diavon leads through manipulation rather than the open force his house is known for is either an evolution or a corruption of the Holden tradition. The distinction depends on whether you believe manipulation is a form of warfare or a betrayal of it.

House Sunder builds the keeps. Quarry masters and fortress builders. Practical, hard-working, lacking the glamour of warriors and mages. Their contribution is etched into the rock of the kingdom — every imposing keep, every fortress town, every defensive position that makes Varkaan’s terrain ungovernable by outsiders is Sunder’s work. Petra Sunder at the ENAA is in the Silver Bulls — the house of endurance and quiet strength. A house that builds things and is rarely credited for what those things make possible.

THE DESERT — AL-KHEM AND THE ISKARI

House Al-Khem and the Iskari population occupy the Sol’telarī interior in a relationship that is simultaneously commercial partnership, cultural tension, and historical injustice.

House Al-Khem predates the Varkaan Dominion. The name itself — Al from the Arabic definite article, Khem from the ancient Egyptian for black land — encodes the Iskari heritage that the house carries. Al’Druhm, the city they founded on the upper River Kýe, was a civic centre of settled Iskari life before Varkaan was a political entity. The house built its wealth on the Sol’telarī’s unique ecology — rare flora and fauna isolated by the highland rain shadow, desert pharmaceutical products from potent healing salves to neurotoxins. Zafira Al-Khem at the ENAA is in the Hollow Hearts. An alchemist from a house whose territory overlaps with the ancestral ground of the people the king is trying to exterminate.

The Iskari are not a house. They are a people. Nomadic tribes descended from pre-kingdom settlers who inhabited the Sol’telarī before Varkaan drew a border around them and called the result a kingdom. Their cultural traditions are not Varkaan traditions. Their spiritual practices — Vak’tirī worship, the old faith, Vorrâ and Asthœl invoked in the desert under a sky where the broken moon shines brighter than the whole one — are the most significant Vak’tirī practice on Eòs outside Iskar itself. Kye’vathn is their cultural convergence point — the seasonal gathering where nomadic tribes come together for trade, water access, and the specific social infrastructure that only exists where scattered people periodically assemble.

The Iskari social position in Varkaan is the one the continental overview identified as belonging to the people who don’t count — except in Varkaan the not-counting is active rather than passive. They are not forgotten. They are present, visible, culturally distinct, economically integrated into the desert economy in ways the kingdom uses but does not formally account for, and the subject of an active political debate about whether they should continue to exist.

House Nomad — recently ennobled from one of Varkaan’s most prominent nomadic tribes — is the kingdom’s attempt to bridge the gap between its administrative apparatus and a population that has historically treated administrative apparatuses as weather. Their ennoblement was a political instrument: create an Iskari noble house, give it a title, use it as a translation layer between the crown’s governance and the desert’s reality. Ashur of the Red Sands at the ENAA is in the Silver Bulls. He is looked down upon by the “true” nobility for his tribal customs. He carries a minor title that is about to become either a political asset or a death sentence depending on what the council decides about the purge.

House Nomad’s position is structurally impossible. They are nobility of a kingdom that is debating whether to eradicate the people they come from. If the purge proceeds, House Nomad either participates in the destruction of their own heritage or refuses and loses the title that is their only institutional protection. If the purge fails, House Nomad’s survival depends on a political environment where the fact that the purge was proposed at all has poisoned the relationship between the Iskari population and the kingdom that governs them. There is no version of this where House Nomad emerges intact. The title was designed as a bridge. The purge debate has turned it into a trap.

THE GENERAL POPULATION

Varkaan’s commoner population distributes across the kingdom’s radically different internal geographies and produces correspondingly different social experiences.

The highland population — mining towns around the lake district, fishing communities, the settlements that provision Druhmir Hold and the Guild infrastructure — lives in functional prosperity. The freshwater abundance from the highland lakes, the employment opportunities in the mining and smithing operations, the secondary economies that form around any major industrial infrastructure. These communities are not wealthy. They are employed, housed, and connected to the Guild economy in ways that give them more material security than most commoner populations on the continent. A miner at Ironvein has harder working conditions than a farmer on the Vespur Plains but more institutional structure supporting him than a Naaren agricultural worker south of Keth’var.

The highland frontier communities are harsher. Stonewatch guards the southern highlands. Blackridge Waystation serves as an alpine relay post. Frosthelm Lodge sits in the highwood. These are communities at the edge of habitable terrain, where the Skyplate Highlands’ magic-resistant properties and the climate’s severity produce a population of people who are hard because the ground they live on is hard. Their social character is the one frontier populations develop everywhere — self-reliant, suspicious of distant authority, loyal to the specific people they can see rather than to the institutional abstractions they can’t.

The Sol’telarī interior is the desert’s experience. The Iskari nomadic populations and the settled communities around Al’Druhm and Kye’vathn live in an economy the kingdom participates in without formally managing. House Al-Khem’s pharmaceutical operations provide employment and commercial structure. The Varkaan garrison and tax infrastructure are present but thin. The actual social organisation of the desert runs on Iskari civic culture and House Al-Khem’s institutional memory — both of which predate the kingdom by enough generations that the question of who is actually governing this territory has never been formally answered because formally answering it would require admitting the kingdom’s claim is newer than the population’s.

The border populations — Brakka on the Caltheran border, Ebonreach on the southern border — live with the specific character of communities that exist because of strategic position rather than economic opportunity. They know what they are. They know the peace is a condition. They know the architecture of their cities carries the evidence of previous conflicts in stonework that doesn’t match and foundations that predate current administrations.

THE WARRIOR CULTURE AS SOCIAL FABRIC

What holds this fractured kingdom together is the martial identity. Not as ideology — as daily practice.

Varkaan is a kingdom where the ability to fight is a social qualification in the way that cultural participation is a social qualification in Halreth and arcane expertise is a social qualification in Caltheran. A young man or woman who demonstrates martial competence enters a different social trajectory than one who does not. Not formally — no law declares warriors superior to scholars. But the practical reality is that Varkaan’s culture, from the battle-carved reliefs in Druhmir Hold’s oldest structures to the Iron Guilds’ oath-swearing at Ironcross to the military hierarchy that structures every noble house’s internal organisation, is built around the assumption that the ability to fight is the foundational human virtue and everything else is secondary.

This produces a social environment where House Thorne from Halreth would not be a necessary anomaly but the baseline. Where Clayrr’s Doctrine of the Unmagicked Field isn’t a specialised discipline but common sense. Where the Eastern frontier communities that every other kingdom treats as peripheral are, in Varkaan, the social ideal — hard people on hard ground doing hard work without complaint.

The martial culture is also what makes the Iskari purge politically possible. In a kingdom where the ability to fight is the foundational social virtue, a population that does not fight — that is nomadic, spiritual, culturally distinct, economically marginal — is a population that the martial hierarchy does not recognise as fully participating in what Varkaan is. The Iskari are not hated for what they have done. They are dismissed for what they are not. The purge proposal is not framed as vengeance. It is framed as administrative efficiency — the removal of a population that does not contribute to the thing the kingdom values most. That the Sol’telarī’s ecology depends on Iskari knowledge, that the desert pharmaceutical economy depends on Iskari labour, that the cultural heritage the Iskari maintain is older and arguably more significant than anything Varkaan has produced — none of this registers in a social calculus that weights martial contribution above everything else.

THE PEOPLE WHO DON’T COUNT

In Varkaan, the people who don’t count are the Iskari. Not by default. By active political decision currently being debated in council chambers.

Every other kingdom’s uncounted are invisible — the Ashen Coast in Naaren, the forest-border communities in Vel’Era, the eastern frontier in Halreth. Varkaan’s uncounted are visible, named, culturally distinct, and the subject of a conversation about whether they should be removed from the kingdom’s territory through organised violence. This is not the quiet neglect of a kingdom that forgot its margins. This is a kingdom looking directly at a population and asking whether that population deserves to continue existing within its borders.

Ashur of the Red Sands sits at the ENAA in the Silver Bulls while this conversation happens at home. He is looked down upon by the “true” nobility for his tribal customs. He holds a minor title that was designed to bridge two cultures and is about to become the proof that the bridge was never structurally sound. He is the most personally endangered student at the ENAA and the least likely to be recognised as such, because the danger he faces is not a rival’s political ambition or a trial’s lethal design — it is his own kingdom deciding that people like him are a problem to be solved.

The Iskari women in the Sol’telarī who gather seasonally at Kye’vathn, who maintain the old faith, who tend the desert ecology that House Al-Khem profits from, who have been there longer than the kingdom that governs them — they are the people the purge would reach first. Not the men with weapons. The women with bone charms who believe the dead walk beside the living. The families whose children have grown up in the desert’s vastness under the broken moon’s light, hearing stories in a language the kingdom’s administrative apparatus does not speak. The elders who remember the names of places the Varkaan maps have renamed.

These are the people the council is debating. The council has not met them. The council does not need to meet them to vote. That is the social structure’s darkest fact — the people whose existence is being debated are not present for the debate, and the system that permits their absence from the conversation about their survival is the same system that permits the conversation to happen at all.

HOW THEY LIVE TOGETHER

They don’t live together so much as they live in the same kingdom while occupying different worlds within it.

The highland aristocracy lives in Druhmir Hold and the fortress cities, surrounded by martial culture, supported by Guild infrastructure, governing a military hierarchy that has functioned for centuries. The Guilds live in the industrial infrastructure, answering to the craft, maintaining a parallel economy the crown cannot access without their cooperation. The desert population — Al-Khem, the Iskari, the nomadic communities — lives in the Sol’telarī interior, governed by social structures older than the kingdom, integrated into the economy in ways the economy uses without acknowledging. The frontier populations live at the edges, hard and self-reliant, loyal to what they can see. The border populations live with the knowledge that the peace is temporary and the architecture remembers the last time it ended.

The martial culture is supposed to be the glue. The shared identity of a kingdom that values the ability to fight is supposed to hold these disparate populations in a single national framework. It has held for centuries. But the purge question is the test of whether shared martial identity can survive the discovery that one portion of the population was never included in the identity in the first place. The Iskari do not fight the way Varkaan fights. They do not swear the way the Guilds swear. They do not build the way House Sunder builds. They exist in the kingdom as something the kingdom has never fully incorporated, and the purge proposal is the first time the kingdom has formally asked whether incorporation is the goal or whether elimination is.

Varkaan’s social structure in 1239 A.M. is a kingdom at the moment before the question is answered. The highland aristocracy, the Guilds, the desert population, the frontier — they are all waiting for the council’s decision, and the decision will determine not just the Iskari’s fate but what Varkaan understands itself to be. A kingdom that commits atrocity against its oldest population is a different kingdom than one that does not, and every social relationship within the hierarchy restructures depending on which kingdom it turns out to be.

The Iron Guilds have not declared a position. House Al-Khem has not declared a position. House Nomad cannot declare a position without declaring which part of their identity they are willing to sacrifice. The crown is seeking agreement it does not yet have from a council divided on the most significant moral question any of them will face in their lifetimes.

And at the ENAA, Valerius Daruun leads the Black Dogs with the confidence of a boy who carries Thor’s mark and has never once considered that the kingdom his uncle governs is on the verge of becoming something he might not want to be heir to. Ashur of the Red Sands endures the Silver Bulls’ trials with the quiet resilience of a man whose people are being debated like a line item. Zafira Al-Khem mixes compounds in the Hollow Hearts and does not say what she thinks about what is happening to the desert her family has lived in longer than Varkaan has existed. Bastion Joric forges and watches and calculates the way Guild people calculate — what the materials cost, what the output produces, whether the equation holds.

The equation has held for centuries. The purge question is the variable that changes every number in it.
