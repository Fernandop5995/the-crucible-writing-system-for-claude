Book 1: Riftmarked

Chapter: 1.5 

*Just five more minutes and then wake me.*
Elaine would have performed the most heinous deed to return to the dream she was having.

It started as a hum in the marrow of her left radius—a vibration so deep it felt like her bones were grinding together. Then the sound came and would not leave, no matter how hard she tried to filter it. The stone walls of the keep were *singing* to the riftmarked. The sounds and emotions of the outside world cascaded over her. The wind outside wasn't just moving air; it was a chaotic tapestry of currents, carrying the emotional residue of six hundred soldiers shivering in the chill.
In the middle of it all one stood out. A hatred so potent it made her head throb with a steady heat, a vengeful lust to kill the ones who had wronged her. It was Melody.

Elaine Morrandis gasped, her eyes snapping open.
The isolation ward was dark, save for the faint, soft glow of a single hanging moss-globe near the entrance, hung up by Lune while she slept, no doubt.
She tried to sit up. She couldn't.
Leather straps held her right wrist and ankles to the iron frame of the cot. Her left arm—the source of the screaming in her head—was immobilized, wrapped in layers of heavy linen and bound to her torso with broad strips of cloth.
Panic, cold and sharp, spiked in her chest. She yanked at the restraint on her right wrist. The leather creaked, but held.
"Do not fight the straps," a deep voice said. It was soft yet firm, and utterly unbothered by her distress. "You will only chafe the skin. Behave.”

West sat on a stool in the corner, almost invisible in the gloom. His white robes caught the faint light, making him look like a ghost haunting the bedside. He was reading a thick tome, his finger tracing a line of text.
Elaine let her head fall back against the pillow. Her breathing was ragged, and sweat had already begun forming on her temples. "Untie me, West."
"I don't think I will." He turned a page. The sound was deafening to her heightened senses—like a tree snapping. She flinched.
"I am the scout commander," she grit out, fighting the nausea rolling in her gut. “Untie me now. I won't ask again.”
"In the field, yes, you are a commander," West agreed, not looking up. "In this room, you are a patient in critical neuro-arcane distress. In here, I outrank the King himself."
Elaine squeezed her eyes shut. The colors floating around the room became too much—she could see the flow of heat rising from West’s skin, a faint aura of pale blue that pulsed with his heartbeat. She could see the steady green and red emanating through the stone walls, all of which shimmered faintly purple. It was distracting. It was maddening.
"I'm in pain. The drug wore off," she whispered. 
"Faster than anticipated," the albino man said. He closed the tome and stood to move with that deceptively languid grace of his, crossing the small space to stand over her. "Brunor wanted you out until morning. Your metabolism had other ideas."
At the mention of Brunor, a different kind of ache settled in her chest. She remembered the War Chamber. She remembered the humiliation of shaking against him, of needing his sword to silence the world. She remembered the look in his eyes—not the respect of a peer, but the terrified pity of a guardian watching something break he was responsible for. 
He grounded me, she thought bitterly. He put me in an enclosure.
"My hand," Elaine said. "It feels like it's melting. I can't take it anymore. Cut it off."
"It isn't," West said. He reached out and touched her forehead with the back of his hand. His skin was cool, dry as parchment. "No fever. The heat is psychosomatic. Your nerves are interpreting the magical influx as pain because they don't know how else to interpret it."
He reached for a clay cup on the bedside table. "Drink."
He held it to her lips. She didn't have a choice. She swallowed. The liquid was thick, tasting of bitter root and stale earth. She forced it down when it almost came back up.
"More sedative?" she asked, grimacing.
"Chromatic suppressant," West corrected. "It will dull the pain by lowering the connection between chroma and your body. It won't make you sleep, but it will make the walls stop singing their tunes."

Elaine lay there, waiting. Slowly, the roar in her head began to recede. The wind outside became just wind. The groan of the stones faded into silence. The colors vanished. The throbbing in her left hand didn't stop, but it dulled to a manageable bruise-ache.
She exhaled, her body going limp against the mattress. 
"Better?" West asked.
“Fuck off," Elaine rasped.
"Mhm, so yes.” West sat on the edge of the bed. It was a breach, intimate in that irritating manner that only healers were allowed to be. He looked at her bound arm. "Lune says the mark is hungry. It consumes your soul to feed the connection to the Rift."
"Lune talks too much," Elaine deflected. "Where is she?"
"Training your scout. The Hollow."
Elaine frowned. "He is dangerous. Is placing him under my care really for the best?”
"He is a conduit of something greater, just as you are dear, and Lune is teaching him how not to explode when that power decides to rear its head. But we are not talking about him. We are talking about Elaine Morrandis, Scout Commander, as she reminded me a few minutes ago."
He reached out and began to undo the leather strap on her right wrist.
"I am releasing one arm. I trust you not to be stupid. If you try to unbind the left, I will sedate you again, and this time I will use the heavy stuff from the Iskari teachings. Do we understand each other?"

Elaine rubbed her freed wrist. The circulation returned with a prickle of needles. "I need to see the map, West. The Naaren spies... Lyra Ounvian. I saw her signature."
"You saw tracks."
"I felt her," Elaine corrected. "The Kymancy...Lune said it's about flow. Emotion. Intent. I felt a cold spot in the woods. A void where empathy should be. That's Lyra."
West watched her, his pale eyes lost in thought.
"You are describing high-level sensory magic, Elaine. You are doing it without training, without an incantation, and without a focus." He leaned in. "A mage you are not. Their discipline in handling the elements is lost on you. You are a simple warrior who has been forcibly plugged into a source meant for a god. If you push it? If you try to hunt this Ounvian woman with your mind before your body adapts..."
"I burn out and die pointlessly,” she finished. "I know."
"You don't just burn out," West said. His voice dropped, losing its professional detachment. "You hollow out. Incorrectly handling your magic eats the person until only the body remains, and the soul has left for another existence. I have seen it happen. I will not watch it happen to you."
Elaine looked away, staring at the dark curtain. "I am the Scout Commander. If I can't scout... if I can't see the enemy..."
"Then you lead," West said firmly. "From a chair. From a bed. You use that sharp, stubborn sense of yours and you let others be your hands."
He stood up, smoothing his white robes. The sun from the high window illuminated him when he did. "I have rounds. The garrison has an outbreak of the Gulv because soldiers are unable to keep their cocks inside their trousers. Try to rest. If the noise comes back, tap the glass on the table. It resonates."
He moved to the curtain.
"West," she called out.
He paused.
"Does he know?" she asked quietly. "Brunor. Does he know how bad it is?"
West didn't turn. "He stood over your bed after you fell asleep. For hours, he watched your chest rise and fall as if he were guarding the gates."
West pulled the curtain aside.
"He knows you are in pain, Elaine. And he is unable to help. Nobody can.”
The curtain fell back into place, leaving Elaine alone in the dim light.
She brought her free hand up to her face, staring at her palm. It was steady now. The suppressant was working. But beneath the silence, beneath the bandages on her left arm, she could still feel it.
That accursed pulse that she wanted to rip out. A heartbeat that wasn't hers.
Thump-thump. Thump-thump. Thump-thump.
It was coming from the East. From the Shadowfen.
I see you, the pulse almost seemed to say. I see you, little vessel.
Elaine Morrandis closed her hand into a fist. She could do nothing but endure as she had most of her life. She closed her eyes and let the song fade into oblivion.

The night had deepened, turning the thin slits of the high windows into obsidian eyes. The infirmary had settled into a hush save for the rustle of a sleeper in the chamber over, the clink of glass from West’s workbench somewhere down the corridor, and the low draft whistling through the stone.
The silence felt weak, like a lie being unveiled. The suppressant was fading.
The thrum in her arm was back, a syncopated beat that gnawed at her wrist. It was like having a second heart implanted in her carpal bones, one that beat too fast and too hard.
She heard them before she saw them.
Footsteps. Two sets. One was light, sharp, striking the stone with the cadence of a fencer—heel-toe, rapid, eager. The other was heavy, deliberate, a rolling gait that absorbed the ground rather than striking it. Phoebe. Brunor. She could feel them, their thoughts and emotions, before she registered their footsteps. 
The curtain swept back.

"They tied you up…” Phoebe spat immediately.

Phoebe Morrandis stood in the opening, still wearing her scout leathers, though she had shed her pauldrons. Her black hair was pulled back in a severe, messy tail, stray strands framing a face that was flushed with cold air and indignation. She looked vibrant, alive, and terrifyingly young.
"It’s for my own good, Bee," Elaine let out weakly, her voice scratching against the dryness of her throat. She tried to smile, but it felt false.
"It looks like you're a prisoner," Phoebe snapped. She marched to the bedside, her hand resting on the pommel of her rapier—a nervous tic she had developed since the promotion. She looked at the heavy linen strapping Elaine’s left arm to her body. "Did West do this? I’ll cut it. Watch your fingers.” 
The captain unsheathed her blade and held it over the bedside.
"You will not," a deep voice rumbled.
Brunor stepped through the curtain.
As he entered the small, enclosed space, the air pressure changed. The high-pitched whine that had been building in Elaine’s ears—the sound of the castle’s wards, the ambient magic of the earth—suddenly cut out. It was as if someone had snuffed a candle in a bright room.
Elaine exhaled, her head falling back against the pillow. The relief was so absolute it bordered on pleasure.
Brunor stopped just inside the threshold, filling the space. He wasn't wearing his cloak anymore, just the dark tunic that strained across his shoulders. The Black Blade hung at his hip, dull and unreflective in the gloom.
He looked at Phoebe, who was still holding the blade out. "West gave strict orders. No agitation. No cutting."
Phoebe turned her head, her eyes flashing steel-blue. "She’s not a mad dog, Brunor. Look at her. She’s... she’s barely there. I won't allow this.”
"I am right here," Elaine said, forcing strength into her tone. "And I can hear you both."
Phoebe turned back, her expression softening into something raw and scared. She sheathed her blade and then sat on the edge of the mattress, careful not to jostle the frame. "They wouldn't let me in earlier. West said you were 'sleeping.' You were shaking, El, in the War Room. You were terrified about something. What was it?”
Elaine looked at her beautiful sister, the one thing on this planet she would give up everything for without a thought beforehand. She wanted to lie. She wanted to say it was just exhaustion, or a fever, or a curse that would pass. But Phoebe was a Captain now. She had led men into the mud. She knew what lies sounded like.
"It’s the mark," Elaine said as if something was broken with her now and couldn't be fixed.
Phoebe’s gaze dropped to the bandaged left arm. She reached out, hovering her hand over the bandages. "Does it hurt?"
"It’s agony," Elaine said. "It feels like I'm trying to hold back floodwater with a paper dam."
"I can take the patrol," Phoebe said suddenly. That ambition, Elaine never got used to seeing in her eyes again, warring with her worry. "The Shadowfen. You were going to lead the reconnaissance next week. I can do it. My squad is ready. My men are getting better, and Ryn said my navigation is perfect."
Elaine felt a spike of cold fear. "No. You will remain here.”
"I am a Captain! Do not keep saving me from what I am destined to do! I have proven myself ten times over, more than most here!”
"You are eighteen," Elaine countered, struggling to sit up against the pillows. Her restrained arm pressed against the binding. "And the Fen is not just trees and swamp right now, Captain. It’s terrible. The land itself is sick. I felt it. If you go out there..."
"If I go out there, I’ll do my job as my title demands," Phoebe insisted. She looked to Brunor for support, who was watching the men below from the window. "Tell her. Tell her I’m ready."
Brunor crossed his arms and turned; worry was evident in his eyes, which was no small thing. “You have the skill, Phoebe. But the Commander is right. The Fen is off limits until we understand what Ounvian is doing. Listen to your commander.”
Phoebe huffed, crossing her arms to mirror him. "So we sit? We wait? I'm not like you. I don't twiddle my thumbs while the world devolves into shit.”
“Phoebe. Careful,” Elaine said.
“Planning is what we do," Brunor continued. "And we let the Commander heal. If you don't like this, then you can hand over your pin right now and go alone.”
*Alone? I would never let her go. This rift is my fate to reckon with.*

Phoebe looked between them in the silence. She was young, but she wasn't blind. 
"Fine," Phoebe said, standing up. She smoothed her tunic. "But I’m not sitting idle. I’m running night drills with the recruits. Someone has to make sure they don't stab themselves."
She leaned down and kissed Elaine’s sweaty forehead. Her lips were cold. "Get better, El. The keep is quiet without you. It’s unnerving. I love you.”
She turned to Brunor, poking him in the chest with a stiff finger. "Watch her. If West tries to feed her that gruel again, stab him."
"Duly noted,” Brunor said.
Phoebe swept out of the curtain, her energy dragging a wake of motion behind her.
Then there was silence.
Real silence. The kind that only existed within ten feet of Brunor Halvyr. And that peace. The one that came when you were near the only one who could illuminate the truths of your hidden world, and everything made sense in the confusion.

"She’s terrified," Brunor said quietly. "She thinks she’s losing you."
"She might be," Elaine whispered.
Brunor stepped forward. He pulled the stool West had used closer to the bed and sat down. The movement brought the hilt of the Black Blade level with her shoulder. The null intensified. It felt like a cool hand pressing against her fevered skin.
"West says the blade helps," he said.
"It does. Greatly.”
"Good." he said unbuckling the sheath and laying it onto the mattress near her arm.

Brunor rested his elbows on his knees and looked at his own fingers, rough and scarred, refusing to meet her eyes.
"I hated it," he said. His voice was a low rumble, intimate in the small space. "Ordering you to stay, seeing you shake, seeing you terrified. I am used to you being the strong one, El. You’re the dancer. You move around the hit. I’m the one who takes it on the shield." He looked up then, and the raw vulnerability in his face broke her heart. "I cannot block this for you. I cannot parry a rune."
Elaine shifted her free hand, reaching out. She touched the back of his hand. His skin was warm and rough. 
"You are blocking it," she said. "Right now. Just by being here."
He turned his hand over, catching her fingers. He didn't squeeze—he was always so careful with her, as if she were made of glass—but his grip was exactly what she needed. What she had begged the gods for all day, and with their mercy, they had finally answered.
"Then I will stay," he said. "I will sit in this chair until you get sick of me."
"You have a garrison to command. You have sleep to catch."
"Reyka has the garrison. And I don't sleep well anyway."
Elaine looked at their joined hands. There were so many things she wanted to say. I love you. Don't look at me differently. I am scared. I think this mark is going to eat me alive and I don't want you to watch it happen. 
But they were mercenaries, commanders, and the world was ending by degrees.
"Tell me about Tellen," she forced out instead. "Phoebe says he has potential. Melody says he’s a liability. Merea thinks he is just some dumb kid with luck.”
Brunor’s thumb brushed the back of her hand. "He fell seven times today. Face first into the mud."
"And?"
"And he got up eight times," Brunor said. A ghost of a smile touched his lips. "He has no form. No balance. But he has something. He hit me."
Elaine raised an eyebrow. "He hit you?"
"A tap. But Tellen found the opening himself and exploited it." Brunor looked at her, his eyes soft. "He fights like someone who has everything to lose."
The silence of the Black Blade wrapped around them, a sanctuary carved out of the chaos. For a moment, the Rift didn't exist. The keep didn't exist. Nothing did. There was only the stone room, the dim light, and the man who kept the noise away.
"Stay," she whispered. “Please.”
"I'm not going anywhere," Brunor promised.
He didn't let go of her hand. And as the night wore on, for the first time since the Mark had flared, Elaine slept without dreaming of the fire.
